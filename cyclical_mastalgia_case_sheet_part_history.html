CYCLICAL BREAST PAIN (MASTALGIA) – HISTORY

Presenting Complaint: Bilateral cyclical breast pain & lumpiness

  1. S – Site “Which part of your breasts feels painful or lumpy? Upper‑outer, inner, behind nipple?”
  2. O – Onset “When did you first notice the discomfort?”
  3. D – Duration/Pattern “Does the pain last the entire cycle or only the week before periods?”
  4. A/R – Aggravating/Relieving “Does caffeine, exercise, bra support, or stress make it worse or better?”
  5. C – Character “Is it a heaviness, stabbing or burning sensation?”
  6. R – Radiation “Does the discomfort spread to the armpit or arm?”
  7. A – Associated symptoms
    • Discrete persistent lump? NICE red‑flag
    • Nipple discharge (bloody / clear)?
    • Skin or nipple changes (dimpling, eczema)?
    • Systemic: weight loss, fevers, night sweats
  8. P – Progression “Has the severity changed over the months?”
  9. P – Pattern “Continuous vs intermittent? Relation to cycle recorded on calendar?”
  10. E – Effect on life “How is pain affecting sleep, work, intimacy?”
  11. R – Red flags “Any new lump persisting beyond period, unilateral bloody discharge?”
  12. S – Severity (scale) “On a scale of 0–10 how bad is the pain at its worst?”

MANAGEMENT PLAN

Urgency & Referral

  • No immediate hospital admission needed (benign cyclical mastalgia – normal obs, no red‑flags on exam).
  • Offer same‑day breast clinic referral only if red‑flag features arise (persistent focal lump >1 cycle, peau‑d’orange skin, nipple retraction, unilateral or blood‑stained discharge). NICE NG12

Investigations (if symptoms persist >3 cycles or atypical)

  • Bilateral mammogram ± targeted ultrasound (≥40 y or focal area) NICE CKS Breast Pain
  • Consider serum prolactin & thyroid profile if galactorrhoea or systemic features.

Explanation & Education

  • Explain benign nature of cyclical mastalgia linked to hormonal fluctuation.
  • Validate anxiety and correct cancer misconceptions.
  • Provide Breast Cancer Care leaflet: “Breast pain (mastalgia)” (QR code / link).

Lifestyle & Non‑pharmacological Measures

  • Diary: record pain severity vs cycle for 2 – 3 months (helps track improvement).
  • Well‑fitting, supportive bra day & night during luteal phase (speaker @ 1:35).
  • Reduce caffeine (<200 mg/day) and saturated fat intake; balanced diet & exercise.
  • Smoking cessation & optimise BMI if overweight.

Pharmacological Options

  1. First‑line: Simple analgesia – paracetamol or NSAID (topical diclofenac gel or oral ibuprofen) 3–5 days pre‑menses.
  2. Adjunct: Topical 5–10% diclofenac gel applied twice daily to tender area.
  3. Evening primrose oil (gamma‑linolenic acid 240 mg TDS) for 3 months – variable evidence but minimal harm. NICE CKS
  4. If persistent & severe after 6 months → consider specialist prescribing of: danazol 200 mg daily OR tamoxifen 10 mg daily (3–6 months) – counsel on adverse effects; consultant initiation.

Follow‑Up & Safety Netting

  • Review in 6 – 8 weeks (one complete cycle) to assess response & reassess breasts.
  • Advise to return sooner if: new discrete lump, lump persists post‑period, nipple discharge, skin/nipple change, systemic symptoms.
  • Provide OOH contact (NHS 111) and clinic number.

Documentation

  • Record chaperone offered & present.
  • Record breast exam findings, patient understanding, leaflet given, analgesia advised, safety‑net advice.

11‑Point Qualitative Feedback (Communication & Consultation Skills)

  1. Simplify jargon: replace phrases like “cyclical mastalgia” without explanation with “hormone‑related breast pain (called cyclical mastalgia)”. GMC GMP §31
  2. Use reflective listening: when Jenny said paracetamol “didn’t do much,” ask dose/frequency and acknowledge frustration.
  3. Avoid colloquialisms (“Kids, you must be having a lot on your plate!!! huh”); maintain professional empathy, e.g., “It sounds exhausting balancing family and this worry—how are you coping?”
  4. Signpost agenda: outline structure at start (“We’ll talk about your symptoms, examine you, then discuss options”). NICE Shared decision‑making NG205
  5. Delay definitive reassurance until assessment complete; premature “it’s not dangerous” risks missing red‑flags.
  6. Check patient preference & cultural beliefs before recommending evening‑primrose oil or supplements.
  7. Address cancer fear using SPIKES: explore perception, invite questions, give knowledge gradually.
  8. Clarify analgesia plan: give exact NSAID dose/duration and warn about GI side‑effects.
  9. Offer choice of immediate imaging vs watchful waiting, documenting Jenny’s preference.
  10. Summarise and verify understanding more than once; invite feedback (“Is there anything I could explain better?”).
  11. Validate emotions: phrases like “I can see this has been worrying you for weeks” before giving advice.

Key Time‑Saving Tweaks for Next Attempt

  1. ☑️ Anchor each section with signposts. “First, I’d like to ask some quick questions about the pain… then examine you… then discuss options.” Keeps track & discourages detours.
  2. ☑️ Prioritise red‑flags early (discharge, persistent lump, skin change). Lifestyle & family questions can follow if time allows.
  3. ☑️ Use single concise summary after history, not two; then transition straight to exam.
  4. ☑️ Batch ICE – Ask ideas/concerns/expectations together (“What’s worrying you most? … What were you hoping today?”) instead of scattered probes.
  5. ☑️ Reassure once. Avoid pre‑exam reassurance; wait until after findings to prevent duplication.
  6. ☑️ Close with a micro‑teach. 60‑second explanation (name, benign, management steps, 2WW triggers) then confirm understanding → finish by 7 : 30.
  7. ☑️ Keep responses ≤15 s each. Longest doctor utterance was 57 s; aim for <20 s to maintain pace.
