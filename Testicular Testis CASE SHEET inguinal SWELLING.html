    DIAGNOSIS

    1. ANATOMICAL: e.g., Right Indirect/Direct Inguinal Hernia; Left Femoral Hernia; Right Inguinoscrotal Hernia.
    2. ETIOLOGICAL: e.g.,
      1. Congenital (Patent Processus Vaginalis for indirect);
      2. Acquired (due to weakness of posterior inguinal wall/Hesselbach's triangle, increased intra-abdominal pressure).
    3. PATHOLOGICAL: e.g., Containing omentum/small bowel loops.
    4. CLINICAL (Status): e.g., Reducible, Irreducible (incarcerated), Obstructed, Strangulated.

    Definition: Hernia is defined as a protrusion of whole or a part of viscus through the wall that contains it. (Examples: Muscle through fascial covering, Brain through fracture of skull, Brain through foramen magnum into spinal canal, abdominal hernias).

    Common Abdominal Hernias: Inguinal (Direct/Indirect/Oblique), Femoral, Umbilical, Incisional, Epigastric, Obturator, Lumbar, Gluteal, Spigelian.

    Complete Inguinal Hernia: Sac contents reach bottom of scrotum.

    • Routine Pre-operative Investigations (if surgery planned): FBC, U&Es, Coagulation, ECG, CXR as per patient profile and local guidelines.

    Patient-Specific Plan for Inguinoscrotal Swelling

  • Initial Investigations
    • Clinical Assessment: Detailed history and physical examination including transillumination (Video 4:10) and assessment of reducibility, cough impulse, and ability to get above the swelling.
    • Blood work (If infection, inflammation, or malignancy suspected, or for pre-operative assessment):
      • Full Blood Count (FBC) with differential (for infection/inflammation).
      • C-Reactive Protein (CRP) / Erythrocyte Sedimentation Rate (ESR) (for infection/inflammation).
      • Urea & Electrolytes (U&Es), Liver Function Tests (LFTs) (if systemic illness or pre-op).
      • Tumour Markers (if testicular mass suspected): Alpha-fetoprotein (AFP), Beta-human Chorionic Gonadotropin (β-hCG), Lactate Dehydrogenase (LDH).
      • .
      • HIV ab
      • HIV ag
      • CD4 count
    • Imaging:
      • Ultrasound (Scrotal/Doppler Ultrasound): Primary imaging modality to assess nature of swelling (cystic vs. solid), contents (bowel, omentum, fluid), blood flow (especially if torsion is suspected), and differentiate between hernia, hydrocele, varicocele, epididymal cyst, tumour.
      • Urine dipstick / Mid-Stream Urine (MSU) for microscopy, culture & sensitivity (if infection like epididymo-orchitis is suspected).
      • STI screen (if epididymo-orchitis suspected in sexually active individuals).
      • CT Scan (Chest/Abdomen/Pelvis): For staging if testicular cancer is confirmed or highly suspected.
    • Other:
      • Semen analysis (if varicocele is present and fertility is a concern).
  • Patient Communication
    • ICE (Ideas, Concerns, Expectations): Elicit patient's understanding, fears (e.g., cancer, infertility, surgery), and what they hope to achieve.
    • Diagnosis disclosure: Clearly explain the most likely diagnosis (e.g., inguinal hernia, hydrocele) and differentials.
    • Explanation of findings: Discuss results of examination and initial investigations in simple terms.
  • Further Investigations (Based on initial findings)
    • Specialized tests:
      • Exploratory surgery (may be diagnostic and therapeutic, e.g., for suspected testicular torsion or strangulated hernia).
      • Fine Needle Aspiration (FNA) or Biopsy: Generally AVOIDED for primary testicular masses due to risk of tumour seeding. Diagnosis is usually made via orchidectomy. FNA might be considered for epididymal lesions if diagnosis is unclear post-ultrasound.
    • Referrals:
      • Urgent Surgical/Urology Referral (same day/2 Week Wait - 2WW pathway if cancer suspected): For suspected testicular torsion (emergency), strangulated/obstructed hernia (emergency), testicular tumour (urgent - 2WW).
      • Routine Surgical/Urology Referral: For uncomplicated hernias, hydroceles, varicoceles, epididymal cysts requiring intervention.
  • Treatment Plan
    • Symptomatic treatment:
      • Analgesia (e.g., Paracetamol, NSAIDs) for pain/discomfort.
      • Scrotal support for comfort (e.g., in epididymo-orchitis, large varicocele, post-op).
      • Rest and avoidance of strenuous activity (especially for acute conditions or post-op).
    • Specific treatment (depends on the cause):
      • Inguinal Hernia (Inguinoscrotal):
        • Non-Medical: Watchful waiting (for minimally symptomatic, reducible hernias in men, with clear red flag advice). Lifestyle modification (weight loss, manage constipation/cough). Truss (limited use, specific patients).
        • Surgical: Herniorrhaphy or Hernioplasty (open or laparoscopic).
      • Hydrocele:
        • Non-Medical: Reassurance (common in newborns, often resolves spontaneously). Scrotal support for comfort.
        • Surgical (if persistent, large, symptomatic, or causing cosmetic concern): Hydrocelectomy (e.g., Jaboulay's procedure, Lord's plication). Aspiration (temporary, high recurrence).
      • Varicocele:
        • Non-Medical: Reassurance if asymptomatic. Scrotal support.
        • Medical: Analgesia if painful.
        • Surgical/Interventional Radiology (if symptomatic, testicular atrophy, or infertility): Varicocelectomy (open or laparoscopic ligation) or percutaneous embolization.
      • Epididymal Cyst / Spermatocele:
        • Non-Medical: Reassurance (usually benign).
        • Surgical (if large, painful, or bothersome): Excision of epididymal cyst.
      • Testicular Torsion (SURGICAL EMERGENCY):
        • Surgical: Immediate surgical exploration, detorsion, and bilateral orchiopexy. If testis is non-viable, orchidectomy.
      • Epididymo-orchitis:
        • Medical: Antibiotics (e.g., Ofloxacin + Doxycycline for <35 yrs; Ciprofloxacin or Co-amoxiclav for >35 yrs, tailored to sensitivities). Analgesia. Scrotal support, rest, hydration. Partner notification and treatment if STI-related.
      • Testicular Tumour:
        • Surgical: Radical inguinal orchidectomy (primary treatment).
        • Medical: Chemotherapy (depending on histology and stage).
        • Other: Radiotherapy (for seminomas), Retroperitoneal lymph node dissection (RPLND) in some cases. Sperm banking prior to treatment.

    • Frequency: Depends on diagnosis and treatment. Post-operatively as per surgical protocol. If watchful waiting for hernia, e.g., annually or if symptoms change.
    • Specialist referrals: Urology, General Surgery, Oncology as appropriate.
    • Allied health:

      • Fertility specialist (if varicocele and infertility, or post-orchidectomy/chemo).
      • Psychological support/Counselling (especially for cancer diagnosis, body image concerns, infertility).
  • Patient Education
    • Warning Signs/Red Flags:
      • For Hernia: Sudden severe pain, swelling becomes hard/tender and irreducible, vomiting, inability to pass stool/wind (signs of strangulation/obstruction).
      • For Testicular Torsion: Sudden onset of severe testicular pain, often with nausea/vomiting (seek immediate medical attention).
      • For Testicular Cancer: Any new lump, change in size/shape/consistency of testis, dull ache, heaviness (self-examination awareness).
      • Post-operative: Signs of wound infection (redness, pus, increasing pain, fever), excessive swelling, bleeding.
    • Educational materials provided: Leaflets/pamphlets specific to their condition (e.g., hernia repair, hydrocele, testicular self-examination).
