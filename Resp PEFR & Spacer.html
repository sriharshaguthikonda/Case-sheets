D. INHALERS (Video 5:20)

  • Types:
    • Reliever (Blue): Salbutamol. (Video 5:29-5:39)
      • Relieves symptoms (chest tightness, wheezing, cough). (Video 5:42-5:49)
      • Dilates airways. (Video 5:51-5:54)

      • Side effects: Tremor, muscle cramps, headache (usually minor and transient). (Video 6:02-6:12)
      • Not used regularly, only as needed. (Video 6:24-6:29)
    • Preventer (Brown): Steroid (e.g., Beclomethasone). (Video 6:32-6:37)

      • Used regularly according to prescribed dose. (Video 6:42-6:57)

      • Action: Decreases airway swelling/inflammation and secretions. (Video 7:19-7:28)
      • Side effects: Oral thrush (fungal infection). (Video 7:34-7:40)
      • Prevention of side effects: Rinse mouth or brush teeth after every use. (Video 7:43-7:48) Advisable to keep in washroom as a reminder. (Video 7:51-7:57)
  • Inhaler Technique: (Video 8:02)
    1. Check expiry date. (Video 8:08-8:12)
    2. Shake well. (Video 8:06-8:08)
    3. If not used for a while (especially reliever), test puff into the air. (Video 8:15-8:29)
    4. Hold upright. (Video 8:34-8:36)
    5. Breathe out fully. (Video 8:39-8:43)
    6. Place mouthpiece in mouth and form a tight seal. (Video 8:46-9:00)

    8. Hold breath for 10 seconds, or as long as comfortable. (Video 9:15-9:20)

    10. Do not take two puffs together. (Video 9:32-9:34)

C. ASTHMA DIARY / PERSONALIZED ASTHMA ACTION PLAN (PAAP)

(Note: All asthmatics should have a PAAP detailing how to monitor asthma, recognize worsening control, and seek emergency help. Monitoring can be symptom-based, peak flow-based, or both, with symptom-based plans preferred for children under 12.)

  • Used for a set period (e.g., two weeks for diagnosis or as advised in the PAAP) to assess control, monitor medication changes, or confirm diagnosis if spirometry is delayed. (Video 3:18)
  • Layout and Information to Record:
    • Days of the week (Monday–Sunday) for one or two weeks, as per PAAP. (Video 3:21-3:27)
    • Peak Expiratory Flow (PEF) readings, if included in PAAP: Morning and evening (best of three blows). For diagnosis, record over 2 weeks to assess variability (≥20% indicates asthma). Not routine for all patients. (Video 3:27-3:37)
    • Reliever inhaler use:
      • For patients on MART (ICS/formoterol, e.g., Symbicort, Fostair): Record extra doses taken for symptom relief beyond maintenance doses, as per PAAP. (Video 3:46-4:01)
      • For patients on separate ICS + SABA (e.g., children 5–11 or specific cases): Record each SABA use (e.g., blue salbutamol inhaler). (Video 3:46-4:01)
      • (NICE Note: Increased reliever use (e.g., >3 times/week for SABA or frequent extra MART doses) indicates worsening asthma control, requiring review.)
    • Asthma symptoms: Record presence of symptoms like shortness of breath, chest tightness, coughing, or wheezing. For children under 5, symptom-based monitoring is preferred over PEF. (Video 4:07-4:18)
    • Night-time waking due to asthma symptoms. (Video 4:20-4:24)
    • Impact on day-to-day activities (e.g., difficulty with normal activities). (Video 4:24-4:29)
  • Weekly Comment Box / Notes on Triggers: (Video 4:34)
    • Record potential triggers for symptom flare-ups (e.g., stress, illness, allergens, exercise) to help identify patterns. (Video 4:40-4:53)
    • Assists healthcare professionals in assessing control and adjusting the PAAP, e.g., no medication change if triggers explain symptoms. (Video 4:53-5:08)

D. INHALERS

(NICE Guideline Note: Inhaler choice is based on a stepwise approach, age, patient preference, and ability to use the device correctly. For adults and adolescents (12+), low-dose ICS/formoterol as-needed (AIR) or Maintenance and Reliever Therapy (MART) is preferred for new diagnoses. For children 5–11, start with ICS + SABA; consider MART if uncontrolled. For under 5, use ICS + SABA via spacer. Spacers are recommended with ICS-containing inhalers to improve delivery and reduce side effects.)

  • Types of Inhaler Regimens:
    • 1. Maintenance and Reliever Therapy (MART) – Adults and Adolescents (12+):
      • Uses a single combination inhaler with Inhaled Corticosteroid (ICS) and Formoterol (e.g., Symbicort, Fostair; colors vary, e.g., red/white, pink/white).
      • Taken daily for maintenance (prevention) and as needed for symptom relief, per PAAP.
      • Reduces reliance on separate SABA inhalers, ensuring ICS with every reliever dose.
      • Prescribed maintenance dose plus maximum extra reliever puffs per day (e.g., 12 puffs total/day for Symbicort 200/6).
      • (NICE Note: Preferred for new diagnoses in adults/adolescents due to reduced severe asthma attacks compared to ICS + SABA.)
    • 2. Anti-Inflammatory Reliever (AIR) Therapy – Adults and Adolescents (12+):
      • Low-dose ICS/formoterol (e.g., Symbicort 100/6) used as needed for symptoms, without daily maintenance, for mild asthma.
      • Ensures anti-inflammatory treatment with every dose, reducing exacerbation risk.
    • 3. Separate Preventer (ICS) and Reliever (SABA) – Children (5–11) or Specific Cases:
      • Preventer Inhaler (ICS):
        • Examples: Beclomethasone, Fluticasone, Budesonide (often brown, orange, or purple). (Video 6:32-6:37)
        • Action: Reduces airway inflammation and swelling, preventing symptoms. (Video 7:19-7:28)
        • Usage: Taken daily as prescribed (e.g., two puffs morning and evening for 400mcg BD if 200mcg/puff). (Video 6:57-7:17)
        • Side effects: Oral thrush, hoarse voice. (Video 7:34-7:40)
        • Prevention: Rinse mouth or brush teeth after use; use a spacer to reduce side effects. (Video 7:43-7:48)
      • Reliever Inhaler (SABA):
        • Example: Salbutamol (typically blue). (Video 5:29-5:39)
        • Action: Quickly opens airways to relieve symptoms (wheezing, coughing, chest tightness). (Video 5:42-5:54)
        • Usage: As needed for symptoms, typically 1–2 puffs. (Video 5:54-5:59)
        • Side effects: Tremor, palpitations, headache (usually minor). (Video 6:02-6:12)
        • (NICE Note: For ages 12+, avoid SABA without ICS. For 5–11, use with ICS; frequent use (>3 times/week) indicates poor control, requiring review.)
    • 4. Children Under 5:
      • Use low-dose ICS (e.g., Beclomethasone) via spacer as preventer, with SABA (e.g., salbutamol) via spacer for symptom relief.
      • MART not typically used due to limited evidence.
      • Monitor symptoms (not PEF) due to difficulty obtaining reliable peak flow readings.
  • General Metered-Dose Inhaler (MDI) Technique: (Video 8:02)
    1. Remove cap.
    2. Check expiry date and dose counter (if available). (Video 8:08-8:12)
    3. Shake the inhaler well. (Video 8:06-8:08)
    4. If new or unused for days, prime by test-spraying 1–2 puffs into the air (away from face). (Video 8:15-8:29)
    5. Hold upright. (Video 8:34-8:36)
    6. Breathe out gently and fully, away from the inhaler. (Video 8:39-8:43)
    7. Place mouthpiece in mouth and form a tight seal with lips (or use spacer for ICS or children). (Video 8:46-9:00)
    8. Start to breathe in slowly and steadily, press canister ONCE, continue inhaling deeply. (Video 9:00-9:15)
    9. Remove inhaler, hold breath for up to 10 seconds or as comfortable. (Video 9:15-9:20)
    10. Breathe out gently.
    11. If a second puff is needed, wait 30–60 seconds, shake again, repeat steps 5–10. (Video 9:25-9:32)
    12. Replace cap.
    13. (NICE Note: Check technique regularly with a healthcare professional. Use spacers with ICS-containing MDIs to improve delivery and reduce side effects, especially for children or those with coordination difficulties.) (Video 9:43-10:00)

E. SPACERS (AEROCHAMBERS) (Video 10:18)

  • Indications for Use:
    • Children. (Video 10:21-10:23)
    • People with acute asthma exacerbations. (Video 10:26-10:30)
    • Mentally challenged individuals who may struggle with inhaler coordination. (Video 10:34-10:38)
  • Sizes (Example colors/ages given):
    • Smallest (Orange color in video): 0-18 months. (Video 10:43-10:55)
    • Medium (Yellow color in video): 1-5 years. (Video 11:00-11:02) (Overlap in age allows choice based on child's face size Video 11:05-11:14)
    • Large (Blue color in video): >5 years (including adults). (Video 11:16-11:21)
  • Spacer Parts: (Video 11:32-11:44)
    •  Mouthpiece,
    • valve,
    • aerochamber,
    • inhaler port.
  • Spacer Technique:
    1. Ensure spacer valve and pointer (if present) are aligned for inhaler attachment. (Video 11:48-11:55)
    2. Shake inhaler well, check expiry. Attach to spacer port. (Video 12:01-12:10)
    3. Ensure inhaler and spacer valve are in the same plane. (Video 12:12-12:14)
    4. For a child, ensure they are not crying; make them comfortable (e.g., cuddle in lap). (Video 12:17-12:32)
    5. Place mouthpiece over child's mouth and nose, ensuring a tight seal. (Video 12:36-12:44)
    6. Confirm tight seal by observing valve movement with breathing. (Video 12:47-12:52)
    7. Once seal is confirmed, press the inhaler canister once. (Video 12:55-13:05)
    8. Allow the child to take 5-6 breaths (count valve movements) without removing the spacer. (Video 13:08-13:26)
    9. Remove spacer. If another puff is needed, wait 30 seconds and repeat. (Video 13:32-13:43)
  • Spacer Care:
    • Before use, check for damage or foreign bodies. (Video 13:49-14:05)
    • Washing: (Video 14:07)
      • Remove inhaler port. Rinse in lukewarm soapy water for 10-15 minutes. (Video 14:13-14:19)
      • Rinse under clean water. (Video 14:22-14:36)
      • Air dry (drip dry). Do not boil, scrub, or use a towel. (Video 14:36-14:41)
      • Wash 1-2 times per week, depending on usage. (Video 14:45-14:50)
    • Replace every 6 months to 1 year, or sooner if broken. (Video 14:53-15:03)
  • School/Caregivers: Inform school nurses/teachers if a child uses a spacer and leave one at school if necessary. (Video 15:08-15:19)
