1ᵗʰ COMPLAINT: TIREDNESS / FATIGUE

  • S: Site / nature "Is the tired feeling generalised or do specific muscles feel weak?"
  • O: Onset "When did you first start feeling unusually tired? Was it sudden or gradual?"
  • D: Duration & Progression "Has it been constant or getting worse over the weeks?"
  • A: Aggravating factors "Does anything in particular make the tiredness worse – such as exertion, missing meals, heat?"
  • R: Relieving factors "Does rest, sleep or fluids help at all?"
  • C: Character "Is it more like physical heaviness, mental fogginess, or both?"
  • R: Radiation "Any weakness spreading to arms / legs or cramps anywhere specific?"
  • A: Associated symptoms "Headache, nausea, vomiting, confusion, muscle cramps, seizures, mood changes?"
  • P: Pattern / Progression "Better in mornings? Worse as the day goes on? Any recent worsening?"
  • E: Effect on life "How is this affecting your work, hobbies and family life?"
  • R: Red‑flags "Any episodes of collapse, seizures, severe confusion, or vomiting blood?"
  • S: Severity (0‑10) "On a scale of 0–10, how bad is the fatigue at its worst?"

    • 2ⁿᵈ COMPLAINT: DIZZINESS / POSTURAL LIGHT‑HEADEDNESS

  • S: Situation "Does it happen when you stand up quickly or even at rest?"
  • O: Onset "How long have the dizzy spells been occurring?"
  • D: Duration "How long does each spell last? Seconds, minutes?"
  • A/R: Aggravating / Relieving "Does sitting or lying help? Any relation to meals or dehydration?"
  • C: Character "Spinning sensation or just light‑headedness? Any visual black‑outs?"
  • A: Associated "Palpitations, chest pain, shortness of breath?"
  • E: Effect "Have you fallen or injured yourself because of it?"

    • SYSTEMATIC ENQUIRY (targeted at potential causes)

    • Thyroid: heat / cold intolerance, weight change, bowel habit?
    • Adrenal insufficiency: skin pigmentation, salt craving, postural dizziness?
    • Heart / kidney / liver failure: ankle swelling, breathlessness, abdominal distension?
    • GI losses: recent vomiting, diarrhoea, excessive sweating?
    • Malignancy / SIADH risk: chronic cough, night sweats, weight loss, change in urine output?
    • Polydipsia or excessive water intake habits?
    • Recent head injury, neurosurgery or meningitis?
    • Medication review:
      • Diuretics – thiazides / loop – dose & duration
      • SSRIs / antipsychotics / carbamazepine?
      • ACE‑i, ARBs
      • PPIs, NSAIDs, MDMA or recreational drugs
    • Fluid intake pattern: litres per day? Alcohol binge?
    • Recent chest infection, pneumonia, or meningitis?

    BACKGROUND HISTORY

    • Past medical: Hypertension, diabetes, heart, liver, kidney disease, endocrine disorders
    • Drug history: as above, OTC painkillers, herbal supplements, recent medication changes
    • Allergies & reactions
    • Family history: endocrine, renal or electrolyte disorders
    • Social: smoking, alcohol (units/week), recreational drugs, occupation (heat exposure), diet (low‑salt?)
    • Functional: Activities of daily living, driving safety

    ICE – Ideas | Concerns | Expectations

    • Ideas: "What do you think might be causing the tiredness?"
    • Concerns: "Anything in particular you’re worried about?"
    • Expectations: "What were you hoping we could do for you today?"

    RED‑FLAG SCREEN

    • Seizures, severe confusion, new focal weakness
    • Severe vomiting or diarrhoea with dehydration
    • Chest pain, severe shortness of breath, collapse

    MANAGEMENT PLAN

    1. Risk Stratification & Urgency

    • Admit to hospital if Na < 125 mmol/L or any severe neurological symptoms (seizure, confusion, GCS drop) or hypovolaemia.
    • This case: Na 123 mmol/L with mild symptoms → senior discussion; likely same-day medical assessment unit (MAU) rather than routine GP follow-up.

    2. Explain Findings

    1. Define hyponatraemia & level (moderate, chronic likely).
    2. Link symptoms (fatigue, cramps, dizziness) to low sodium.
    3. Likely thiazide-induced but need to confirm and exclude other causes (endocrine, SIADH, fluid imbalance).

    3. Immediate Actions (Primary Care / While Awaiting Admission)

    1. Stop bendroflumethiazide immediately; substitute with ACE-i/ARB or CCB per hypertension guidance.
    2. Give patient ER leaflet on fluid restriction 1 L/day until reviewed (if euvolaemic).
    3. Advise normal dietary salt/protein intake, avoid excess free water/alcohol.
    4. Safety net: come to ED if headache, vomiting, confusion, seizure, collapse.

    4. Investigations

    • Repeat U&Es within 24 h; FBC, blood film.
    • Serum osmolality & paired urine osmolality + sodium (same sample).
    • TFTs (TSH, free T4) to exclude hypothyroidism.
    • Morning serum cortisol (08–09 h) ± Short Synacthen if low.
    • Chest X-ray if SIADH suspected (malignancy, pneumonia).
    • Random glucose to exclude hypertonic hyponatraemia.

    5. Volume Status–Directed Therapy

    • Examine for dehydration (skin turgor, mucous membranes), oedema, ascites, JVP.
    • If hypovolaemic → IV isotonic saline 0.9 % with monitoring.
    • If hypervolaemic (HF, cirrhosis) → fluid + salt restriction, loop diuretics, specialist referral.
    • If euvolaemic SIADH suspected → fluid restriction 800–1000 mL/day; consider demeclocycline/tolvaptan under specialist.

    6. Specialist Referral

    • Endocrinology if adrenal insufficiency or persistent euvolaemic hyponatraemia.
    • Renal team for persistent or severe cases or requiring hypertonic saline.
    • Respiratory / oncology if suspicion of paraneoplastic SIADH (e.g., lung mass on CXR).

    7. Follow-Up

    • Repeat sodium 24 h after medication stop; then every 48–72 h until stable > 130 mmol/L.
    • Blood pressure review after change of antihypertensive.
    • Arrange GP review in 1 week; earlier if labs worsen.

    8. Patient Education & Support

    • Provide written leaflet on hyponatraemia (symptoms, red-flags, diet, fluid balance).
    • Signpost to NHS website: www.nhs.uk/conditions/hyponatraemia (QR-code on leaflet).
    • Encourage partner to attend future consultations for shared understanding.

    9. Documentation & DVLA

    • Record capacity assessment, explanation, and consent for plan.
    • Discuss temporary fitness to drive; advise patient to avoid driving until sodium ≥ 125 mmol/L and dizziness resolved (DVLA guidance).
    • Create clear Plan/Problem list in notes; issue Med3 if symptomatic time-off needed.

    10. Teach-Back & Closing

    1. Ask: “Can you tell me in your own words what is causing your symptoms and what we’re doing next?”
    2. Correct misunderstandings, provide contact numbers (GP practice, 111, 999).
