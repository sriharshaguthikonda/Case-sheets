EPIPEN USAGE & ANAPHYLAXIS MANAGEMENT: COUNSELING A MOTHER

A. SCENARIO INTRODUCTION & INITIAL APPROACH (Video 0:22)

  • Typical Scenario: You are an FY2 doctor. Amanda, the mother of a 3-year-old child, has come to you. Her child was brought to the hospital for anaphylaxis for the second time. (Video 0:22-0:35)
  • Task Variation:
    • Teaching station: Mother doesn't know how to use the EpiPen; teach her. (Video 0:37-0:43)
    • Combined station: Take a brief history of anaphylaxis (symptoms, current status) and then teach EpiPen use. (Video 0:45-0:48)
  • History Taking (if combined station): (Video 0:48-0:57)
    • Symptoms the child had during the anaphylactic episode.
    • Child's current symptoms/condition.
    • Ask about other common anaphylaxis symptoms (referencing a list). (Video 0:57-1:19)
      • Difficulty/noisy breathing, swelling of throat/mouth, difficulty swallowing/talking, wheezing, persistent cough, loss of consciousness, collapse, pale/floppy (child), lightheadedness, faintness, confusion, tachycardia, anxiety, cyanosis.
    • Confirm the child's current well-being. (Video 1:23-1:26)
    • Crucial Question: "What is the allergen?" Identify the trigger for the allergic reaction. (Video 1:29-1:35) This is important for long-term management (avoidance). (Video 1:35-1:42)

B. EPIPEN DEVICE OVERVIEW (Video 1:42)

  • Types of Devices in Exam:
    • Original EpiPen. (Video 1:45)
    • EpiPen Trainer (most common in exams). (Video 1:49-1:51)
      • If trainer: Can demonstrate the steps physically. (Video 1:55)
      • If original: DO NOT demonstrate by activating. Verbally explain. (Video 1:57-2:00)
      • Distinguishing: Trainers will be clearly labeled "EpiPen Trainer." Originals will say "EpiPen Adrenaline Auto-injector." (Video 2:47-2:58)
  • Instructions on the Device: The steps for use are written directly on the EpiPen. Read and explain these to the mother. (Video 2:06-2:16)
  • Device Features:
    • Elliptical shape for easy grip. (Video 3:00-3:07)
    • Needle is never visible (safety mechanism). (Video 3:07-3:10, 3:25-3:29)
      • The orange tip extends to cover the needle after use. (Video 3:14-3:25)
    • One-time use only. (Video 3:10-3:12)

C. PRE-USE CHECKS FOR EPIPEN (Video 3:35)

  • Always carry two EpiPens. (Video 3:32-3:35)
  • Check Expiry Date: Ensure the pen is not expired. (Video 3:38-3:42)
  • Check Clear Window (Viewing Window): (Video 3:42-3:43)
    • In a real EpiPen, this window allows inspection of the adrenaline solution. (Video 3:45-3:52)
    • Solution should be clear and transparent. (Video 3:50-3:55)
    • Do NOT use if: The solution has changed color (pink, brown), is cloudy, or contains solid particles, even if not expired, as the medication has decayed. (Video 3:55-4:04)

D. EPIPEN ADMINISTRATION TECHNIQUE (DEMONSTRATION WITH TRAINER) (Video 2:22 (instructions read), Video 4:15 (demonstration start))

  1. "Pull off the blue safety cap." (This is the end opposite the orange tip). (Video 2:24-2:27, Video 4:19-4:20)
  2. "Hold the orange tip approximately 10 cm from the outer thigh." (Video 2:27-2:32)
  3. "Jab firmly into the outer thigh. Listen for a click." (The click indicates activation). (Video 2:32-2:37, Video 4:20-4:23 on own thigh)
  4. "Hold firmly against the thigh for 3 seconds." (Previously was 10 seconds, now 3 seconds as per this video's pen). (Video 2:37-2:39)
  5. Remove the EpiPen.
  6. Massage the injection site for about 10 seconds (not explicitly stated on this pen's instructions but common practice).

E. POST-ADMINISTRATION ACTIONS & ADVICE (Video 2:39)

  • Call Emergency Services: "Dial 999, ask for an ambulance, and state ANAPHYLAXIS." This ensures a priority response. (Video 2:39-2:41, Video 4:31-4:36)
  • While Waiting for Ambulance: (Video 4:42-4:45)
    • The patient should relax and not move around. (Video 4:51-4:54)
    • Positioning:
      • Lie down. (Video 4:54-4:57) (Legs raised if feeling faint - Video 6:02-6:04)
      • If short of breath: Head up (sit up). (Video 4:57-5:00, Video 6:06-6:09)
      • If nausea/vomiting: Left lateral position. (Video 5:00-5:03, Video 6:04-6:06)
      • Do not allow the patient to stand or walk. (Video 6:09-6:11)
  • Second EpiPen Dose: (Video 5:03)
    • If symptoms are not improving after 5 minutes, a second EpiPen can be used. (Video 5:06-5:12, Video 5:58-6:02 (time of administration), Video 6:18-6:22)
    • This is why carrying two EpiPens is recommended. (Video 5:12-5:14)
  • Disposal of Used EpiPens: Give used EpiPens to ambulance staff, pharmacist, or hospital doctors for safe disposal and replacement. (Video 5:18-5:27)
  • School Arrangements: Children often have two EpiPens for home and two for school. (Video 5:27-5:34)
  • Note the Time: Record the time the EpiPen was administered. (Video 5:37)
  • If in Doubt, Administer: If unsure whether symptoms are anaphylaxis or a milder allergy, it is safer to give the EpiPen. (Video 5:47-5:59)
  • Further Actions if Needed:
    • Call parents/emergency contact. (Video 6:11-6:15)
    • Commence CPR if there are no signs of life. (Video 6:22-6:25)
    • If asthmatic, give reliever inhaler via spacer if available. (Video 6:25-6:29)

F. MOTHER'S POTENTIAL QUESTIONS & ANSWERS (Video 6:33-6:38)

  • Q: If my child is wearing trousers, should I remove them?
    • A: No, you can administer the EpiPen through clothing (trousers). (Video 6:38-6:48)
  • Q: If I use a second EpiPen, should it be on the same leg or a different leg?
    • A: It doesn't matter; either leg is fine. (Video 6:48-6:57)

G. ALLERGEN AVOIDANCE & CONCLUDING ADVICE (Video 6:58-7:02)

  • Emphasize the importance of identifying and avoiding the known allergen. (Video 7:00-7:04)
  • Example: If allergic to peanuts, advise keeping the child away from peanuts and checking food labels (cookies, cakes) carefully. (Video 7:04-7:28)

Ensure the mother feels comfortable and confident in using the EpiPen trainer if provided. (Video 7:28-7:37)

HOW TO USE AN EPIPEN: A STEP-BY-STEP GUIDE

A. INTRODUCTION TO EPIPEN & ANAPHYLAXIS (Video 0:13)

  • Purpose of EpiPen: Used for anaphylaxis. (Video 0:20-0:24)
  • Signs & Symptoms of Anaphylaxis (Examples): (Video 0:24-0:44)
    • Cough, wheezing. (Video 0:26-0:30)
    • Rashes on the body. (Video 0:30-0:33)
    • Floppy child. (Video 0:37-0:39)
    • Tachycardia, peripheral cyanosis. (Video 0:39-0:43)
  • Cause of Anaphylaxis: Allergy to a substance (e.g., peanuts in foods like cookies, cakes). (Video 0:46-1:04)
  • Importance of Carrying an EpiPen: Individuals with a history of anaphylaxis should always carry an EpiPen. (Video 1:08-1:17)
  • Doctor's Role: Explain how to use the EpiPen. (Video 1:17-1:22)
  • Self-Explanatory Device: Instructions are printed on the EpiPen itself. (Video 1:26-1:31)
  • Using a Trainer Pen: The video uses an EpiPen trainer for demonstration purposes. An original pen should not be activated for demonstration. (Video 1:31-1:38)

B. PRE-USE CHECKS FOR EPIPEN (Video 1:38)

  • 1. Expiry Date: (Video 1:41-1:44)
    • Always check the expiry date on the original EpiPen. (Trainer pens show batch numbers). (Video 1:44-1:57)
  • 2. Clear Window (Viewing Window): (Video 1:57-1:59)
    • Inspect the medication through the clear window. (Video 1:59-2:01)
    • The solution should be transparent. (Video 2:03-2:07)
    • Do NOT use if: The solution has changed color (e.g., pink, brown), is cloudy, or contains sediments/particles. This indicates the medication has decayed, even if not expired. (Video 2:07-2:19)
    • Recap: Check expiry date and clear window before use. (Video 2:26-2:31)

C. EPIPEN ADMINISTRATION TECHNIQUE (Video 2:34)

  • Reading the Steps from the Pen: (Video 2:40-2:45)
    1. "Pull off the blue safety cap." (Video 2:49-2:52)
    2. "Hold orange tip approximately 10 centimeters from the outer thigh." (Video 2:52-2:58)
    3. "And jab firmly into the outer thigh. Listen for click." (The orange end is where the needle comes out). (Video 2:58-3:05)
    4. "Hold firmly against the thigh for three seconds." (Video 3:09-3:14)
      • Tip for counting 3 seconds under stress: Say "one thousand, two thousand, three thousand" or "one Mississippi, two Mississippi, three Mississippi" to ensure adequate time. (Video 3:25-3:55)
    5. "Then what you do, you dial triple nine (999), ask for an ambulance, and state anaphylaxis." (Video 3:14-3:20)
  • Holding Technique: (Video 3:58-4:00)
    • Hold with thumb on the blue side (or away from the orange tip). (Video 4:00-4:03)
    • Mnemonic: "Blue to the sky, orange to the thigh." (Video 4:03-4:09, 4:11-4:14)
  • Demonstration of Administration: (Video 4:26-4:45)
    • Hold correctly ("Blue to the sky, orange to the thigh"). (Video 4:28-4:30)
    • Remove the blue safety cap. (Video 4:30-4:33)
    • Hold 10 cm away from the outer thigh. (Video 4:33-4:37)
    • Jab firmly into the outer thigh. Count: "One Mississippi, two Mississippi, three Mississippi." (Video 4:37-4:42)
    • Remove pen.

D. POST-ADMINISTRATION & ADDITIONAL ADVICE (Video 4:45)

  • One-Time Use: EpiPens are for single use only. (Video 4:45-4:48)
  • Carry Two EpiPens: Always advise carrying two EpiPens. (Video 4:48-4:52)
  • Administering Through Clothing: It is acceptable to administer the EpiPen through trousers/clothing. (Video 4:52-4:58)
  • If in Doubt, Give EpiPen: If unsure whether it's a severe allergic reaction (anaphylaxis) or just a mild allergy, it's safer to administer the EpiPen. "Give EpiPen, save lives." (Video 4:58-5:06)
  • While Waiting for Ambulance (Patient Positioning): (Video 5:10-5:16)
    • Patient should remain calm and avoid moving or running around. (Video 5:16-5:22)
    • Sit or lie down. (Video 5:22-5:28)
    • If nausea/vomiting: Left lateral position. (Video 5:28-5:30)
    • If breathing difficulty: Head up (sit up). (Video 5:30-5:34)
  • Second Dose: If symptoms are not improving after 5 minutes, the second EpiPen can be used. (Video 5:36-5:44, 5:48-5:52)
  • Disposal & Replacement: Give the used EpiPen(s) to ambulance staff, hospital staff, or return to a pharmacy for safe disposal and to arrange for a replacement. (Video 5:55-6:08)
  • Allergen Avoidance:
    • Identify and avoid the trigger. (Video 6:10-6:14)
    • Advise checking ingredients in foods (e.g., for peanuts in cakes, cookies). (Video 6:14-6:30)
  • Provide Warning Signs & Leaflets: Explain warning signs of anaphylaxis and consider providing informational leaflets. (Video 6:30-6:36)

Reiterate that all instructions are typically written on the EpiPen device itself. (Video 6:36-6:43)
