1st COMPLAINT: Redness
  • S: Site & Laterality
    • Which eye?
      • One eye (Unilateral)
      • Both eyes (Bilateral)
    • Location on eye
      • Localized (sectoral)
      • Diffuse (generalized)
      • Ciliary flush (ring around iris)
  • O: Onset
    • Gradual
    • Sudden
      • e.g., after trauma, cough, sneeze → Subconjunctival Hemorrhage
  • D: Duration (linked with pain)
    • Short + Pain → Inflammatory
    • Long + Less/No Pain → Chronic Inflammation / Benign
    • Long + No Pain → Malignant
  • A/R: Aggravating / Relieving Factors
    • Aggravating Factors
      • Light (Photophobia)
      • Screen use
      • Wind / Dry air
      • Contact lens wear
    • Relieving Factors
      • Darkness
      • Eye drops
      • Rest / Closing eyes
  • C: Character/Type
    • Of redness
      • Bright red patch
      • Pink / Injected vessels
    • Of associated sensation
      • Pain (dull, sharp, aching, throbbing)
      • Gritty / Foreign body sensation
      • Itching
      • Burning
    • Of discharge
      • Watery (viral, allergic)
      • Purulent / pus-like (bacterial)
      • Mucoid / stringy (allergic)
  • R: Radiation
    • If pain present, does it radiate?
      • Forehead / Brow
      • Temple
      • Jaw / Teeth
  • A: Associated Symptoms
    • Pain / headache
      • No → Subconjunctival Hemorrhage
      • Yes → Glaucoma, Keratitis, Uveitis
    • Vision changes
      • Blurred vision
      • Halos around lights
    • Nausea & Vomiting
    • Fever
    • Interference with eye movement
  • P: Progression
    • Progressive
      • Very rapid → Inflammatory / Infectious
      • Rapidly → Malignant
      • Slowly → Benign
    • Regressive (Resolving)
    • Static (Unchanging)
    • Static → Rapid (Benign → Malignant change)
  • P: Pattern
    • Continuous
    • Intermittent
      • Worse in morning (Blepharitis)
      • Worse in evening (Dry Eye)
      • Associated with allergens (Allergic Conjunctivitis)
  • E: Effect on life
    • Impacting daily activities
      • Driving
      • Reading
      • Work / School
  • R: Red Flags
    • Significant vision loss
    • Severe, deep eye pain
    • Hazy / cloudy cornea
    • Fixed or irregular pupil
    • Vomiting (with headache/pain)
    • Recent chemical or penetrating injury
  • S: Scale (0-10)
    • Severity of pain
    • Severity of itching
    • Severity of light sensitivity
2ND COMPLAINT: EYE PAIN / HEAD PAIN (Related to Ophthalmic Conditions)
  1. SITE (S):
    • Where exactly is the pain located?
      • Is it in one eye (unilateral) or
      • both eyes (bilateral)?
      • Is the pain felt
        • within the eye itself,
        • around the eye (periorbital), or
        • more like a headache?
      • If headache, is it localized?
        • Frontal?
        • Temporal? (Giant Cell if temporal, especially in older adults)
        • Occipital?
        • Generalized?
      • Is it a surface pain (like something in the eye) or a deep, aching pain?
      • Can you point to the exact spot of maximum pain? (e.g., specific area of sclera for Scleritis)
  2. ONSET (O):
    • Sudden? (e.g., after trauma, acute angle closure Glaucoma)
    • Gradual over hours/days/weeks?
    • Was there anything specific that seemed to trigger the pain? (e.g., injury, bright light exposure, prolonged reading, specific activity)
  3. DURATION (D):
    • How long have you been experiencing this pain? (Hours, days, weeks, months)
    • Â± Painlessness (This section from your original HTML seems more relevant to characterizing a *lesion/swelling* rather than pain itself. For pain, we'd focus on its presence/absence/fluctuation.)
      1. Short = Inflammatory [Pain]
      2. A bit long = Malignant [No pain]
      3. Long = Chronic inflammation [Less pain]
      4. Long = Benign [No pain]
  4. AGGRAVATING/RELIEVING FACTORS (A/R):
    • Aggravating Factors: What makes the pain worse?
      • Eye movement? (Consider: Optic Neuritis, Scleritis)
      • Bright lights (photophobia)?
      • Darkness or dim light? (Consider: Acute Angle Closure Glaucoma - AACG)
      • Reading or close work?
      • Touching or pressure on the eye/head?
      • Heat or cold?
      • Bending over or straining?
      • Specific times of day?
        • Morning -Intracranial hypertension
    • Relieving Factors: What makes the pain better or go away?
      • Resting the eyes?
      • Dark room / avoiding light?
      • Pain medications (OTC or prescribed)? Which ones and do they help?
      • Cold or warm compresses?
      • Specific positions?
      • Does pressure on the eye relieve or worsen it?

  5. CHARACTER/TYPE (C):
    • How would you describe the pain? (Ask patient to use their own words, then offer prompts if needed)
      • Sharp, stabbing, shooting?
      • Dull, aching, throbbing?
      • Burning, gritty, foreign body sensation?
      • Pressure-like, tight band? (Consider: Glaucoma if pressure-like headache)
      • Boring?
  6. RADIATION (R):
    • Does the pain spread or radiate anywhere else?
      • To the other eye?
      • To the temple, jaw, neck?
      • Around the head?
  7. ASSOCIATED SYMPTOMS (A):
    • Visual Changes:
      • Blurred vision? (Sudden or gradual?)
      • Seeing halos around lights? (Consider: AACG)
      • Photophobia (sensitivity to light)?
      • Loss of vision (partial or complete)?
      • Floaters or flashes of light?
      • Double vision (diplopia)?
    • Eye Appearance:
      • Redness of the eye? (Localized or diffuse?)
      • Watering or discharge from the eye? (Nature of discharge: watery, mucoid, purulent)
      • Swelling of the eyelids or around the eye?
      • Pupil changes noticed by patient?
    • Systemic Symptoms:
      • Nausea and/or Vomiting? (Common with AACG, migraine)
      • Fever, chills? (Suggests infection)
      • Headache (if primary complaint is eye pain, and vice versa)?
      • Phonophobia (sensitivity to sound)?
    • Symptoms related to specific conditions:
      • Pain or difficulty interfering with eye movement? (Consider: Optic Neuritis, orbital inflammation)
      • Symptoms of Polymyalgia Rheumatica (aching/stiffness in shoulders, hips)? (Associated with Giant Cell Arteritis)
      • Symptoms of Rheumatoid Arthritis or other autoimmune conditions? (Associated with Scleritis, Uveitis)
      • Scalp tenderness, jaw claudication? (Associated with Giant Cell Arteritis)
      • Recent cold/sinus infection? (Sinusitis can cause referred eye/head pain)
  8. PROGRESSION (P):
    • How has the pain changed since it first started?
      • Is it getting progressively worse, better, or staying the same?
      • If it's recurrent, are the episodes becoming more frequent, more severe, or lasting longer? (Consider: Optic Neuritis if recurrent with visual disturbance)
    • Response to any previous treatments?
      1. Very rapid = Inflammatory
      2. Rapidly = Malignant
      3. Slowly = Benign
    • Regressive (inflammatory)
      • - This could be relevant if pain is improving
    • Static
    • Static  --> Rapid [Benign --> Malignant]
      • - More for lesions/swellings 
  9. PATTERN (P):
    • Is the pain continuous or intermittent?
    • If intermittent, is there any pattern to when it occurs? (e.g., time of day, after specific activities)
    • Any periods completely free of pain?
  10. RED FLAGS (R): (Symptoms suggesting a serious underlying cause needing urgent attention)
    • Sudden, severe, unrelenting eye pain.
    • Sudden loss of vision.
    • Marked eye redness with a fixed, mid-dilated pupil (suggests AACG).
    • Photophobia with deep eye ache (suggests iritis/uveitis).
    • Proptosis (bulging of the eye).
    • Pain with eye movements, especially with vision loss (suggests Optic Neuritis).
    • New onset headache in an older adult, especially with temporal tenderness or jaw claudication (Giant Cell Arteritis).
    • Associated neurological deficits (e.g., weakness, numbness, diplopia not resolving).
    • History of recent eye surgery or trauma with increasing pain.
    • Vomiting associated with eye pain/headache.
    • Intracranial hypertension
      • -Seizures

  11. EFFECT ON LIFE (E):
    • How is this pain affecting your daily activities (e.g., ability to read, work, drive, sleep)?
    • Has it caused you to miss work or school?
    • How is it affecting your mood or general well-being?
  12. SCALE (S) (Severity):
    • On a scale of 0 to 10, where 0 is no pain and 10 is the worst pain imaginable:
      • What number would you give your pain right now?
      • What is its average severity?
      • What is its severity at its worst?

FUNDOSCOPY PROTOCOL

Ensure appropriate setting (dimmed room if possible), patient comfort, and proper ophthalmoscope use (correct lens, appropriate distance). Examine both eyes, comparing findings. Start with the patient looking straight ahead, then ask them to look in different directions to visualize different quadrants of the retina. Finally, ask the patient to look directly at the light to view the macula.

Assess the Red Reflex:
  • Presence and Character:
    • Is the red reflex present
    • symmetrical in both eyes?
    • Is it bright and orange-red, or dull/absent?
    • Are there any
      • opacities,
      • dark spots, or
      • white reflections (leukocoria) within the red reflex?
  • Potential Abnormalities:
    • Absent or diminished red reflex:
      • Cataracts,
      • vitreous hemorrhage,
      • large retinal detachment,
      • severe corneal opacity.
    • Leukocoria (white reflex):
      • Retinoblastoma (in children),
      • cataract,
      • Coats' disease,
      • persistent fetal vasculature.
    • Dark spots:
      • Floaters,
      • localized opacities.
Examine the Optic Disc (Optic Nerve Head):
  • Colour:
    • Normally pinkish-orange
    • or
    • yellowish-orange.

    • Is it pale (atrophy) or
    • hyperemic (swollen/inflamed)?
  • Contour / Margins:
    • Are the disc margins
      • sharp and distinct, or
      • blurred/indistinct?
    • Is there elevation of the disc (papilledema)?
  • Cup-to-Disc Ratio (C/D Ratio):
    • Assess the size of the central physiological cup relative to the entire disc diameter (e.g., 0.3).
    • Is the cup symmetrically sized between the two eyes?
    • Is there cupping (enlargement of the cup, >0.5-0.6) or notching?
  • Spontaneous Venous Pulsation (SVP):
    • Often present in healthy individuals (subtle pulsation of retinal veins as they cross the disc).
    • Absence can be normal but new absence in the presence of other signs might be significant.
  • Potential Abnormalities:
    • Papilledema (swollen disc, blurred margins, elevated):
      • Raised intracranial pressure.
    • Optic atrophy (pale disc):
      • Previous optic nerve damage,
      • glaucoma,
      • ischemia.
    • Glaucomatous cupping (increased C/D ratio, notching):
      • -Glaucoma.
    • Optic disc drusen:
      • Hyaline bodies,
      • can mimic papilledema.
    • Optic neuritis: Swollen, hyperemic disc, often with pain on eye movement and vision loss.
Examine the Retinal Vessels (Arteries and Veins):
  • Artery-to-Vein (A/V) Ratio:
    • Normally veins are slightly larger than arteries (A/V ratio approx. 2:3 or 4:5).
    • Is there arterial narrowing (attenuation) or venous dilation?
  • Arterial Light Reflex:
    • Observe the central light streak on arteries.
    • Is it normal, broadened ("copper wiring"), or obscured ("silver wiring")?
  • Arteriovenous (AV) Crossing Changes:
    • Where arteries cross veins, observe for:
      • Nipping/constriction of the vein.
      • Banking/deflection of the vein.
      • Salus' sign (deflection of vein).
      • Gunn's sign (tapering of vein on either side of crossing).
  • Vessel Tortuosity:
    • Are the vessels abnormally twisted or tortuous?
  • Pulsations:
    • Arterial pulsations are usually abnormal (unless very low IOP).Note spontaneous venous pulsations at the disc.
  • Potential Abnormalities:
    • Arterial narrowing, AV nipping, copper/silver wiring:
      • -Hypertensive retinopathy.
    • Venous dilation and tortuosity, retinal hemorrhages (flame-shaped, dot/blot):
      • Diabetic retinopathy,
      • retinal vein occlusion.
    • Emboli (e.g., Hollenhorst plaques):
      • Retinal artery occlusion.
    • Neovascularization (new abnormal vessel growth):
      • -Proliferative diabetic retinopathy,
      • vein occlusions.
Examine the Retinal Background / General Fundus:
  • Colour and Pigmentation:
    • Note the general colour (orange-red) and any variations in pigmentation.
  • Retinal Nerve Fibre Layer (RNFL):
    • Subtle striations radiating from the optic disc, best seen with green (red-free) light.
    • Look for defects or thinning (can indicate glaucoma or optic neuropathy).
  • Haemorrhages:
    • Type:
    • Flame-shaped (nerve fibre layer),
    • dot/blot (deeper retinal layers),
    • pre-retinal (boat-shaped).

    • Location and extent.
  • Exudates:
    • Hard exudates: Yellowish, waxy deposits (lipid residue from leaky vessels).
    • Cotton wool spots (soft exudates): White, fluffy patches (axoplasmic debris from retinal ischemia).
  • Other Lesions:
    • Drusen: Yellowish deposits (associated with age-related macular degeneration if in macula).
    • Pigmentary changes, scarring.
  • Potential Abnormalities:
    • Haemorrhages, exudates, microaneurysms: Diabetic retinopathy, hypertensive retinopathy, vein occlusions.
    • Retinal detachment: Retina appears elevated, undulating, sometimes with a visible tear.
    • Choroiditis/Retinitis: Inflammatory patches.
Examine the Macula and Fovea:
  • Location: Typically temporal to the optic disc (approx. 2 disc diameters away).
  • Foveal Reflex:
    • A small, bright pinpoint light reflex at the center of the fovea (may be absent in older individuals or with macular pathology).
  • Appearance:
    • The macula is a darker, avascular area.
    • Look for any abnormalities: Haemorrhages, exudates, drusen, pigmentary changes, swelling, cysts.
  • Potential Abnormalities:
    • Age-related Macular Degeneration (AMD): Drusen, pigmentary changes, neovascularization, haemorrhage.
    • Diabetic Maculopathy: Exudates, macular edema.
    • Macular hole.
    • Cherry-red spot: Central retinal artery occlusion.
Examine the Vitreous (if visible):
  • Look for floaters, haziness, or cells.
  • Potential Abnormalities: Vitreous haemorrhage, uveitis (inflammatory cells).
Document Findings:
  • Systematically record findings for each eye, using diagrams if helpful.
  • Note any asymmetries between the eyes.
