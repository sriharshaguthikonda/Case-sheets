    FUNDOSCOPY PROTOCOL

    Ensure appropriate setting (dimmed room if possible), patient comfort, and proper ophthalmoscope use (correct lens, appropriate distance). Examine both eyes, comparing findings. Start with the patient looking straight ahead, then ask them to look in different directions to visualize different quadrants of the retina. Finally, ask the patient to look directly at the light to view the macula.

    Assess the Red Reflex:
    • Presence and Character:
      • Is the red reflex present
      • symmetrical in both eyes?
      • Is it bright and orange-red, or dull/absent?
      • Are there any
        • opacities,
        • dark spots, or
        • white reflections (leukocoria) within the red reflex?
    • Potential Abnormalities:
      • Absent or diminished red reflex:
        • Cataracts,
        • vitreous hemorrhage,
        • large retinal detachment,
        • severe corneal opacity.
      • Leukocoria (white reflex):
        • Retinoblastoma (in children),
        • cataract,
        • Coats' disease,
        • persistent fetal vasculature.
      • Dark spots:
        • Floaters,
        • localized opacities.
    Examine the Optic Disc (Optic Nerve Head):
    • Colour:
      • Normally pinkish-orange
      • or
      • yellowish-orange.

      • Is it pale (atrophy) or
      • hyperemic (swollen/inflamed)?
    • Contour / Margins:
      • Are the disc margins
        • sharp and distinct, or
        • blurred/indistinct?
      • Is there elevation of the disc (papilledema)?
    • Cup-to-Disc Ratio (C/D Ratio):
      • Assess the size of the central physiological cup relative to the entire disc diameter (e.g., 0.3).
      • Is the cup symmetrically sized between the two eyes?
      • Is there cupping (enlargement of the cup, >0.5-0.6) or notching?
    • Spontaneous Venous Pulsation (SVP):
      • Often present in healthy individuals (subtle pulsation of retinal veins as they cross the disc).
      • Absence can be normal but new absence in the presence of other signs might be significant.
    • Potential Abnormalities:
      • Papilledema (swollen disc, blurred margins, elevated):
        • Raised intracranial pressure.
      • Optic atrophy (pale disc):
        • Previous optic nerve damage,
        • glaucoma,
        • ischemia.
      • Glaucomatous cupping (increased C/D ratio, notching):
        • -Glaucoma.
      • Optic disc drusen:
        • Hyaline bodies,
        • can mimic papilledema.
      • Optic neuritis: Swollen, hyperemic disc, often with pain on eye movement and vision loss.
    Examine the Retinal Vessels (Arteries and Veins):
    • Artery-to-Vein (A/V) Ratio:
      • Normally veins are slightly larger than arteries (A/V ratio approx. 2:3 or 4:5).
      • Is there arterial narrowing (attenuation) or venous dilation?
    • Arterial Light Reflex:
      • Observe the central light streak on arteries.
      • Is it normal, broadened ("copper wiring"), or obscured ("silver wiring")?
    • Arteriovenous (AV) Crossing Changes:
      • Where arteries cross veins, observe for:
        • Nipping/constriction of the vein.
        • Banking/deflection of the vein.
        • Salus' sign (deflection of vein).
        • Gunn's sign (tapering of vein on either side of crossing).
    • Vessel Tortuosity:
      • Are the vessels abnormally twisted or tortuous?
    • Pulsations:
      • Arterial pulsations are usually abnormal (unless very low IOP).Note spontaneous venous pulsations at the disc.
    • Potential Abnormalities:
      • Arterial narrowing, AV nipping, copper/silver wiring:
        • -Hypertensive retinopathy.
      • Venous dilation and tortuosity, retinal hemorrhages (flame-shaped, dot/blot):
        • Diabetic retinopathy,
        • retinal vein occlusion.
      • Emboli (e.g., Hollenhorst plaques):
        • Retinal artery occlusion.
      • Neovascularization (new abnormal vessel growth):
        • -Proliferative diabetic retinopathy,
        • vein occlusions.
    Examine the Retinal Background / General Fundus:
    • Colour and Pigmentation:
      • Note the general colour (orange-red) and any variations in pigmentation.
    • Retinal Nerve Fibre Layer (RNFL):
      • Subtle striations radiating from the optic disc, best seen with green (red-free) light.
      • Look for defects or thinning (can indicate glaucoma or optic neuropathy).
    • Haemorrhages:
      • Type:
      • Flame-shaped (nerve fibre layer),
      • dot/blot (deeper retinal layers),
      • pre-retinal (boat-shaped).

      • Location and extent.
    • Exudates:
      • Hard exudates: Yellowish, waxy deposits (lipid residue from leaky vessels).
      • Cotton wool spots (soft exudates): White, fluffy patches (axoplasmic debris from retinal ischemia).
    • Other Lesions:
      • Drusen: Yellowish deposits (associated with age-related macular degeneration if in macula).
      • Pigmentary changes, scarring.
    • Potential Abnormalities:
      • Haemorrhages, exudates, microaneurysms: Diabetic retinopathy, hypertensive retinopathy, vein occlusions.
      • Retinal detachment: Retina appears elevated, undulating, sometimes with a visible tear.
      • Choroiditis/Retinitis: Inflammatory patches.
    Examine the Macula and Fovea:
    • Location: Typically temporal to the optic disc (approx. 2 disc diameters away).
    • Foveal Reflex:
      • A small, bright pinpoint light reflex at the center of the fovea (may be absent in older individuals or with macular pathology).
    • Appearance:
      • The macula is a darker, avascular area.
      • Look for any abnormalities: Haemorrhages, exudates, drusen, pigmentary changes, swelling, cysts.
    • Potential Abnormalities:
      • Age-related Macular Degeneration (AMD): Drusen, pigmentary changes, neovascularization, haemorrhage.
      • Diabetic Maculopathy: Exudates, macular edema.
      • Macular hole.
      • Cherry-red spot: Central retinal artery occlusion.
    Examine the Vitreous (if visible):
    • Look for floaters, haziness, or cells.
    • Potential Abnormalities: Vitreous haemorrhage, uveitis (inflammatory cells).
    Document Findings:
    • Systematically record findings for each eye, using diagrams if helpful.
    • Note any asymmetries between the eyes.
