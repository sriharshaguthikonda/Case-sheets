HISTORY OF PRESENTING COMPLAINT — SODA CRAPPERS

1️⃣ Generalised “Fit” (Febrile Convulsion)

  • Site / type — “Did the shaking involve the whole body or start on one side?”
  • Onset — “Exactly what time did it begin, and was it during a fever spike?”
  • Duration — “Roughly how many seconds or minutes did the jerking last?” (NICE)
  • Aggravating / Relieving — “Any lights, sounds or positions that seemed to trigger or stop it?”
  • Character — Rhythmical/tonic-clonic? Eyes open? Cyanosis?
  • Radiation — N/A (generalised)
  • Associated symptoms — Tongue-bite, incontinence, post-ictal confusion, vomiting.
  • Progression / pattern — “Any earlier small jerks or spasms this week?”
  • Effect on life — Parental anxiety, sleep disruption, child’s play.
  • Red flags — Focal deficits, prolonged > 15 min, status epilepticus (NG217)
  • Severity (0-10) — Parental perceived danger.

2️⃣ Fever & Ear-pain

  • Site
  • Onset
  • Duration / max temp
  • A/R — Paracetamol/ibuprofen response (NG91 AOM)
  • Character
  • Associated — otorrhoea, reduced hearing, URTI symptoms
  • Red flags — photophobia, neck-stiffness, non-blanching rash

3️⃣ Systemic Review

  • Cough, work of breathing, wheeze
  • Vomiting, diarrhoea, reduced feeds
  • Dizziness, pallor, cyanosis
  • Rash (meningococcal)
  • Headache, fontanelle bulge, photophobia

4️⃣ Background

  • Past medical: previous seizures, chronic conditions, hospitalisations
  • Birth & developmental milestones
  • Vaccination record (confirm MenB / MMR dates)
  • Drug history / allergies (reaction type)
  • Family history: epilepsy, febrile convulsions
  • Social / safeguarding: carers, smoking, housing, parental support
  • ICE: Ideas (epilepsy?), Concerns (brain damage), Expectations (plan)

MANAGEMENT PLAN

OBO Urgency

  • Admit for observation if ≥ 2 seizures in 24 h, focal deficit, or red-flags (meningism, petechiae, persistent drowsiness).
  • Otherwise: observe 4 h afebrile then discharge with safety-net.

Immediate actions

  1. Repeat vital signs; bedside glucose.
  2. If seizure recurs > 5 min → IV/BUC lorazepam per weight (hospital protocol).
  3. Antipyretic comfort care: paracetamol 15 mg/kg PO; alternate with ibuprofen 10 mg/kg 6-hourly.

Otitis media therapy

  • Explain natural course (usually resolves in 3 days) NICE NG91.
  • Offer analgesia-first strategy plus anaesthetic ear-drops.
  • Give delayed amoxicillin 5-day script (30 mg/kg/day TDS) to be used if no improvement or discharge appears.

Parent education & resources

  • Demonstrate seizure first-aid (recovery position, remove hazards, time the fit).
  • Provide NHS leaflets: “Febrile convulsions” and “Ear infection — does my child need antibiotics?”.
  • Teach-back: ask Diana to repeat red-flags and first-aid steps.

Safety-net & follow-up

  • Call 999 if fit ≥ 5 min, repeated within same illness, breathing difficulty or unresponsive.
  • Same-day review if fever > 5 days, non-blanching rash, neck-stiffness, ear swelling.
  • GP review in 48 h if ear-pain not settled; routine hearing check at 3 months.

Documentation

  • Record full history, exam, mum’s understanding, safety-netting, weight-based doses.
  • Code SNOMED: 19154000 (Simple febrile convulsion), H65.9 (Acute otitis media).

11-Point Qualitative Feedback

  1. Language : Prefer plain English – e.g. “keep her temperature down” instead of “control fever and other precautionary measures”.
  2. Listening : Follow ear-tugging cue with pain/hearing questions.
  3. Rapport : Begin with explicit empathy: “I can see how scary that was.”
  4. Issues : Elicit mother’s biggest worry early (ICE framework).
  5. Consultation structure : Signpost sections to avoid topic-hopping.
  6. Professionalism : Offer chaperone for ear exam (GMC Good Medical Practice).
  7. Cultural sensitivity : Check for medication or cultural preferences.
  8. Handling difficulty : Validate epilepsy fear before explaining.
  9. Teamwork : State escalation to paeds registrar if repeat seizure.
  10. Self-awareness : Acknowledge need to confirm antibiotic plan with senior.
  11. Documentation : Record weight-based dosing and teach-back.
