TEACHING THE CANCER PATHWAY

A. INTRODUCTION & INITIAL INTERACTION (Video 0:19)

  • Scenario: You (FY2 doctor) are asked to teach a final year medical student about the cancer pathway. (Video 0:19-0:27)
  • Initial Steps:
    • Build rapport: "Hello, how are you?" (Video 0:27-0:32)
    • Assess prior knowledge: "How much do you know? What do you want to learn?" (Video 0:32-0:34)

B. OVERVIEW OF CANCER DETECTION PATHWAYS (Video 0:37)

Explain that cancer can be detected through several routes:

  • 1. Two-Week Urgent Pathway (GP Referral): (Video 0:43-0:45)
    • Patient presents to GP with suspicious symptoms.
    • GP performs initial investigations. (Video 0:48-0:51)
    • If cancer is suspected, patient is referred to a specialist and should be seen within two weeks. (Video 0:53-0:57)
    • Specialist conducts further investigations. (Video 0:57-1:02)
  • 2. A&E Pathway: (Video 1:02-1:04)
    • Patient presents to Accident & Emergency directly due to severe symptoms or acute condition. (Video 1:04-1:16)
    • Cancer is diagnosed or highly suspected during A&E assessment. (Video 1:16-1:22)
    • Specialist involvement and further investigations follow. (Video 1:24-1:29)
  • 3. Screening Pathway: (Video 1:29-1:32)
    • Cancers detected through national screening programs.
    • Examples: Breast screening, cervical screening. (Video 1:32-1:42)
    • PSA testing (informed choice program for men) can also lead to detection. (Video 1:42-1:51)
    • If abnormalities are found during screening, further investigations and referrals are made. (Video 1:53-2:06)
  • 4. Incidental Findings Pathway: (Video 2:06-2:08)
    • Cancer detected unexpectedly during investigations for an unrelated condition. (Video 2:08-2:14)

Focus teaching time on the management steps rather than just these detection routes. (Video 2:27-2:34)

C. STEPS IN THE CANCER MANAGEMENT PATHWAY (POST-SUSPICION) (Video 2:34)

Using Lung Cancer as an Example for Clarity: (Video 2:38-2:45)

  • 1. Suspicion & Initial GP Assessment: (Video 2:47)
    • Patient presents with symptoms suggestive of cancer (e.g., for lung cancer: blood in phlegm, weight loss, appetite loss, tiredness, lumps, smoking history). (Video 2:50-3:02)
  • 2. Referral to Specialist: (Video 3:04-3:07)
    • GP refers patient to a specialist (e.g., respiratory physician for suspected lung cancer).
    • Patient should be seen by the specialist within two weeks (urgent pathway). (Video 3:07-3:13)
  • 3. Diagnostic Tests: (Video 3:16-3:18)
    • Specialist arranges further investigations to confirm diagnosis and determine the type/extent of cancer.
    • Example (Lung Cancer): Bronchoscopy (with biopsy if needed), CT scan, CT-guided biopsy. (Video 3:20-3:39)
  • 4. Multidisciplinary Team (MDT) Meeting: (Video 3:50)
    • Once diagnosis and staging information is available, the case is discussed in an MDT meeting. (Video 3:50-3:52)
    • The MDT decides on the best possible care plan for the patient. (Video 3:52-3:56)
    • MDT Members (Examples): GPs, surgeons, physicians (e.g., respiratory), oncologists (radiotherapist, medical oncologist), specialist nurses, occupational therapists, physiotherapists, palliative care specialists. (Video 3:58-4:16)
  • 5. Discussing Treatment Options with Patient: (Video 4:17-4:24)
    • The MDT's recommendations and treatment options are explained to the patient. (Video 4:24-4:28)
    • Treatment is decided collaboratively with the patient; it's not solely the doctor's decision. (Video 4:28-4:32)
    • Patient has the right to select or refuse treatment options. (Video 4:42-4:51)
    • Present options in the patient's best interest. (Video 4:51-4:57)
    • Types of Treatment (Examples): Surgery, chemotherapy, radiotherapy, or combinations. (Video 4:58-5:04)
  • 6. Follow-up and Monitoring: (Video 5:10-5:12)
    • After treatment, regular follow-up appointments and monitoring are essential. (Video 5:12-5:24)
  • 7. Palliative Care & End-of-Life Care: (Video 5:26-5:30)
    • Discussion about palliative care plans, which focus on comfort and quality of life. (Video 5:26-5:32, 5:43-5:48)
    • Care can be provided at home, in a care home, or hospice. (Video 5:32-5:39)
    • Palliative care is a broad term; end-of-life care is a part of it, focusing on the last few days/weeks. (Video 5:48-5:56)
    • May involve medications for symptom control (e.g., morphine, hyoscine, antiemetics). (Video 5:56-6:06)

Teaching these steps using a specific cancer example (like lung or bladder cancer) makes it easier for the student to understand. (Video 6:38-7:14)

The main focus for the exam is explaining these core management pathway steps. (Video 7:10-7:27)
