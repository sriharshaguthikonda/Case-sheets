    INDICATIONS FOR SURGERY IN MNG

    Multinodular Goitre (MNG) alone is not an indication for surgery. Surgery is considered based on specific clinical factors:

    1. Large size causing pressure symptoms
    2. Malignant change (usually into follicular carcinoma)
      1. Fine Needle Aspiration Cytology (FNAC)
      2. Clinical suspicion based on:
        • Age
        • Male sex
        • Recent rapid growth (compared to sudden rapid growth due to hemorrhage)
        • Recurrent Laryngeal Nerve (RLN) involvement (e.g., change in voice, similar to parotid mixed tumor transformation involving facial nerve)
        • Hard texture
        • Fixity
        • Lymphadenopathy
    3. Toxic change to Toxic Multinodular Goitre (TMNG) (secondary thyrotoxicosis)
    4. Cosmetic concerns (thyroid disorders are common in young females)
    5. Patient’s wishes

    6. Subtotal Thyroidectomy

    Indications:

    • MNG (Multinodular Goitre)
      • 7/8th gland removed or
      • 5-10gms gland left in tracheoesophageal groove
    • Both are difficult on-table assessment of tissue to be left

    In toxic patients, thumb distal phalanx size tissue left on both sides.
    In euthyroid patients, little finger distal phalanx size left on both sides.

    Reasons for tracheoesophageal groove:

    • To prevent injury to Recurrent Laryngeal Nerve (RLN)
    • To preserve parathyroid glands
    • Even after cutting blood supply to gland, it can survive because of:
      • Esophageal artery
      • Tracheal artery

    Complications: Thyrotoxicosis

    Treatment: Anti-thyroid drugs

    Adverse Drug Reaction (ADR): Agranulocytosis (patient can die of it)

    Due to risk of complications, shifting towards near/total thyroidectomy. Even if hypothyroidism occurs, Hormone Replacement Therapy (HRT) is easier.

    Incision: Kocher’s Incision

    Emil Theodor Kocher was a Swiss physician and medical researcher who received the 1909 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine for his work in the physiology, pathology, and surgery of the thyroid. Among his many accomplishments are the introduction and promotion of aseptic surgery and scientific methods in surgery, specifically reducing the mortality of thyroidectomies to less than 1% in his operations.

    He was the first Swiss citizen and the first surgeon to ever receive a Nobel Prize. He was considered a pioneer and leader in the field of surgery in his time. Kocher’s forceps, a surgical instrument with serrated blades and interlocking teeth at the tips used to control bleeding in surgery, is named after him.
