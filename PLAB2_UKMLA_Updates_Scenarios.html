SCENARIO 1: COUNSELING WIFE OF PATIENT WITH FRONTO-TEMPORAL DEMENTIA (Video 0:25)

  • Situation: In GP surgery, a 65-year-old female patient has brought in her husband, Mr. White (69), who has been diagnosed with Fronto-temporal Dementia (FTD). The task is to talk to the wife and address her concerns. This is primarily a counseling station. (Video 0:25-1:05)
  • Understanding Fronto-temporal Dementia (FTD): (Not for direct explanation to patient, but for doctor's understanding) (Video 1:05-1:19)
    • Affects frontal and temporal lobes of the brain. (Video 1:26-1:35)
    • These lobes control behavior, emotions, and language. (Video 1:35-1:40)
    • Common Manifestations: (Video 1:47)
      • Changes in behavior (e.g., impulsivity, poor hygiene). (Video 1:55, 2:21-2:27)
      • Trouble with speech (e.g., talking slowly, using wrong words, mixing up sentences). (Video 1:55, 2:03-2:10)
      • Difficulty with thinking, easily distracted. (Video 1:56, 2:16-2:21)
      • Memory problems. (Video 1:56, 2:33)
    • Cause: Abnormal protein buildup in the brain. (Video 2:38-2:45)
    • Diagnosis: CT or MRI scan. Lumbar Puncture (LP) may be done to rule out other conditions like Alzheimer's. (Video 2:45-2:58)
    • Treatment: Symptomatic management based on individual patient presentation. Includes medications for behavior, CBT, physiotherapy, occupational therapy, and communication aids. (Video 3:00-3:30)
  • Approaching the Wife's Concerns: (Video 3:36)
    • Acknowledge her reason for visit: "I can see from the notes that you are here and want to talk to me about your husband. Tell me how I can help you." (Video 3:47-3:54)
    • Wife expresses feeling low and overwhelmed with caregiving. (Video 3:54-4:00, 4:21-4:32)
    • Address Husband's Condition (briefly): (Video 4:20-4:40)
      • When was he diagnosed?
      • What does she understand about FTD?
      • How is he being managed? What support is he getting?
      • What does she expect from the medical team for him?
    • Address Wife's Well-being (Primary Focus): (Video 4:32-4:45 (talking about her))
      • Explore impact on her: "How has this impacted you?"
      • Depression Screening (Quick): Since she feels low. (Video 5:42-5:49)
        • Mood: Ask her to score her mood. (Video 5:49-5:55, 5:55-6:01)
        • Anhedonia: "Do you enjoy things you used to enjoy before?" (Video 5:55-5:55)
        • Eating and sleeping patterns. (Video 6:01-6:07)
        • Suicidal Ideation (Crucial): "Have you ever tried harming yourself?" (Can be phrased gently, e.g., "Sometimes when people are in this situation, they think of harming themselves..."). (Video 6:07-6:33)
      • Psychosocial History: Who else is in the family? Is she the sole carer? (Video 5:04-5:10, 5:10-5:17, 6:47-6:54)
      • Past Medical History & Lifestyle: Briefly. (Video 6:54-7:12)
    • Management & Support for the Wife: (Video 7:16)
      • Emotional Support: Counseling, support groups (meeting others in similar situations). (Video 7:39-8:08)
      • Practical Support:
        • Carer's assessment.
        • Respite care: Temporary care options to give her time to rest and recharge. (Video 8:08-8:32)
        • Financial aid and carer's allowances. (Video 8:38-8:45)
      • Education: Workshops, advice on managing dementia. (Video 8:52)
      • Local Services: Memory clinics, dementia cafes (similar to support groups). (Video 8:52-9:11)
      • Monitor her mood and coping; advise her to return if things get harder. (Video 9:05-9:17)
    • The station is primarily about supporting the wife (the carer), though understanding the husband's condition is context. (Video 9:44-9:57)

SCENARIO 2: COUNSELING PATIENT WORRIED ABOUT INHERITING HUNTINGTON'S DISEASE (Video 10:05)

  • Situation: In GP, a 27-year-old patient is worried about inheriting Huntington's Disease because her mother has been diagnosed. This is a genetic counseling type of station. (Video 10:09-10:32) (Similar approach to Sickle Cell/Cystic Fibrosis counseling).
  • Understanding Huntington's Disease (Doctor's Knowledge): (Video 10:42-11:08)
    • H: Hyperkinetic movements (e.g., chorea, tics). (Video 11:18-11:38)
    • U: Unsteady gait, clumsiness. (Video 11:44-11:51)
    • N: Neuropsychiatric symptoms (e.g., depression - ask about mood, anhedonia, eating, sleeping). (Video 11:56-12:09)
    • T: Triggers (e.g., stress can exacerbate symptoms). (Video 12:14-12:20)
    • I: Impaired cognition (e.g., memory loss). (Video 12:20-12:34)
    • N: Nystagmus or abnormal eye movements. (Video 12:34 (Nagmus -> Nystagmus)-12:40)
    • G: Genetic - Autosomal Dominant inheritance. (Video 12:40-12:53)
    • Age of Onset: Typically 30s-50s, but variable. Daughter may develop at a different age than mother. (Video 12:51-13:29)
    • Progression: Gets worse over time. Later symptoms: weight loss, dysphagia. (Video 13:30-13:41)
    • Cure/Treatment: No cure, but symptoms can be managed. (Video 13:48-13:54)
  • Counseling the Patient: (Video 13:52)
    • Acknowledge her worry: "I understand you're worried about inheriting Huntington's because your mother has it." (Video 14:14-14:21)
    • Assess Understanding: "What do you know about Huntington's disease?" (Video 15:56-15:58)
    • Information about Mother: Age of onset, current condition, how she's being looked after. (Video 16:02-16:18)
    • Assess Patient's Symptoms: Ask if she is experiencing any symptoms of Huntington's (coordination, memory, walking). (Video 16:18-16:24)
    • Family History & Planning: Siblings? Partner? Planning children? (Crucial for genetic counseling context). (Video 16:43-17:00, 1031s-1037s)
    • Explain Inheritance (Autosomal Dominant): (Video 14:21)
      • There is a 50% chance of inheriting the gene if one parent has it. (Video 18:38-18:42)
      • Draw a simple Punnett square or diagram to illustrate this 50% chance. (Video 14:40-15:11)
      • Explain that not everyone with the gene develops symptoms at the same age or in the same way as their affected parent. (Video 18:42-18:55)
    • Offer Genetic Testing & Counseling: (Video 18:00-18:13, 1130s-1136s)
      • Explain the process and implications of genetic testing.
      • Refer to genetic counseling services for detailed discussion and support. (Video 20:14)
    • Management of Huntington's (if asked about mother or if patient tests positive): Symptomatic - depression (CBT, antidepressants), mood stabilizers, managing daily tasks (ramps, stair lifts), eating/communication (high-calorie diet, speech devices, feeding tubes for dysphagia), physiotherapy. (Video 19:18-19:50) (This is secondary to her immediate concern about inheritance).
    • Options for Future Family Planning (if she has the gene): (Video 20:34)
      • Pre-implantation Genetic Diagnosis (PGD/PGT). (Video 20:41-12:46)
      • IVF, adoption. (Video 20:46-12:52)
    • Provide leaflets and resources for more information. (Video 20:20-20:26)

SCENARIO 3: GENERAL ANXIETY DISORDER (GAD) (Video 22:12)

  • Overlap with Depression: Many symptoms overlap (e.g., sleep problems, energy loss, concentration issues). (Video 22:20-22:33)
  • Key Features of GAD: (Video 22:38-22:47)
    • Persistent worry about something. (Video 22:47-22:54)
    • Anxiety, tension. (Video 23:00-23:07)
    • Physical symptoms: Headaches, muscle stiffness. (Video 23:07-23:14)
    • Concentration problems. (Video 23:14-23:20)
    • Hyperarousal/Irritability. (Video 23:25-23:43)
    • Lack of energy, restlessness. (Video 23:33-23:50)
    • Sleep problems. (Video 23:50-24:00)
    • Intense feeling of being afraid something bad will happen. (Video 24:00-24:05)
  • Distinguishing GAD from Depression: (Video 24:12)
    • Depression core: Sadness, anhedonia, helplessness. (Video 24:42-14:50)
    • Duration for diagnosis: GAD symptoms for at least a few months (e.g., 6 months). Depression diagnosis can be made after >2 weeks of low mood. (Video 24:50-25:17)
  • Patient Scenario (Medical Student with Anxiety): (Video 25:43)
    • Final year medical student feels anxious, gets tremors when doing procedures, lacks confidence, can't concentrate, fears making mistakes. (Video 25:48-26:23)
    • History Taking: Duration (few months/one year), impact on life/work, specific anxiety symptoms, physical symptoms (sleep, fatigue, headache, muscle stiffness), concentration, irritability. Rule out medical causes (thyroid, blood sugar). Rule out depression (mood, anhedonia, suicidal thoughts). Lifestyle and psychosocial history (support system). (Video 26:23-28:01)
  • Management of GAD (similar to depression initially): (Video 25:22)
    • Lifestyle changes: Exercise, relaxation, yoga, good diet, regular sleep, limit caffeine/alcohol. (Video 26:00 (lifestyle) then 1693s-1704s)
    • Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT). (Video 25:30)
    • Explore non-pharmacological options first. Avoid rushing to medication even if patient requests. (Video 28:24-28:42)
    • Antidepressants can be considered if needed later. (Video 25:30-25:37, 1722s-1729s)
  • If talking to a medical professional (student, colleague), medical terms can be used. (Video 29:23-29:42)

SCENARIO 4: HYPEREMESIS GRAVIDARUM (Video 29:47)

  • Situation: Patient (27 years old) presents with sickness and vomiting at 12 weeks pregnant. (Video 29:54-30:00)
  • History Taking:
    • Elaborate on sickness/vomiting (ODIPARA/SOCRATES). (Video 30:07-30:20, 1832s-1839s) (Morning sickness is common in pregnancy). (Video 30:20-30:32)
    • Rule out Dehydration: Thirst, urine color changes, dry mouth/lips. (Crucial with vomiting/diarrhea). (Video 30:54-31:06, 1861s-1866s)
    • Other symptoms: Abdominal pain, breast tenderness, headache, loose stools. (Video 31:14-31:35)
    • Rule out trauma/head injury. (Video 31:35-31:41) (Rule out differentials, but the diagnosis is usually clear).
    • Pregnancy History: Current pregnancy well-being, first pregnancy?, complications, antenatal visits. (Video 31:41-32:00, 1900s-1913s)
    • Past Medical History (e.g., thyroid problems), medications (any OTC for nausea?). (Video 32:00-1919s, 1919s-1945s)
    • Social support at home. (Video 32:45-1952s)
  • Examination & Investigations: Vitals, general physical exam, urine analysis (check for ketones if prolonged vomiting - examiner might give this finding). (Video 32:52-1958s, 1984s-1990s)
  • Diagnosis & Explanation: (Video 33:36)
    • "This vomiting is likely due to your pregnancy, a condition called Hyperemesis Gravidarum." (Video 33:36-2021s)
    • Reassure: Unlikely to harm the baby if treated effectively. Needs monitoring. (Video 33:54-34:00)
  • Management:
    • Consider admission if severe.
    • IV fluids if dehydrated. (Video 34:00-34:07, 2138s-2144s)
    • Antiemetic medications (e.g., "Spiroloxin" - likely a misspoken name, common antiemetics in pregnancy would be Cyclizine, Prochlorperazine, Ondansetron). Vitamin B6 can also be given. (Video 34:00-34:13, 2113s-2119s)
    • Address patient's main concern: "Is it going to affect my baby?" Answer: No, with treatment. (Video 34:19-20:31)
    • Steroids in severe, refractory cases (not initial plan). (Video 35:25-21:31)
    • Focus on initial management for the exam. Involve seniors. (Video 35:31-21:50)

SCENARIO 5: CARBIMAZOLE & AGRANULOCYTOSIS (Video 35:56)

  • Situation: Patient (40 y/o, gender varies in recall) on Carbimazole for 3 weeks for hyperthyroidism, presents with sore throat. (Video 36:04-22:14s) (Thyroid issues more common in females). (Video 36:45-22:53s)
  • Suspicion: Sore throat in a patient on Carbimazole is a red flag for Agranulocytosis. (Video 37:01-22:21s) (Agranulocytosis is not common but a serious side effect). (Video 36:16-22:28s)
  • History Taking:
    • Elaborate on sore throat (SOCRATES), fever. (Video 37:01-22:28s)
    • Confirm Carbimazole use: "Taking it regularly? As prescribed?" (Video 37:15-22:47s)
    • Ask about other Carbimazole side effects: Joint pain, facial swelling, skin problems, change in urine/stool color, abdominal pain (pancreatitis risk). (Video 37:27-22:59s)
    • Quick past personal history, LMP, pregnancy plans (Carbimazole is teratogenic; Propylthiouracil - PTU - preferred if pregnancy planned/first trimester). (Video 38:11-23:44s)
  • Examination & Investigations: Vitals, ENT exam, Thyroid Function Tests (TFTs), Full Blood Count (FBC - crucial for WBC/neutrophil count). (Video 38:50-23:02s) (Examiner gives high temperature). (Video 39:02-23:07s)
  • Explanation & Management: (Video 39:07)
    • "It looks like you might have a side effect of Carbimazole called agranulocytosis. Carbimazole can lower white blood cells, which fight infection, making you prone to infections." (Video 39:15-23:38s)
    • Immediate action: Discontinue Carbimazole. (Video 39:46-23:57s)
    • If in GP, send patient to hospital. (Video 39:57-24:02s)
    • Start broad-spectrum antibiotics. Supportive care. (Video 40:02-24:09s)
    • Monitor blood tests regularly. (Video 40:09)
    • Involve specialist (Endocrinologist). They may consider alternative medication (e.g., PTU if appropriate later, once agranulocytosis resolves). (Video 40:09-24:24s)

SCENARIO 6: LITHIUM TOXICITY (Video 40:43)

  • Situation: 70 y/o patient on Lithium for Bipolar Disorder, brought by son due to tremor and difficulty in coordination. (Video 40:50-25:03s)
  • Initial Approach: Focus on current symptoms first. (Video 41:55-25:20s)
    • Elaborate on tremors (SOCRATES, resting tremor? pin-rolling? duration, worsening?). (Video 42:06-25:56s) (Consider Parkinson's in elderly).
    • Elaborate on coordination problems/difficulty walking (dizziness, unsteadiness, falls, weakness). (Video 42:02-26:15s)
  • Medication History (Lithium): (Video 42:30)
    • Duration of Lithium use. (Video 42:30-26:38s)
    • Bipolar disorder status (mood, eating, sleeping). (Video 42:43-26:55s)
    • Compliance: "Taking it regularly? As prescribed?" (Important as patients may misunderstand). (Video 43:02-27:25s)
    • Symptoms of Lithium Toxicity: Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, excessive thirst, frequent urination, headache, blurred vision. (Video 43:33-27:45s)
  • Examination & Investigations: Quick past personal/social history. Neurological exam. Routine bloods, Lithium level. (Video 44:05-28:10s)
  • Management of Suspected Lithium Toxicity: (Video 44:10-28:26s)
    • Admit patient.
    • Discontinue Lithium immediately. (Video 44:32-28:39s)
    • IV fluids (if dehydrated from nausea/vomiting). (Video 44:39-28:45s)
    • If overdose: Gastric lavage. (Video 44:45-28:51s)
    • Severe cases: Hemodialysis. (Video 44:45-28:51s)
    • Regular blood test monitoring.
    • Involve Psychiatrist. (Video 44:51-28:58s)
  • Prevention of Future Lithium Toxicity (Patient Education if time allows): (Video 45:32)
    • Attend scheduled blood tests for Lithium level monitoring. (Video 45:38-29:45s)
    • Maintain consistent salt intake (gradual reduction if needed; low sodium can increase lithium levels). (Video 45:53-30:38s)
    • Stay hydrated, especially during exercise/hot weather. (Video 46:38-30:45s)
    • Limit alcohol (can cause dehydration). (Video 46:45-30:52s)
    • Inform other healthcare providers/pharmacists about Lithium use when starting new medications. (Video 46:52-31:06s)
