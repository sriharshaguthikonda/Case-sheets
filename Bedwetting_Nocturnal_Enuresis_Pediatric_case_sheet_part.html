• S: Site
  • O: Onset
    • Age when bedwetting started or became a concern.
    • Primary Enuresis: Child has never achieved consistent nighttime dryness for at least 6 months.
    • Secondary Enuresis: Child achieved at least 6 months of nighttime dryness and then started wetting again.
      • If secondary, ascertain age of achieving prior dryness and age of relapse.
      • Any specific triggers or events associated with the onset of secondary enuresis (e.g., stress, illness, new sibling, starting school)?
  • D: Duration
    • How long has the bedwetting been occurring (for current episode if secondary, or overall if primary)?
    • If secondary, how long was the period of dryness?
  • A: Aggravating / Relieving Factors
    • Aggravating Factors:
      • Increased fluid intake, especially in the evening/before bed? (Type of fluids - e.g.,
        • caffeinated,
        • sugary drinks)
      • Stressful events or periods?
        • school
        • parents fighting
        • Social media
        • Parental control

      • Constipation?
      • Deep sleep (child is difficult to arouse)?
        • too much play exhausted?
      • Cold weather?
      • Certain foods or drinks (less common, but some parents report associations)?
      • Illness (e.g., UTI, viral illness)?
      • Forgetting to use the toilet before bed?
    • Relieving Factors (or attempted interventions):
      • Fluid restriction in the evening?
      • Scheduled waking at night to void? (By whom? Successful?)
      • Use of bedwetting alarms? (Ever tried? Success rate?)
      • Medications? (e.g., Desmopressin (DDAVP), Imipramine - past or current use, effectiveness, side effects)
      • Positive reinforcement / reward charts?
      • Addressing constipation?
      • Avoiding known dietary triggers?
      • Child spontaneously wakes to void on some nights?
  • C: Character (of the wetting episode)
    • Volume of urine:
      • Small amount, just damp?
      • Large amount, soaking through bedding?
    • Timing of wetting:
      • Typically occurs
        • early in the night,
        • middle, or
        • towards morning?
      • Does the child wake up after wetting, or sleep through it?
    • Nature of stream (if observed during daytime voiding): Strong, weak, interrupted? (Relevant if daytime symptoms are also present).
    • Is the child aware of the sensation of needing to urinate before wetting at night?
    • Any straining or difficulty initiating urination (day or night)?
  • R: Radiation
    • Not applicable to bedwetting itself.
    • This element is not typically used for enuresis.
  • A: Associated Symptoms
    • Daytime Urinary Symptoms (Crucial to ask, as it may indicate underlying bladder dysfunction rather than simple nocturnal enuresis):
      • Daytime incontinence (wetting)?
      • Urgency (sudden, strong need to urinate)?
      • Frequency (urinating more often than usual)?
      • Hesitancy (difficulty starting urine stream)?
      • Straining to urinate?
      • Intermittent stream?
      • Dribbling or post-void dribbling?
      • Pain or burning during urination (dysuria) (suggests UTI)?
      • Holding maneuvers (e.g., squatting, leg crossing, pressing on perineum to prevent wetting)?
    • Bowel Symptoms:
      • Constipation (frequency of bowel movements, stool consistency, straining, pain)? (Very common co-morbidity)
      • Encopresis (fecal soiling)?
    • Sleep-Related Symptoms:
      • Snoring, observed apneas, restless sleep (suggests sleep-disordered breathing, which can contribute)?
      • Difficulty waking the child?
    • Neurological Symptoms:
      • Weakness or changes in gait?
      • Back pain, sacral dimple, hairy patch over the spine (suggests possible spinal cord abnormality like spina bifida occulta)?
      • Changes in bowel/bladder control that are new and sudden (beyond just wetting).
    • General/Systemic:
      • Increased thirst (polydipsia) and increased urination (polyuria) (suggests diabetes mellitus or diabetes insipidus)?
      • Fever, malaise (suggests infection)?
      • Poor growth, failure to thrive?
      • Edema (swelling)?
    • Psychological/Behavioral:
      • Recent stressors or changes in family/school environment?
      • Signs of anxiety, depression, or behavioral problems?
      • Child's emotional reaction to bedwetting (embarrassment, guilt, shame)?
  • P: Pattern (Frequency)
    • How many nights per week does bedwetting occur? (e.g., every night, 3-4 times/week, occasionally)
    • How many times per night does wetting occur? (Once, multiple times)
    • Is there any pattern to dry nights vs. wet nights?
  • P: Progression
    • Is the frequency/severity of bedwetting increasing, decreasing, or staying the same over time?
    • Any changes in associated symptoms?
  • E: Effect on life (Child and Family)
    • Child:
      • Emotional impact (shame, embarrassment, guilt, low self-esteem, anxiety)?
      • Impact on social activities (e.g., avoiding sleepovers, camps)?
      • Impact on sleep quality?
      • Child's perception of the problem? (Are they bothered by it?)
    • Family:
      • Parental stress, frustration, or anxiety?
      • Impact on family routines (e.g., laundry burden)?
      • Impact on siblings?
      • Any punitive measures used (which are generally discouraged)?
  • R:Red flags

  • S: Severity / Scale
    • Often judged by:
      • Frequency of wet nights per week.
      • Number of wetting episodes per night.
      • Volume of urine (small vs. soaking).
      • Impact on the child's and family's quality of life.
    • A specific numerical scale for bedwetting itself is not standard, but these factors contribute to assessing clinical significance.

Additional Contextual Information to Gather for Bedwetting:

  • Developmental History: Milestones achieved, any developmental delays.
  • Family History: History of enuresis in parents or siblings (strong genetic component).
  • Fluid Intake: Type and amount of fluids consumed throughout the day, especially in the evening.
  • Toileting Habits/Training: Age of toilet training, current daytime toileting routine, resistance to using toilet.
  • Sleep Patterns: Bedtime, typical sleep duration, depth of sleep, any sleep disturbances.
  • Psychosocial Environment: Major life changes, family stressors, school environment, peer relationships.
  • Past Medical History: Previous UTIs, kidney problems, neurological conditions, diabetes, sickle cell disease.
