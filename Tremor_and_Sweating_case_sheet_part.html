• S: Site & Laterality
    • Where exactly is the tremor? (e.g., Hands, fingers, head, jaw, legs)
    • Is it on one side (unilateral) or both (bilateral)?
    • If bilateral, did it start on one side and then spread?
  • O: Onset
    • Was the onset sudden (suggests vascular like hemorrhage/ischemia, trauma) or gradual (suggests neoplastic, degenerative)?
    • If sudden, was there any loss of consciousness, headache, or recent trauma?
  • D: Duration
    • How long have you had this condition (days, months, years)?
    • How long do individual episodes last?
  • A/R: Aggravating / Relieving Factors
    • Aggravated by: What makes it worse?
      • Specific situations - (e.g., Stress, anxiety,
      • fatigue,
      • holding a posture,
      • caffeine).
    • Relieved by: What makes it better? (e.g., Rest, sleep, voluntary movement of the limb, alcohol).
    • Is the tremor present when the limb is completely at rest (Resting Tremor)?
    • Does it appear or worsen when you move or reach for something (Action/Intention Tremor)?
    • Does it appear when holding a posture, like holding arms out (Postural Tremor)?
    • Can you describe the movement? (e.g., "pill-rolling" of the fingers).
  • C: Character / Type
    • fine
    • coarse
    • athetoid
  • R: Radiation / Spread
    • Has the tremor spread from its original location to other body parts over time?
  • A: Associated Symptoms
    • Thyroid
      • Swelling/pain in the neck
      • Pain
    • Parkinsonian: 
      • Slowness of movement (bradykinesia),
      •  stiffness (rigidity), 
      • balance problems or falls,
      • micrographia),
      • soft/monotone speech,
      •  reduced facial expression.
    • Cerebellar: 
      • Clumsiness, difficulty with rapid movements, 
      • unsteady "drunk-like" gait,
      • slurred speech (dysarthria),
      • abnormal eye movements (nystagmus).
    • Endocrine/Systemic:
      • Sweating,
      • palpitations, 
      • weight loss,
      •  heat intolerance,
      • anxiety,
      • changes in appetite.
    • Cortical (Stroke/Lesion):
      • Weakness,
      •  numbness, 
      • speech problems (aphasia),
      • confusion,
      • seizures,
      • incontinence.
  • P: Progression
    • How has it changed over time?
      • Very Rapid: Suggests hemorrhagic or inflammatory cause.
      • Rapidly progressing: Suggests malignancy.
      • Slowly progressing: Suggests benign cause.
      • Regressive (improving): Suggests vascular event (TIA) or inflammatory process.
      • Static (unchanging): Can be post-traumatic or congenital.
  • P: Pattern
    • continuous or
    • intermittent (comes and goes)?
      • Pheochromocytoma
    • Is there a pattern related to time of day or specific triggers?
  • E: Effect on Life
    • How does it impact daily tasks like writing, eating, dressing, or work?
    • Does it cause social embarrassment?
  • R: Red Flags
    • Sudden, severe "thunderclap" headache.
    • Associated fever and stiff neck.
    • Sudden onset with confusion, weakness, or speech loss.
    • Early, frequent falls.
    • New onset of tremor after age 50.
    • Rapid progression of symptoms.
  • S: Scale of Zero to 10
    • On a scale of 0-10, how severe is the tremor at its worst?
