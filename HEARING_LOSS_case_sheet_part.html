  2. ONSET (O):
    1. Did it start
      1. sudden (e.g., after loud noise exposure, trauma, viral illness) or
      2. gradual over time?
    2. Anything specific that you were doing or that happened when it started?
  3. DURATION (D):
    1. How long have you had the hearing loss?
    2. Is it constant or does it fluctuate? If fluctuating, describe the pattern.
  4. AGGRAVATING/RELIEVING FACTORS (A/R):
    1. Aggravating Factors: What makes the hearing loss seem worse?
      1. Noisy environments (difficulty discriminating speech)?
      2. Presence of a cold or sinus infection?
      3. Earwax buildup?
      4. Certain medications?
    2. Relieving Factors: Does anything improve your hearing?
      1. Turning the head to one side?
      2. Cleaning the ear (though generally not advised without professional guidance)?
      3. Hearing aids (if used, how effective are they)?
      4. Quieter environments?
  5. CHARACTER/TYPE (C):
    1. How would you describe the hearing loss?
      1. Difficulty hearing certain sounds (e.g., high-pitched like female/children's voices, low-pitched, speech in general)?
      2. Difficulty understanding speech, especially in noisy backgrounds or in group conversations?
      3. Does sound seem muffled, distorted, or unclear?
      4. Is it like a blockage in the ear or just a reduction in overall volume?
      5. Do you hear better in noisy environments than in quiet ones (paracusis Willisiana - suggests conductive loss)?
      6. Do you have difficulty hearing on the telephone?
  6. RADIATION (R): (Not typically applicable for hearing loss itself)
  7. ASSOCIATED SYMPTOMS (A):
    1. Ear pain (otalgia)?
    2. Tinnitus (ringing, buzzing, hissing in the ears)? Is it in the same ear(s) as the hearing loss?
    3. Dizziness or vertigo (sensation of spinning or unsteadiness)?
    4. Ear discharge (otorrhea)? (Color, consistency, odor, amount)
    5. Feeling of fullness or pressure in the ear?
    6. Headache?
    7. History of recurrent ear infections (otitis media)?
    8. History of exposure to loud noises (occupational, recreational, military)?
    9. Things you put in ear!
    10. Family history of hearing loss (and at what age)?
    11. Use of potentially ototoxic medications (e.g., certain antibiotics like gentamicin, aminoglycosides, diuretics like furosemide, chemotherapy agents, high-dose aspirin)?
    12. Recent head trauma or ear surgery?
    13. Neurological symptoms (e.g., facial weakness, numbness, visual changes)?
    14. Autoimmune conditions?
  8. PROGRESSION (P):
    1. Is the hearing loss getting progressively worse, better, or staying the same?
    2. If worsening, how quickly is it progressing?
    3. Has it changed in nature (e.g., started with high frequencies, now affects all)?
    4. Response to any previous treatments or interventions?
  9. PATTERN (P):
    1. Is it a continuous, stable loss, or does it fluctuate significantly from day to day or over longer periods?
  10. EFFECT ON LIFE (E):
    1. How does the hearing loss affect your daily communication (understanding conversations, phone calls)?
    2. Does it impact your social interactions, work performance, or safety (e.g., hearing alarms, traffic)?
    3. Do you find yourself asking people to repeat themselves often?
    4. Do you turn up the volume on the TV or radio louder than others prefer?
    5. Has it led to any feelings of social isolation, frustration, or anxiety?
    6. Impact on hobbies or enjoyment of music/sounds?
  11. RED FLAGS (R):
    1. Sudden onset sensorineural hearing loss (SSNHL) â€“ requires urgent assessment.
    2. Unilateral hearing loss of unknown cause (could indicate retrocochlear pathology like an acoustic neuroma).
    3. Hearing loss associated with neurological symptoms (e.g., facial weakness, vertigo, severe headache, visual changes, ataxia).
    4. Hearing loss following significant head trauma.
    5. Pulsatile tinnitus associated with unilateral hearing loss.
    6. Visible abnormality in the ear canal or eardrum on otoscopy (e.g., tumor, cholesteatoma, foreign body).
    7. Rapidly progressive hearing loss.
    8. Hearing loss in a child with speech and language delay.
  12. SCALE (S) (Severity/Impact):
    1. On a scale of 0-10, where 0 is normal hearing and 10 is complete deafness in that ear, how would you rate your hearing loss?
    2. (Alternatively, patient's subjective assessment: "How much of a problem is your hearing loss for you on a scale of 0-10?")
