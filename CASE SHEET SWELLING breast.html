    Patient-Specific Plan for Breast Swelling/Lump

  • Initial Investigations (Triple Assessment is key for discrete breast lumps)
    • Clinical Examination: (Already performed as detailed previously).
    • Blood work (If infection, inflammation, or malignancy suspected, or for pre-operative assessment):
      • Full Blood Count (FBC) with differential.
      • C-Reactive Protein (CRP) / Erythrocyte Sedimentation Rate (ESR).
      • Liver Function Tests (LFTs), Bone Profile (Calcium, ALP) (if metastases suspected or pre-op).
      • Coagulation screen (if surgery or biopsy planned).
      • Tumour markers (e.g., CA 15-3, CEA - more for monitoring than diagnosis, but may be baseline).
    • Imaging:
      • Mammography: Especially for women >35-40 years (age threshold can vary by local guidelines), or younger if high clinical suspicion or strong family history. Provides views to detect masses, calcifications, architectural distortion.
      • Breast Ultrasound: Useful for all ages. Differentiates cystic from solid lesions. Characterizes solid lesions. Guides biopsies. Often first-line imaging in younger women (<35-40) due to breast density.
      • MRI Breast: For specific indications e.g., screening high-risk women (BRCA carriers), assessing extent of known cancer (especially lobular), evaluating implant integrity, problem-solving when mammography/ultrasound are inconclusive, monitoring response to neoadjuvant chemotherapy.
    • Pathology (Biopsy - image-guided if lump is non-palpable or for precise targeting):
      • Fine Needle Aspiration Cytology (FNAC): Provides cells for cytological assessment. Can diagnose malignancy, assess cystic lesions. Less invasive but may be inconclusive.
      • Core Needle Biopsy (CNB): Preferred method for solid lesions. Provides tissue for histological diagnosis, grading, and receptor status (ER, PR, HER2) in cancer, which is crucial for treatment planning.
      • Vacuum-Assisted Biopsy (VAB): Can obtain larger tissue samples, useful for microcalcifications or small lesions.
      • Excision Biopsy (Surgical Biopsy): May be diagnostic and therapeutic for some benign lesions or if needle biopsies are inconclusive.
  • Patient Communication
    • ICE (Ideas, Concerns, Expectations): Discuss patient's understanding, fears (especially cancer anxiety), and desired outcomes. Address any myths or misconceptions.
    • Diagnosis disclosure: Clearly explain the findings from the triple assessment. If benign, provide reassurance. If malignant, deliver news compassionately, ensure support (e.g., presence of relative, breast care nurse).
    • Explanation of findings: Use simple language, diagrams if helpful. Explain the implications of the diagnosis.
  • Further Investigations (If malignancy confirmed/suspected, for staging)
    • Specialized tests:
      • Genomic assays on tumour tissue (e.g., Oncotype DX, MammaPrint) for certain ER+ early-stage cancers to assess risk of recurrence and benefit from chemotherapy.
      • BRCA1/BRCA2 genetic testing (if strong family history or specific criteria met, as per NICE CG164).
    • Imaging for Staging (if invasive cancer confirmed):
      • Chest X-ray (or CT Chest).
      • CT Abdomen/Pelvis.
      • Bone Scan.
      • PET-CT (in selected cases, e.g., inflammatory breast cancer, locally advanced disease, or to clarify equivocal findings on other scans).
    • Referrals:
      • Urgent Referral to Breast Clinic (2 Week Wait - 2WW pathway): For any patient with suspected breast cancer (e.g., discrete hard lump, skin changes, new nipple retraction, bloody discharge, unexplained axillary lump).
      • Multidisciplinary Team (MDT) review for all confirmed cancer cases.
      • Referral to Clinical Genetics (if indicated for familial risk assessment).
      • Referral to Oncology (Medical, Radiation), Plastic Surgery (for reconstruction options).
  • Treatment Plan (Based on Triple Assessment and MDT discussion if malignant)
    • Symptomatic treatment:
      • Analgesia (Paracetamol, NSAIDs) for breast pain (mastalgia).
      • Supportive bra.
      • Warm compresses for mastitis.
    • Specific treatment (depends on the cause):
      • Fibroadenoma:
        • Non-Medical: Observation and reassurance if small (<3cm), asymptomatic, and diagnosis is confident. Regular self-examination.
        • Surgical: Excision if large, symptomatic (painful), growing, patient preference, or if diagnosis uncertain after triple assessment. Vacuum-assisted excision may be an option for smaller fibroadenomas.
      • Breast Cyst:
        • Non-Medical: Reassurance if simple cyst and asymptomatic.
        • Procedure: Aspiration (often ultrasound-guided) if symptomatic (painful, large) or to confirm diagnosis (fluid typically straw-coloured or greenish; send for cytology if blood-stained or residual mass). If collapses and symptoms resolve, no further action.
        • Surgical: Excision if recurrent symptomatic cysts, blood-stained aspirate with atypical cells, or if a solid component is present.
      • Mastitis/Breast Abscess:
        • Medical: Antibiotics (e.g., Flucloxacillin, Co-amoxiclav, Clarithromycin if penicillin-allergic). Analgesia.
        • Non-Medical: Advice on continued breastfeeding or breast pumping if lactational to ensure milk drainage. Warm compresses. Adequate fluid intake.
        • Procedure (for Abscess): Ultrasound-guided aspiration or surgical incision & drainage.
      • Fibrocystic Changes/Fibroadenosis:
        • Non-Medical: Reassurance, well-fitting supportive bra. Dietary modifications (e.g., reducing caffeine - evidence is limited but some women find it helpful).
        • Medical: Analgesia for cyclical mastalgia. Evening primrose oil (evidence limited, but may be tried). Danazol or Tamoxifen for severe, persistent mastalgia (specialist use only due to side effects).
      • Duct Ectasia/Periductal Mastitis:
        • Non-Medical: Reassurance. Smoking cessation (strong association). Warm compresses for symptoms. Nipple hygiene.
        • Medical: Antibiotics if acute inflammation/infection. Analgesia.
        • Surgical (if persistent troublesome discharge, recurrent infection, or fistula): Excision of the affected major duct(s) (Hadfieldâ€™s operation) or total duct excision.
      • Intraductal Papilloma:
        • Surgical: Microdochectomy (excision of the single duct containing the papilloma) or major duct excision if multiple papillomas or atypia.
      • Breast Cancer (MDT-led complex treatment):
        • Surgery:
          • Breast Conserving Surgery (BCS - lumpectomy/wide local excision) followed by Radiotherapy.
          • Mastectomy (simple, modified radical).
          • Axillary Surgery: Sentinel Lymph Node Biopsy (SLNB) if clinically node-negative; Axillary Lymph Node Dissection (ALND) if SLNB positive or clinically node-positive.
          • Breast Reconstruction options (immediate or delayed).
        • Radiotherapy: Adjuvant after BCS, post-mastectomy in certain high-risk cases, palliative for metastases.
        • Chemotherapy: Neoadjuvant (before surgery to shrink tumour), Adjuvant (after surgery to reduce recurrence risk), Palliative (for metastatic disease).
        • Endocrine (Hormone) Therapy: For ER/PR positive cancers (e.g., Tamoxifen, Aromatase Inhibitors).
        • Targeted Therapy: For HER2 positive cancers (e.g., Trastuzumab (Herceptin), Pertuzumab).
    • Admission required: Yes (for most surgical procedures, acute abscess drainage, complications of cancer treatment) / No (for outpatient management of simple cysts, fibroadenomas under observation, uncomplicated mastitis).
  • Follow Up
    • Frequency: Depends on diagnosis. Benign conditions may not need routine follow-up unless symptomatic. Post-cancer treatment involves regular surveillance (clinical and imaging) as per NICE guidelines.
    • Specialist referrals: Breast Surgeon, Oncologist (Medical/Radiation), Breast Care Nurse, Clinical Geneticist, Psychologist, Physiotherapist, Lymphedema specialist as needed.
    • Allied health:
      • Breast Care Nurse Specialist: Essential for support, information, and coordination of care, especially in cancer.
      • Physiotherapist: For post-operative shoulder mobility, lymphedema prevention/management.
      • Psychological support / Counselling.
      • Lymphedema services.
  • Patient Education
    • Warning Signs/Red Flags: Any new lumps, changes in existing lumps, skin changes (dimpling, puckering, redness, rash), nipple changes (retraction, discharge especially bloody), persistent pain, unexplained swelling in axilla or arm.
    • Breast awareness advice / Breast self-examination (BSE) technique (though routine BSE promotion has varied, awareness of what is normal for them is key).
    • Information about national breast screening programs (e.g., NHS Breast Screening Programme).
    • Educational materials provided: Leaflets/pamphlets from reliable sources (e.g., Breast Cancer Now, Macmillan Cancer Support, NHS).
    • Useful resources/links.
    • Information on risk factors and risk reduction strategies if appropriate.
