URINE DIPSTICK ANALYSIS: TEACHING GUIDE

A. SCENARIO INTRODUCTION & INITIAL INTERACTION (Video 0:21)

  • Scenario: You (FY2 doctor) are asked by a nursing staff member (Sarah) to teach her how to perform a urine analysis (dipstick test). (Video 0:24-0:40)
  • Teaching Station Format: Typically involves teaching, assessing the learner's understanding, and providing feedback, but often without letting the learner perform the full procedure. (Video 0:40-0:55)
  • Initial Steps:
    • Build rapport with Sarah. (Video 0:55-0:57)
    • Assess her existing knowledge and what specifically she wants to learn about urine dipstick testing. (Video 0:57-1:01)
    • Briefly explain what a urine dipstick test is used for. (Video 1:01-1:07)

B. URINE SAMPLE COLLECTION & IDENTIFICATION (Video 1:10)

  • Type of Sample Needed: Midstream Urine (MSU) Sample. (Video 1:14-1:22)
    • Explain to Sarah (how she would instruct a patient):
      • Provide the patient with a sterile container. (Video 1:25-1:28)
      • Instruct the patient to start urinating into the toilet, pass a small amount of urine first. (Video 1:28-1:32)
      • Then, collect the middle part of the urine stream into the container. (Video 1:32-1:37)
      • The rest of the urine can be passed into the toilet. (Video 1:37-1:38)
    • Important Note: The urine must be collected directly into the provided sterile container. Do not collect in another container and transfer, as this can affect results, especially for infection. (Video 1:41-1:57)
  • Patient Identification: Crucial to ensure the sample belongs to the correct patient. (Video 2:07-2:15)
    • Check the sample bottle for correct details: Name, Date of Birth, Hospital Identification Number. (Video 2:41-2:47)
    • Proper labeling is vital to avoid mix-ups. (Video 2:15-2:22)

C. INITIAL VISUAL ASSESSMENT OF URINE (INSPECTION) (Video 2:29)

Before dipstick testing, visually inspect the urine for three characteristics: (Video 2:50-2:53)

  • 1. Color: (Video 2:55-2:58)
    • Normal: Straw-colored (indicates good hydration). (Video 2:58-3:06)
    • Dark/Concentrated: May indicate dehydration. (Video 3:09-3:14)
    • Red: Hematuria (blood in urine), could be due to kidney or bladder problems. (Video 3:14-3:20) (Can also be caused by beetroot consumption - Video 3:27-3:30)
    • Orange: May indicate jaundice or certain medications. (Video 3:22-3:24)
    • Brown: Could be due to certain medications. (Video 3:33-3:37)
    • приятели
  • 2. Clarity: (Video 3:45-3:47)
    • Normal: Clear. (Video 3:47-3:49)
    • Cloudy: May indicate UTI or kidney issues. (Video 3:51-4:01)
    • Frothy: May indicate proteinuria. (Video 3:59-4:03)
  • 3. Smell (Odor): (Video 4:08-4:11)
    • Assess from a distance, do not sniff directly. (Video 4:11-4:13)
    • Offensive/Foul: May indicate UTI. (Video 4:15-4:17)
    • Sweet: May indicate glucosuria (e.g., in diabetes). (Video 4:17-4:22)

This initial visual assessment phase can take a few minutes. (Video 4:37-4:42)

D. URINE DIPSTICK TEST PROCEDURE (Video 4:42)

  • Dipstick Bottle & Strip:
    • Check the expiry date on the dipstick bottle. (Video 4:51-4:57)
    • Open the bottle and take out one dipstick. Close the bottle immediately. (Video 4:57-5:02)
  • Dipping the Strip: (Video 5:02)
    • Immerse the dipstick fully into the urine sample, ensuring all reagent squares are covered. (Video 5:05-5:13)
    • Dip for a few seconds. (Video 5:15-5:18)
    • Remove the strip and tap off excess urine against the side of the container or blot on a tissue paper (to prevent reagent run-off). (Video 5:22-5:26)
  • Waiting & Reading Time: (Video 5:26-5:28)
    • The waiting time before reading is specified on the dipstick bottle. (Video 5:28-5:33)
    • Generally, start reading most parameters (e.g., glucose, bilirubin, ketones, specific gravity) after 60 seconds. (Video 5:36-5:47)
    • Leukocytes typically require a longer waiting time: 90 to 120 seconds in total. (Video 5:50-6:00)
    • If no clock is available, estimate the time. By the time you read from glucose up to leukocytes, sufficient time usually passes for the leukocyte reading. (Video 6:10-6:44)
    • Always refer to the instructions on the specific dipstick bottle being used. (Video 6:48-6:57)
  • Comparing to Chart: Compare the color changes on the dipstick squares to the reference chart on the bottle.

E. SUMMARY OF KEY STEPS (Video 6:59)

  1. Rapport building.
  2. Confirm patient identity.
  3. Ensure Midstream Urine (MSU) sample.
  4. Visual assessment: Color, Clarity, Smell.
  5. Check dipstick bottle expiry.
  6. Dip the strip, remove excess.
  7. Wait for the specified time (e.g., 60 seconds for most, 90-120 seconds for leukocytes).
  8. Compare colors to the chart on the bottle and record results.

This station is considered relatively straightforward if the steps are followed systematically. (Video 6:57-7:01)

URINE DIPSTICK TEST: PRACTICAL PROCEDURE GUIDE

A. SAMPLE COLLECTION & PATIENT IDENTIFICATION (Video 0:13)

  • Sample Type: Mid-stream urine (MSU) is required. (Video 0:13-0:17)
    • Instruct the patient to pass a few drops of urine first, then collect the subsequent stream. (Video 0:17-0:21)
    • The sample must be collected directly into the provided sterile container; do not transfer from another container. (Video 0:23-0:29)
  • Patient Identification: Before testing, verify the patient's identity on the sample container (Name, Date of Birth, etc.). This is crucial. (Video 0:29-0:42)

B. INITIAL VISUAL INSPECTION OF URINE (Video 0:45)

  • 1. Color: (Video 0:45-0:48)
    • Normal: Straw color. (Video 0:48-0:51)
    • Dark straw: May indicate dehydration. (Video 0:51-0:54)
    • Blood/Red: Hematuria (causes can include kidney problems, bladder issues, stones). (Video 0:54-1:04)
    • Orange: Could be due to medications like Rifampicin. (Video 1:04-1:08)
  • 2. Clarity: (Video 1:11-1:14)
    • Normal: Clear. (Video 1:14-1:16)
    • Cloudy: May suggest a Urinary Tract Infection (UTI). (Video 1:16-1:24)
  • 3. Smell (Odor): (Video 1:26-1:29)
    • Assess from a distance after opening the container. (Video 1:29-1:34)
    • Foul-smelling: May indicate UTI. (Video 1:34-1:40)
    • Fruity smell: Could suggest Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA). (Video 1:40-1:47)

C. URINE DIPSTICK TEST PROCEDURE (Video 1:50)

  • Check Dipstick Bottle: Verify the expiry date on the dipstick bottle. (Video 1:54-2:00)
  • Dipstick Parameters (Examples): (Video 2:02-2:18)
    • Glucose (Diabetes, DKA). (Video 2:08, 2:20-2:24)
    • Ketones (DKA). (Video 2:10, 2:24-2:27)
    • Specific Gravity (Density - changes with dehydration). (Video 2:11, 2:27-2:31)
    • Blood (Kidney problems like glomerulonephritis, stones). (Video 2:14, 2:34-2:40)
    • pH (Acidic or alkaline urine). (Video 2:20, 2:40-2:47)
    • Protein (Preeclampsia, kidney problems). (Video 2:27, 2:47-2:59)
    • Nitrites & Leukocytes (UTI). (Video 2:38, 2:59-3:06)
  • Performing the Test: (Video 3:06)
    1. Open the urine sample container. (Video 3:10-3:13)
    2. Remove one dipstick from its bottle and close the bottle. (Video 3:13-3:19)
    3. Fully immerse all reagent squares of the dipstick into the urine sample for a few seconds. (Video 3:24-3:31)
    4. Remove the dipstick and tap off excess urine against the side of the container or blot it on a paper towel. (Video 3:31-3:38)
  • Reading the Results: (Video 3:42)
    • Waiting times are specified on the dipstick bottle. (Video 3:43-3:50)
    • Typically, read most parameters at exactly 60 seconds. (Video 3:50-3:54)
    • Leukocytes are read later, typically at 90 to 120 seconds. (Video 3:50-3:54)
    • If no watch/clock is available, by the time you read from glucose (bottom of strip) upwards to leukocytes, enough time (extra 30-60 seconds) would have passed for an accurate leukocyte reading. (Video 4:01-4:14, 4:32-4:43)
    • Hold the dipstick horizontally next to the color chart on the bottle to compare and read results. (Video 4:06-4:16) Start reading from the bottom (e.g., glucose) upwards. (Video 4:14-4:18)

D. POST-TEST ACTIONS (Video 4:43)

  • Discard the used dipstick strip appropriately. (Video 4:43-4:48)
  • Dispose of gloves and any other waste (e.g., if wearing a gown). (Video 4:48-4:51)
  • Thank the patient/colleague. (Video 4:51)
