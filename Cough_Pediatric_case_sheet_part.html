• S: Site (Perceived Origin)
    • Wet vs. Dry (see Character) can also give clues to the site/level of airway involvement.
    • Does the cough feel like it's coming from the throat, chest, or elsewhere?
      • tickling sensation in the throat (suggesting upper airway)?
      • Is it a deep cough felt in the chest (suggesting lower airway)?
  • O: Onset
    • sudden
    • gradual?
    • Categorization by duration (as per your C-MINT related points):
      • Acute: < 3 weeks
      • Subacute: 3-8 weeks
      • Chronic: > 8 weeks
      • In children, chronic cough is often defined as > 4 weeks.
  • D: Duration
    • How long has this particular episode of coughing been present?
    • If recurrent, how long do episodes typically last? How frequent are the episodes?
  • A: Aggravating / Relieving Factors (incorporating your C-MINT related A/R)
    • Aggravating Factors:
      • Positional changes? (e.g., worse when lying flat - suggestive of post-nasal drip, GERD, heart failure)
      • Time of day (Diurnal Variations)?
        • Worse at night/early morning? (Asthma, post-nasal drip, GERD)
        • Nocturnal cough is a key indicator for asthma in children.
      • Seasonal variations? (Allergic rhinitis, asthma triggered by seasonal allergens)
      • Exposure to irritants (smoke, dust, fumes, strong odors)?
      • Cold air?
      • Exercise or exertion? (Exercise-induced asthma/bronchoconstriction)
      • Talking or laughing?
      • Eating or drinking? (Aspiration, GERD, tracheo-esophageal fistula in infants)
    • Relieving Factors:
      • Specific positions? (e.g., sitting up)
      • Medications (e.g., inhalers, cough suppressants, antacids)?
      • Warm drinks, honey (for symptomatic relief)?
      • Steam inhalation?
      • Avoiding known triggers?
  • C: Character / Nature / Type (incorporating your C-MINT related points 1 & 5)
    • Dry
      • Dry Cough (Non-productive):
      • Often irritating, tickling.
      • Common in viral infections, asthma, GERD, ACE inhibitor use, interstitial lung disease, early pneumonia.
      • Habit cough, psychogenic cough.
      Productive
      • Amount: Scant, moderate, copious?
      • Color: Clear, white (mucoid - viral, chronic bronchitis), yellow, green (purulent - bacterial infection), rusty (pneumococcal pneumonia), pink frothy (pulmonary edema)?
        Green/yellow sputum doesn't always mean bacterial infection in children, can be seen with viral too.
      • Consistency: Thin, thick, tenacious?
      • Odor: Foul-smelling (anaerobic infection, bronchiectasis, lung abscess)?
      • Blood (Hemoptysis): Streaks of blood, frank blood? (Needs urgent evaluation - TB, malignancy, bronchiectasis, PE, severe infection).
      • Common in bronchitis, pneumonia, bronchiectasis, COPD exacerbation, cystic fibrosis.
    • Sound/Quality of Cough:
      • Barking (like a seal): Croup (laryngotracheobronchitis).
      • Paroxysmal (fits of coughing): Pertussis (whooping cough), asthma, aspiration.
        • Associated inspiratory "whoop" in pertussis. Post-tussive emesis common in pertussis.
      • Hacking: Often seen with smoking or chronic irritation.
      • Wheezy cough: Asthma, bronchiolitis.
      • Brassy: Tracheal compression.
      • Bovine (non-explosive, weak): Vocal cord paralysis, neuromuscular weakness.
  • R: Radiation
  • A: Associated Symptoms
    • Fever, chills (infection).
    • Shortness of breath (dyspnea) - at rest or on exertion (pneumonia, asthma, COPD, heart failure, PE).
    • Wheezing (asthma, COPD, bronchiolitis).
    • Chest pain or tightness (pneumonia, pleurisy, bronchitis, PE, cardiac causes, GERD).
      • Is the pain pleuritic (worse on inspiration or coughing)?
    • Sore throat, runny nose, nasal congestion (upper respiratory tract infection, post-nasal drip).
    • Hoarseness or voice changes (laryngitis, GERD, vocal cord issues, tumor).
    • Heartburn, acid reflux sensation (GERD).
    • Fatigue, malaise.
    • Body aches (myalgia), joint pain (arthralgia) (viral infections).
    • Weight loss, night sweats (TB, malignancy, chronic infection).
    • Hemoptysis (coughing up blood - see Character).

    • Poor feeding or decreased appetite in infants/children.
    • Lethargy, irritability in children.
    • Cyanosis (bluish discoloration of lips/skin) - serious sign.
    • Respiratory distress signs in children: nasal flaring, grunting, intercostal/subcostal retractions, tachypnea.
    • Apneic spells in infants (can be associated with pertussis or severe RSV).
    • Stridor (high-pitched inspiratory noise - croup, foreign body, laryngomalacia).
  • P: Pattern (Temporal Relationship - overlaps with Aggravating/Relieving)
    • Continous
    • Intermittent
      • frequency
  • P: Progression
    • Is the cough getting better, worse, or staying the same over its duration?
    • Has the character of the cough changed (e.g., from dry to productive)?
    • Have new symptoms developed alongside the cough?
  • E: Effect on life
    • Impact on sleep (patient's and family members').
    • Effect on daily activities, ability to work or attend school.
    • Limitation of physical activity or exercise tolerance.
    • Social embarrassment or avoidance.
    • Impact on feeding, growth, and development in children.
    • Effect on play and interaction with peers.
    • Stress or anxiety related to the cough.
  • S: Severity / Scale
    • Subjective rating by patient (e.g., on a scale of 0-10, how bothersome is it?).
    • Frequency of coughing fits (e.g., number of times per hour/day).
    • Does it interrupt conversation or activities?
    • Does it cause syncope (cough syncope)?
    • Does it induce vomiting (post-tussive emesis)?
    • Parental assessment of severity, impact on child's well-being.

Additional Contextual Information to Gather for Cough:

  • Smoking history (active or passive exposure).
    Exposure to secondhand smoke is a major risk factor for respiratory illnesses in children.
  • Occupational or environmental exposures (dust, chemicals, allergens).
  • Past medical history (asthma, COPD, allergies, GERD, heart disease, previous pneumonia, TB exposure).
    History of prematurity, congenital lung/heart conditions, immunodeficiency, atopy.
  • Family history (asthma, allergies, cystic fibrosis, alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency, TB).
  • Medication history (especially ACE inhibitors, beta-blockers).
  • Vaccination status (Influenza, Pneumococcus, Pertussis, Measles).
  • Possibility of foreign body aspiration (sudden onset of choking and coughing, especially in toddlers).
  • Daycare attendance (increased exposure to infections).
