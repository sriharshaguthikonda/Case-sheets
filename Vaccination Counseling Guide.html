VACCINATION COUNSELING & HANDLING REFUSAL

A. SCENARIO 1: MOTHER'S CONCERNS ABOUT 8-WEEK VACCINATIONS (Video 0:26)

  • Situation: You are in a GP setting. A mother of an 8-week-old boy wants to know about the vaccines her baby can receive and has concerns. (Video 0:30-0:50)
    • Age variation: Baby might be 6 weeks and due in 2 weeks, or already 8 weeks. (Video 0:54-1:08)
  • Resource: A vaccination chart will be provided in the cubicle. Use it to explain. (Video 2:04-2:22)
  • Vaccines at 8 Weeks: (Video 2:22)
    1. 6-in-1 Vaccine: (Video 2:28)
      • Protects against: Diphtheria, Tetanus, Pertussis (Whooping Cough), Polio, Haemophilus influenzae type b (Hib), and Hepatitis B. (Video 2:30-2:44)
      • Site: Given in the thigh(s). (Video 2:47-2:52)
      • Schedule: Given at 8, 12, and 16 weeks. (Video 4:12-4:15)
      • Type: Killed vaccine (does not contain live organisms), so no risk of getting the diseases from the vaccine. Produces good immunity. (Video 4:18-4:36)
    2. Meningococcal Group B (MenB) Vaccine: (Video 2:56-2:58)
      • Site: Given in the left thigh. (Video 3:00-3:05)
    3. Rotavirus Vaccine: (Video 3:05-3:07)
      • Administration: Oral (drops by mouth). (Video 3:07-3:11)
  • Addressing Mother's Concerns: (Video 3:29)
    • Common concerns: Which vaccines, injection sites. (Video 3:31-3:44)
    • Provide the vaccination chart to the mother for her reference. (Video 3:44-3:51)
  • Side Effects of Vaccines: (Video 4:42-4:44)
    • General/Common: Pain, redness, swelling at the injection site, fever. (Video 4:50-4:56, 5:44-5:49)
      • MenB vaccine can sometimes cause high-grade fever. Advise paracetamol prophylactically or if fever develops (e.g., then and there, and every 4 hours). (Video 4:58-5:13, 5:33-5:38)
    • Other common but usually mild: Sickness, irritability, loss of appetite for a day or two. (Video 5:16-5:19)
    • Rare/Uncommon: Fits (febrile convulsions due to high fever, which can be prevented by managing fever), anaphylaxis. Explain these are rare. (Video 5:19-5:30, 5:52-5:55)
    • Focus on common side effects when explaining, not extreme rare ones initially. (Video 5:40-5:42)
    • Rotavirus specific side effects: Diarrhea (common). (Video 6:20-6:29, 6:57-6:59) Rarely, intussusception (do not usually explain rarest risks). (Video 6:40-6:54)
    • MenB specific side effects: Fever, unusual crying, irritability, diarrhea, vomiting. Advise keeping child hydrated. Symptoms usually resolve in a day or two. (Video 7:02-7:26)
  • Contraindications/Precautions for 6-in-1 Vaccine: (Video 5:55)
    • Allergy to previous vaccination. (Video 5:57-6:00)
    • High temperature at the time of vaccination (can delay). (Video 6:00-6:03)
    • Neurological problems like poorly controlled epilepsy (needs specialist review before vaccinating). (Video 6:03-6:17)
  • Identifying Allergies in Infants: Difficult at 8 weeks.
    FAMILY H/O:
    (e.g., maternal egg allergy) might raise caution as some vaccines have egg derivatives. (Video 7:33-7:58)

B. SCENARIO 2: TEACHING A STUDENT HOW TO APPROACH VACCINATION REFUSAL (Video 1:15)

  • Student's Goal: Learn how to approach a parent refusing vaccination for an 8-week-old. (Video 1:56-2:00)
  • Approach to Parents Refusing Vaccination: (Video 8:20)
    1. Active Listening: Listen carefully to the parent's reasons and concerns. (Video 8:22-8:30)
    2. Acknowledge Emotions & Validate Concerns: Understand their perspective without getting frustrated. "I can see you don't want your child to get the vaccination." (Video 8:28-8:38, 9:29-9:33, 9:38-9:43)
    3. Non-Judgmental Manner: Communicate respectfully. (Video 8:41-8:47)
    4. Explore Reasons for Refusal: Try to find out why they are refusing. Parents usually act in what they believe is the best interest of their child. (Video 8:48-9:08)
      • They might have misinformation or specific fears (e.g., side effects like fits, anaphylaxis). (Video 10:06-10:17)
    5. Address Misinformation with Evidence: (Video 10:28)
      • Provide clear, evidence-based information. Explain the actual risks vs. benefits. (Video 10:30-10:41)
      • Clarify common myths (e.g., MMR and autism). Ask where they got their information. (Video 10:47-11:10)
      • Cite reliable sources: WHO, NICE, NHS guidelines, and offer to refer them to these resources. (Video 11:22-11:37)
      • State clearly: e.g., "No relationship between MMR and autism/enterocolitis based on current data." (Video 11:37-11:47)
    6. Respect Autonomy: Parents have the right to refuse vaccination. Respect their decision, even if due to religious beliefs. (Video 12:35-12:46)
    7. Explain Risks of Not Vaccinating: Gently explain the potential consequences of not vaccinating (e.g., the child getting preventable diseases like pertussis, which can be life-threatening). (Video 12:57-13:26)
    8. Involve Senior Doctors: If unable to convince the parents or address their concerns adequately, do not hesitate to involve a senior colleague. (Video 13:34-13:49)

Remember, the vaccination chart is a key tool in the exam for these scenarios. (Video 14:01-14:13)
