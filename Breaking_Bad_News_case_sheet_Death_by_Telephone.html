BREAKING BAD NEWS: DEATH OF A CHILD (SPIKES PROTOCOL)

S: Setting Up the Interview

  1. PREPARATION & ENVIRONMENT
    • Review all patient notes and clinical events.
    • Find a private, quiet room with no interruptions. (1:01)
    • Have tissues and water available. (2:04)
  2. INTRODUCTION "Hello, my name is Dr. Smith. I'm one of the doctors who was looking after Daniel."
    • Introduce yourself and anyone else present. (1:46)
    • Confirm the identities of the family members. (1:52)
    • Sit down and ensure a comfortable, non-confrontational arrangement. (2:01)

P: Perception (Assess what they know)

  1. EXPLORE THEIR UNDERSTANDING "Before we go any further, could you tell me what you understand about what's happened so far?" (2:12)
    • Also ask: "What have the team already told you about what happened?" to identify information gaps.
    • Listen carefully to their response to gauge their understanding of the timeline and severity. (2:18)
  2. GATHER KEY CONTEXT
    • Timeline of deterioration: "Can you walk me through what you saw from when he was admitted to when he seemed to get worse?"
    • Premorbid health & frailty: "Before this illness, what was he like day-to-day? Was he managing on his own?"
    • Relevant history: "Did he have any ongoing health problems? Was he a smoker?"
    • Previous discussions: "Had you ever discussed his wishes for a situation like this? Did the team talk to you about treatments like the breathing machine or resuscitation?"
  3. SUMMARISE THEIR PERCEPTION "So from what you've said, he became breathless on Monday, and the nurses told you the infection was getting worse. Is that right?"

I: Invitation (Ask to give information)

  1. SEEK PERMISSION "Is it okay for me to explain what happened from a medical perspective?" (2:38)

K: Knowledge (Give the news)

  1. WARNING SHOT "I'm very sorry, but the news is not good." (2:43)
  2. DELIVER THE NEWS CLEARLY "Despite our best efforts, Daniel has passed away. I am so sorry." (2:46)
    • Use clear, simple, and unambiguous language (e.g., "died" or "passed away").

E: Emotions & Empathy

  1. PAUSE AND OBSERVE
    • Stop talking and allow for silence. This is crucial. (2:50)
  2. IDENTIFY & ACKNOWLEDGE THE EMOTION "I can see this is a huge shock." (3:04)

S: Strategy and Summary

  1. SUMMARISE AND EXPLAIN EVENTS "I know this is a lot to take in. Would it be helpful if I explained the events that led to this?" (3:20)
    • Provide a brief, clear, jargon-free summary of the clinical deterioration. (3:30)
  2. PLAN IMMEDIATE NEXT STEPS
    • Offer them time alone. (4:18)
    • Ask if they would like to see their loved one and prepare them for what to expect. (4:24)
  3. OFFER SUPPORT & PRACTICAL HELP
    • Support Network: "Is there anyone we can call for you? Who do you have at home for support?" (4:35)
    • Spiritual/Cultural Needs: "Are there any religious or cultural customs that are important to you now that we need to be aware of?"
    • Hospital Support: "We have a chaplaincy service and bereavement officers who are here to help. Would you like me to contact them for you?" (4:42)
    • Infection Control (if relevant): "Because this was a severe infection, are you or anyone at home feeling unwell at the moment?"
  4. PLAN ADMINISTRATIVE STEPS
    • Gently introduce necessary formalities. "In a little while, we will need to talk about the next steps, like the death certificate and the role of the coroner, but there is no rush at all." (4:53)
  5. PROVIDE FOLLOW-UP & CLOSURE
    • Provide a clear point of contact on the ward for future questions. (5:12)
    • Ensure they have a clear plan before they leave the room (e.g., who is taking them home).
