Hematemesis (Vomiting Blood)

1ST COMPLAINT: VOMITING BLOOD

S: Site, Severity & Quantification
  • CONFIRM SOURCE: Was it truly vomited (from the stomach) or coughed up (hemoptysis) or from a nosebleed (epistaxis)?
  • QUANTITY: How much blood was there?
    • Streaks of blood? Teaspoon? Tablespoon? A cupful?
  • SEVERITY (Hemodynamic Status):
    • Dizziness, lightheadedness (especially on standing), palpitations, sweating, confusion -> Signs of significant blood loss / shock.
"Can you confirm you vomited the blood, not coughed it up? How much came up, and what did it look like? Did you feel dizzy or faint afterwards?"
O: Onset & Context
  • ACUTE ONSET AFTER RETCHING/VOMITING:
    • Mallory-Weiss Tear (tear in the esophageal lining).
  • ACUTE, SUDDEN, LARGE VOLUME:
    • Esophageal Varices (especially in a patient with known liver disease).
    • Bleeding Peptic Ulcer.
  • MORE INSIDIOUS / RECURRENT:
    • Gastritis, Esophagitis, Peptic Ulcer Disease (PUD).
"When did this happen? What were you doing just before you vomited blood? Had you been vomiting or coughing forcefully beforehand?"
D: Duration & Frequency
  • How many episodes of hematemesis in the last 24 hours?
  • Has this ever happened before?
"Was this a single vomit, or has it happened multiple times?"
A/R: Aggravating & Relieving Factors
  • AGGRAVATING MEDICATIONS:
    • NSAIDs (Ibuprofen, Naproxen), Aspirin, Steroids -> PUD, Gastritis.
    • Anticoagulants (Warfarin, DOACs) / Antiplatelets (Clopidogrel).
  • AGGRAVATING SUBSTANCES:
    • Alcohol binge -> Mallory-Weiss Tear, Alcoholic Gastritis, Varices.
    • Spicy foods.
"Are you taking any medications like anti-inflammatories, aspirin, or blood thinners? How much alcohol do you drink?"
C: Character of Vomitus
  • BRIGHT RED BLOOD:
    • Indicates an active, brisk bleed. (e.g., Varices, Arterial ulcer bleed).
  • "COFFEE GROUND" EMESIS:
    • Dark brown/black, granular material. Indicates a slower bleed where blood has been altered by stomach acid. (e.g., PUD, Gastritis).
  • CLOTS PRESENT:
    • Suggests a more significant bleed.
  • MIXED WITH FOOD?
"Can you describe exactly what it looked like? Was it bright red blood, or was it dark and grainy like coffee grounds? Were there any clots?"
R: Radiation of Pain
  • Epigastric pain radiating to the back -> Can indicate a penetrating posterior duodenal ulcer.
  • Painless massive bleed is a classic presentation of Esophageal Varices.
"Are you having any abdominal pain? If so, where is it, and does it travel anywhere, like to your back?"
A: Associated Symptoms
  • OTHER GI SYMPTOMS:
    • Melena: Black, tarry, foul-smelling stools. Crucial sign of an Upper GI Bleed.
    • Hematochezia: Passing fresh red blood from the rectum (indicates a very large, rapid UGIB).
    • Abdominal pain, heartburn, dyspepsia (indigestion).
    • Difficulty or pain on swallowing (dysphagia/odynophagia).
  • SIGNS OF CHRONIC LIVER DISEASE:
    • Jaundice (yellow skin/eyes), ascites (swollen abdomen with fluid), spider nevi, easy bruising, confusion.
  • CONSTITUTIONAL SYMPTOMS:
    • Unexplained weight loss, loss of appetite. -> Red Flag for Malignancy.
"Have you noticed any changes in your bowel movements, like black, tarry stools? Any heartburn, weight loss, or yellowing of your skin?"
P: Progression
"Is the bleeding getting worse with each episode?"
P: Pattern
"Is this the first time this has ever happened?"
E: Effect on Life
  • Severe anxiety.
  • Inability to tolerate oral intake.
"How has this episode affected you? Are you feeling very anxious or weak?"
R: Red Flags
  • HEMODYNAMIC INSTABILITY: Tachycardia (>100 bpm), Hypotension (SBP <100 mmHg), postural dizziness, confusion, cold peripheries.
  • LARGE VOLUME of bright red blood or ongoing hematemesis.
  • STIGMATA of CHRONIC LIVER DISEASE (high risk for variceal bleed).
  • Associated WEIGHT LOSS and DYSPHAGIA (suspicious for malignancy).
  • Known AORTIC ANEURYSM or GRAFT (risk of catastrophic aorto-enteric fistula).
"I must check again for any warning signs. Are you feeling dizzy, confused, or sweaty? Is your heart racing?"
S: Severity / Scale
  • Clinical assessment (e.g., Glasgow-Blatchford Score) is used to determine risk and need for urgent intervention.
"Based on your symptoms, this is a serious condition that needs immediate attention."
