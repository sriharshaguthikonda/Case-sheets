FOCUSED HISTORY – SODA-CRAPPERS (7-month-old putting fingers in mouth)

  1. S – Situation / Specifics
    • Onset of hand-to-mouth behaviour; constant vs spells; relation to naps/feeds.
    • Fingers only or also toys/objects? NHS Teething hub
  2. O – Onset
    • Sudden vs gradual; any trigger (illness, new foods/teething flare).
  3. D – Duration
    • How long; pattern change over days/weeks.
  4. A/R – Aggravating / Relieving
    • Worse at night, hunger, or during flares?
    • What helps: chilled teething ring, cuddling, feeds? NHS tips for teething
  5. C – Character
    • Teething features: drooling, gum-rubbing, irritability (fever/diarrhoea not typical). NHS: teething symptoms
    • Mouth issues: white plaques that don’t wipe off, ulcers, bleeding gums, halitosis, feed refusal (thrush/HSV). NHS: oral thrush
    • Ear pulling, drool rash?
  6. R – Risks / Red flags
    • Fever with amber/red features, poor perfusion, non-blanching rash, respiratory distress, dehydration. NICE NG143 traffic-light
    • Foreign-body risk: access to button batteries, magnets, small parts, water beads; choking episodes? GOSH: button batteries
    • Note: diarrhoea or high fever are not reliable teething symptoms. NHS
  7. P – Progression / Pattern
    • Better/worse trend; day vs night; sleep effect.
  8. E – Effect on life
    • Feeds (volume/solids), sleep, parental coping.
  9. R – Related systems
    • ENT: ear pain, coryza, cough.
    • Contacts with mouth illnesses (HFM).
    • Growth/weight concerns.
  10. S – Severity / Scale
    • Parent distress 0–10; goals/expectations.
  11. Paediatric Context
    • Dentition timing 6–12 mo; any eruptions yet? NHS Teething
    • Development: mouthing is typical at this age (supervise; choking prevention). NHS choking advice
    • Environment: older siblings’ toys, coin/battery storage, supervision during play. GOSH safety

MANAGEMENT PLAN

1) Urgency (Decide now)

  • Admit/ED same day: any red NG143 features; suspected foreign-body ingestion (esp. button battery/magnet); unable to maintain hydration. NICE NG143 GOSH button battery
  • Manage in primary care: well child, feeding ok, typical teething/mouthing, no red flags.

2) Non-pharmacological advice (first-line)

  • Chilled teething ring or cool wet washcloth; avoid frozen-solid rings; supervise use. NHS tips
  • If on solids (≥6 mo): soft cold foods (e.g., melon, yoghurt); always supervise to prevent choking. NHS
  • Home safety: keep small parts, button batteries, magnets, water beads locked away. GOSH
  • Do not use amber teething jewellery (choking/strangulation risk). HSE Ireland safety advice

3) Medicines for discomfort (if distressed)

  • Give paracetamol or ibuprofen per NHS age/weight tables (check bottle strength). NHS paracetamol dosing NHS ibuprofen dosing
  • Do not give paracetamol and ibuprofen at the same time; for pain you may alternate doses if still in pain after 1 hour (keep a written schedule; never exceed max daily doses). NHS: ibuprofen with other meds NHS: paracetamol with other meds
  • Teething gels: limited evidence; only pharmacist-recommended infant products as second-line; avoid adult mouth-ulcer gels and salicylate-containing gels. NHS MHRA 2018 update MHRA: salicylate gels

4) Manage common differentials

  • Suspected oral thrush → infant oral nystatin; treat breastfeeding parent if symptomatic; maintain hygiene of bottles/soothers. NHS: breastfeeding & thrush
  • Suspected HSV gingivostomatitis → analgesia, cool fluids/soft foods; admit if hydration inadequate; consider aciclovir only in early severe cases/immunocompromise. RCH guideline
  • Suspected hand-foot-mouth → supportive care (cool fluids, soft foods, paracetamol/ibuprofen). NHS: HFM

5) Dental prevention

  • Start brushing at first tooth with a tiny smear fluoride toothpaste; twice daily incl. bedtime. NHS: looking after baby’s teeth
  • Register with a dentist when the first milk tooth appears or before 12 months; NHS dental care for children is free. NHS: children’s teeth

6) Safety-netting & parent resources

  • Return/ED now if: breathing difficulty, non-blanching rash, lethargy, severe dehydration (no tears/↓ wet nappies), persistent high fever, poor feeding, or suspected foreign-body (esp. button battery). NICE NG143 GOSH
  • Teach choking first aid for under-1s (5 back blows then 5 chest thrusts). NHS choking first aid

7) Follow-up & admin

  • If uncomplicated teething → self-care; review if pain persists or feeding/sleep is substantially affected.
  • Document: exam findings, weight/dose advice, non-pharm measures, pharmacy advice given, safety-netting, dental plan, who was present/consent.
