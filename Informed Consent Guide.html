C. EXPLAINING INFORMED CONSENT USING AN EXAMPLE (KNEE REPLACEMENT FOR CHRONIC ARTHRITIS) (Video 1:55)

The core idea is to explain everything about a proposed procedure so the patient can make an informed decision. (Video 1:44-1:55)

  • 1. Benefits: (Video 2:43-2:49)
    • Explain the positive outcomes. E.g., for knee replacement: improved ability to walk, increased mobility. (Video 2:58-3:07)
  • 2. Alternatives: (Video 3:15-3:17)
    • Discuss other available options, including their pros and cons. (Video 3:17-3:23)
    • E.g., for knee arthritis: Physiotherapy, ongoing painkiller use. (Video 3:26-3:34)
    • Discuss benefits of knee replacement vs. side effects of long-term painkillers. (Video 3:37-3:44)
  • 3. Listen: (Video 3:55-3:57)
    • Actively listen to the patient's concerns and questions. (Video 3:57-4:01)
    • Allow the patient a chance to voice their worries (e.g., about pain, mobility restrictions post-surgery). (Video 4:04-4:20)
  • 4. Assume Capacity: (Video 4:20-4:23)
    • Start with the presumption that the adult patient has the capacity to make their own decisions. (Video 4:26-4:33)
    • If during the conversation, the patient seems confused or unable to understand, then assess capacity further. (Video 4:33-4:44) (Capacity assessment: understanding, retention, weighing information, communicating decision - Video 9:18-9:31)
  • 5. Nature of the Treatment/Procedure: (Video 4:51-4:55)
    • Explain what the treatment involves (e.g., surgical option, medical option). (Video 4:58-5:05)
  • 6. Communicate Risks: (Video 5:05-5:09)
    • It's the doctor's duty to inform the patient about potential risks and complications. (Video 5:09-5:13)
    • Also, explain how these risks are managed. E.g., post-surgical pain managed with painkillers; risk of infection managed with antibiotics. (Video 5:13-5:24)
  • 7. Empower the Patient: (Video 5:27-5:31)
    • The patient has the ultimate right to make the decision (autonomy). (Video 5:31-5:38)
    • They can refuse treatment. (Video 5:38-5:43)
    • They have the right to a second opinion or to speak with other members of the healthcare team (e.g., consultant) before deciding. (Video 5:46-5:56)
  • 8. Document: (Video 6:00-6:04)
    • Document the discussion and the patient's decision in their medical records. (Video 6:04-6:13)

Informed consent is about ensuring the patient is fully aware of benefits, risks, alternatives, indications, and the nature of the procedure to make their own decision. (Video 6:19-6:45)

Using a specific example (like knee replacement or cholecystectomy) makes the explanation clearer for the learner. (Video 6:49-7:02)

D. GMC'S SEVEN PRINCIPLES OF CONSENT (Briefly mentioned, aligns with BALANCED) (Video 7:17-7:23)

  1. Patients have the right to be involved and supported in decision-making. (Video 7:25-7:35)
  2. Decision-making is an ongoing dialogue with relevant information exchange. (Video 7:35-7:45)
  3. Patients have the right to be listened to, given necessary information, time, and support to understand. (Video 7:45-7:54) (Corresponds to "Listen")
  4. Doctors must find out what matters to the patient to share relevant information on benefits, harms, and alternatives (including no action). (Video 7:56-8:06) (Corresponds to "Benefits," "Risks," "Alternatives")
  5. Presume all adult patients have capacity. Capacity can only be judged lacking for a specific decision at a specific time after legal assessment. (Video 8:18-8:32) (Corresponds to "Assume Capacity")
  6. Decisions for patients lacking capacity must be in their overall benefit, in consultation with those close to them or with power of attorney. (Video 8:34-8:55)
  7. Patients whose right to consent is affected by law should still be supported to be involved and exercise choice if possible. (Video 8:55-9:04)

The main scenario is about explaining the informed consent steps, not primarily assessing competency unless concerns arise. (Video 9:30-9:40)
