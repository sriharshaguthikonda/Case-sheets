MANAGEMENT PLAN — Medication Overuse Headache (MOH)

OBO Urgency

  • Red flags → same-day ED/urgent referral (treat as secondary headache until excluded): sudden severe “thunderclap”, meningism/fever, new neuro deficit, reduced GCS, head trauma, immunosuppression, pregnancy/puerperium, malignancy/anticoagulation, escalating pattern with systemic features. NICE CKS: Headache assessment & red flags
  • Typical MOH (no red flags): manage in primary care; routine neurology referral if uncertainty or failed withdrawals. NICE CKS MOH management
  • Consider specialist/inpatient tapered withdrawal if strong opioids, barbiturates, or benzodiazepines are overused, or significant comorbidity. NICE CKS

Patient-Specific Plan (Explain, Agree, Support)

  1. Diagnose & explain MOH simply; agree a “quit date” for overused acute meds; give written info.
    • Patient info: NICE (patient): Treatment for MOH, Migraine Trust, BASH resources
    • Start/continue a headache diary (frequency, severity, triggers, medication use). NICE CKS
  2. Core treatment — withdraw the overused acute medication(s)
    • Stop all overused acute headache drugs for ≥1 month. Stop abruptly for triptans, ergotamines and simple analgesics (paracetamol/NSAIDs). NICE CKS • NICE (patient)
    • If overusing strong opioids/barbiturates/benzodiazepines: avoid abrupt cessation; arrange specialist-led slow taper ± inpatient setting. NICE CKS
    • Set expectations: headaches may worsen for 1–2 weeks; withdrawal symptoms (nausea, sleep disturbance, anxiety, tachycardia) can occur and typically improve after 1–2 weeks; full recovery can take 2–3 months. NICE CKS
    • Avoid “rescue” analgesics during withdrawal (does not improve outcomes); plan non-drug coping strategies (rest, fluids, sleep hygiene, relaxation, cold packs; brief time off work if needed). NICE CKS (basis for recommendation)
  3. Address the underlying primary headache
    • Reassess at 4–8 weeks after starting withdrawal; then manage migraine/tension-type per guidance (acute + preventive). NICE CKS • NICE CG150
    • Consider starting preventive therapy during or after withdrawal if headaches remain frequent/bothersome (e.g., propranolol/topiramate/amitriptyline as per local/NICE migraine guidance). Note: evidence on prophylaxis to aid withdrawal is limited; the key is successful withdrawal. NICE CKS (prophylaxis notes) • BASH 2019
  4. Prevent relapse (long-term rules)
    • Limit all acute headache medicines to ≤2 days/week (≤8 days/month) after the first 2 months post-withdrawal. NICE CKS
    • Screen & manage comorbid stress/anxiety/depression & other chronic pain (these increase MOH risk). NICE CKS
    • Discuss safer acute options tailored to underlying headache (e.g., triptan ± antiemetic for migraine) with strict frequency limits; avoid opioids/codeine-containing combos. NICE CG150
  5. Follow-up & review schedule
    • Telephone/face-to-face support at ~2 weeks (withdrawal support).
    • Clinic review at 4–8 weeks (diary check, underlying headache plan).
    • Further review at 3 months (relapse prevention, adjust preventive).
  6. When to refer
    • Diagnostic uncertainty, atypical symptoms, red flags, refractory chronic daily headache despite withdrawal.
    • Repeated failed withdrawal attempts; overuse of strong opioids/barbiturates/benzodiazepines; significant comorbidities (consider inpatient/specialist detox). NICE CKS
    • Access to multidisciplinary pathways: NHS Modernising Patient Pathways: MOH (2023)
  7. Safety-netting (tell the patient)
    • Seek urgent care for red flags (sudden “worst ever” headache, fever/neck stiffness, new weakness/vision/speech change, head injury, pregnancy/puerperium, cancer/anticoagulation).
    • If withdrawal symptoms are severe/unmanageable (persistent vomiting/dehydration, confusion, uncontrolled pain) → contact GP/111/ED.

Documentation

  • Document diagnosis explanation, agreed stop date, diary issued, safety-netting, and follow-up appointments.
