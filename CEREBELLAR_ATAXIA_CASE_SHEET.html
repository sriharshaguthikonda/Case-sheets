EXAMINATION COMPONENTS (Overview starts at 2:17, elements detailed from 3:18)

The video references a slide showing these components, and the speaker discusses them generally rather than demonstrating each one sequentially on a patient for the full duration. Specific phrases/tests mentioned are timestamped below.

SPEECH (Discussed at 2:22 and from 3:23)
  • Ask patient to repeat phrases:
    • "British Constitution"
    • "Baby Hippopotamus"
    • "42 West Register Street"
  • Look for: Dysarthria (slurred speech), scanning speech (syllables separated), staccato speech (jerky, explosive).
EYE MOVEMENTS (Nystagmus) (Discussed at 2:30 and from 3:31)
  • Ask patient to follow your finger in an "H" pattern.
  • Observe for nystagmus (involuntary rhythmic eye movements).
  • Note direction of fast phase, gaze-evoked nystagmus. Cerebellar nystagmus is typically worse on looking towards the side of the lesion.
CO-ORDINATION
  • Upper Limb:
    • Finger-to-Nose Test (2:34 and from 3:34): Patient touches their nose, then your finger, repeatedly. Move your finger to different positions.
      Look for: Dysmetria (misjudging distance/target), intention tremor (tremor worsens on approaching target), past-pointing.
    • Dysdiadochokinesia (Rapid Alternating Movements) (2:35 and from 3:35): E.g., rapidly pronating and supinating hands on thighs or in the air.
      Look for: Clumsy, irregular, slow movements.
      1. Finger-tapping or opening/closing fists
      2. Foot-tapping
      3. Speech evaluation: rapidly repeating syllables like "pa-ta-ka", "papa", "tata", or "lala"
  • Lower Limb:
    • Heel-to-Shin Test (2:36 and from 3:36): Patient places one heel on the opposite knee and slides it down the shin to the ankle.
      Look for: Inaccuracy, wobbling, decomposition of movement.
BALANCE (Gait & Stance)
  • Gait (Walking) (2:39 and from 3:39): Observe patient walking normally.
    Look for: Broad-based gait, unsteadiness, staggering, difficulty turning.
  • Tandem Gait (Heel-to-Toe Walking) (2:39 and from 3:39): Walking in a straight line, placing heel directly in front of the toe of the other foot.
    Exacerbates unsteadiness.
  • Romberg Test (2:40 and from 3:40): Patient stands with feet together, arms by their sides.
    1. Eyes open.
    2. Eyes closed (stand by to prevent fall).
    Positive if patient sways significantly or loses balance *only when eyes are closed* (suggests sensory ataxia). If unsteady with eyes open, more likely cerebellar. In cerebellar ataxia, patient is often unsteady with eyes open and closed.
TONE (Mentioned as additional at 2:55 and from 3:54)
  • Assess muscle tone in upper and lower limbs (passive movements).
    Look for: Hypotonia (decreased tone) can be a feature of cerebellar lesions, especially acute ones. Pendular reflexes may also be seen.

MANAGEMENT DISCUSSION (Starts around 4:15)

  • Keep patient in hospital.
  • Investigations:
    • Blood tests (4:22) (to rule out infection, metabolic causes)
    • Urine test (4:25) (for UTI)
    • CT / MRI Scan of the brain (4:31) (to look for structural lesions like tumor, stroke, atrophy)
  • Explain that symptoms are concerning and further investigation is needed to find the cause.
