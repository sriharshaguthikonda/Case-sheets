    MANAGEMENT PLAN
    • Initial Investigations
      • Blood work: Usually not required unless systemically unwell or suspected complication (e.g., FBC, CRP if mastoiditis suspected).
      • Imaging: Usually not required unless complications suspected (e.g., CT scan for mastoiditis or intracranial spread).
      • Other:
        • Otoscopy: (Key for diagnosis - bulging, red, immobile tympanic membrane; possible perforation with discharge).
        • Tympanometry: (May show Type B or C curve, useful if diagnosis uncertain or to assess for OME).
    • Patient Communication
      • ICE: Explore patient/parent's Ideas (e.g., "Is it a bad infection?"), Concerns ("Will it affect hearing long-term?", "Does my child need antibiotics?"), Expectations ("I want something to stop the pain quickly.").
      • Diagnosis disclosure: Explain AOM, likely viral in many cases, often self-limiting.
      • Explanation of findings: Describe otoscopy findings simply.
    • Further Investigations
      • Specialized tests: Audiometry if hearing concerns persist post-resolution or with recurrent AOM.
      • Referrals: ENT referral if: recurrent AOM (e.g., 3+ in 6 months, 4+ in 12 months), persistent OME post-AOM, complications suspected, diagnostic uncertainty.
    • Treatment Plan
      • Symptomatic treatment:
        • Analgesia:
        • Paracetamol fever or
        • Ibuprofen pain
        • Anti emetics
        • prochlorperazine
      • Specific treatment: (Based on NICE CG69 - Otitis Media (Acute))
      • Non-Medical:
        1. Watchful waiting/Safety netting: Advise that symptoms usually resolve in 3 days; provide "delayed/back-up prescription" for antibiotics in some cases.
        2. Warm compress to the ear may provide comfort.
        3. Encourage fluid intake.
      • Medical:
        • Analgesia (as above).
        • Antibiotics (e.g., Amoxicillin first-line, or Clarithromycin if penicillin allergy):
          • Consider immediate antibiotics if: systemically unwell, symptoms of serious illness, high risk of complications (e.g., immunosuppression, significant comorbidities), child <2 years with bilateral AOM, or child with otorrhoea due to AOM.
          • Consider delayed prescription if not meeting immediate criteria but at risk of worsening.
        • Decongestants/Antihistamines: Not recommended by NICE for AOM.
      • Surgical: Myringotomy with or without grommet insertion for recurrent AOM meeting specific criteria (not for uncomplicated acute episode).
      • Admission required: Rarely. Yes if: suspected serious complication (e.g., mastoiditis, meningitis, facial palsy), systemically very unwell, unable to manage oral intake/medication.
    • Follow Up
      • Frequency: Review if symptoms worsen or do not improve within 2-3 days (if on watchful waiting) or after completing antibiotics. Follow-up for hearing check if OME suspected post-AOM.
      • Specialist referrals: As above (ENT).
      • Allied health: Speech and language therapy if prolonged OME impacts development.
    • Patient Education
      • Warning Signs/Red Flags: Worsening pain despite analgesia, new swelling/redness behind ear, facial weakness, high fever, stiff neck, lethargy, persistent vomiting.
      • Educational materials provided: Leaflet on AOM, managing fever, pain relief.
      • Useful resources/links: NHS website information on AOM.


      • Patient-Specific Plan

      1. Acute Otitis Media (AOM)

      1. Initial Investigations
        • Blood work: Usually not required unless systemically unwell or suspected complication (e.g., FBC, CRP if mastoiditis suspected).
        • Imaging: Usually not required unless complications suspected (e.g., CT scan for mastoiditis or intracranial spread).
        • Other:
          • Otoscopy: (Key for diagnosis - bulging, red, immobile tympanic membrane; possible perforation with discharge).
          • Tympanometry: (May show Type B or C curve, useful if diagnosis uncertain or to assess for OME).
      2. Patient Communication

        • Diagnosis disclosure: Explain AOM, likely viral in many cases, often self-limiting.
        • Explanation of findings: Describe otoscopy findings simply.

        • Referrals: ENT referral if: recurrent AOM (e.g., 3+ in 6 months, 4+ in 12 months), persistent OME post-AOM, complications suspected, diagnostic uncertainty.
      4. Treatment Plan
        • Symptomatic treatment:
          • Analgesia: Paracetamol or Ibuprofen for pain and fever (dose according to age/weight).
        • Specific treatment: (Based on NICE CG69 - Otitis Media (Acute))
        • Non-Medical:

          • Decongestants/Antihistamines: Not recommended by NICE for AOM.
        • Surgical: Myringotomy with or without grommet insertion for recurrent AOM meeting specific criteria (not for uncomplicated acute episode).
        • Admission required: Rarely. Yes if: suspected serious complication (e.g., mastoiditis, meningitis, facial palsy), systemically very unwell, unable to manage oral intake/medication.

        • Specialist referrals: As above (ENT).

        • Educational materials provided: Leaflet on AOM, managing fever, pain relief.
        • Useful resources/links: NHS website information on AOM.

      2. Otitis Externa (OE)

      1. Initial Investigations
        • Blood work: Usually not required unless systemically unwell or suspected malignant OE (e.g., FBC, ESR, CRP, Glucose in diabetics).
        • Imaging: Rarely. CT/MRI if malignant OE or complications suspected.
        • Other:
          • Otoscopy: (Key - inflamed, swollen ear canal, debris, discharge; TM may be obscured or normal).
          • Ear swab for MC&S: If recurrent, severe, not responding to initial treatment, or in immunocompromised patients.
      2. Patient Communication
        • ICE: Ideas ("Is it an infection from swimming?"), Concerns ("Will it spread?", "How long until the pain stops?"), Expectations ("I need strong antibiotics.").
        • Diagnosis disclosure: Explain Otitis Externa (inflammation/infection of ear canal).
        • Explanation of findings: Describe otoscopy findings.
      3. Further Investigations
        • Specialized tests: As above (swab).
        • Referrals: ENT referral if: severe OE, not responding to primary care treatment, suspected malignant OE, canal very swollen preventing drop administration (may need wick/aural toilet), diagnostic uncertainty.
      4. Treatment Plan
        • Symptomatic treatment:
          • Analgesia: Paracetamol, Ibuprofen. Codeine if severe pain.
        • Specific treatment: (Based on NICE CKS - Otitis Externa)
        • Non-Medical:
          1. Keep ear dry: Avoid swimming, use earplugs or cotton wool with petroleum jelly when showering/bathing.
          2. Avoid inserting anything into the ear canal (cotton buds, fingers).
          3. Remove any hearing aids from affected ear until resolved.
        • Medical:
          • Topical Acetic Acid 2% spray/drops: For mild OE.
          • Topical antibiotic drops/spray: (e.g., Ciprofloxacin, Gentamicin, Neomycin-containing).
            • first-line for moderate/severe OE to reduce inflammation and pain (e.g., Otomize, Gentisone HC).
          • Aural toilet/microsuction: By trained professional if canal occluded by debris/swelling.
          • Ear wick: May be inserted by GP/ENT if canal very swollen to help deliver drops.
          • Oral antibiotics: If infection spreading beyond ear canal (cellulitis of pinna/face), systemic upset, or in immunocompromised/diabetic patients with more severe OE. Flucloxacillin or Clarithromycin.
          • Antifungal drops: If fungal infection suspected or confirmed (otomycosis).
        • Surgical: Rarely needed for uncomplicated OE. Incision & drainage if abscess forms.
        • Admission required: Yes if: suspected malignant (necrotizing) otitis externa, severe cellulitis, systemically unwell requiring IV antibiotics.
      5. Follow Up
        • Frequency: Review in 1 week if not improving or sooner if worsening. For malignant OE, close specialist follow-up.
        • Specialist referrals: ENT as above.
      6. Patient Education
        • Warning Signs/Red Flags: Worsening pain despite treatment, spreading redness/swelling, facial weakness, persistent fever, discharge not improving.
        • Educational materials provided: Leaflet on Otitis Externa, how to apply ear drops correctly, water precautions.
        • Useful resources/links: NHS website.

      3. Otitis Media with Effusion (OME / Glue Ear)

      1. Initial Investigations
        • Blood work: Not applicable.
        • Imaging: Not applicable.
        • Other:
          • Otoscopy: (Dull, retracted tympanic membrane, air-fluid levels or bubbles, yellowish/blueish discoloration).
          • Tympanometry: (Key - typically Type B curve indicating middle ear fluid).
          • Audiometry: (To quantify conductive hearing loss).
      2. Patient Communication
        • ICE: Ideas ("Is it an infection?", "Will it go away by itself?"), Concerns ("Will it affect my child's speech/schooling?"), Expectations ("Do they need grommets straight away?").
        • Diagnosis disclosure: Explain OME (fluid in middle ear without acute infection).
        • Explanation of findings: Show/explain otoscopy and tympanometry results.
      3. Further Investigations
        • Specialized tests: Repeat audiometry and tympanometry after observation period.
        • Referrals: ENT referral if persistent OME (>3 months) with significant hearing loss or impact on development/quality of life. Speech and Language Therapy assessment if concerns about development.
      4. Treatment Plan
        • Symptomatic treatment: Manage any associated nasal congestion if relevant (though evidence for decongestants in OME is poor).
        • Specific treatment: (Based on NICE CG60 - Otitis Media with Effusion in under 12s)
        • Non-Medical:
          1. Active Observation ('Watchful Waiting'): For 3 months initially, as many cases resolve spontaneously.
          2. Autoinflation (e.g., Otovent device): Consider for children able to cooperate, during observation period.
          3. Educational support: Advise on communication strategies (face child, reduce background noise).
        • Medical:
          • Hearing Aids: Consider if OME persistent and surgery contraindicated or declined, or as an interim measure.
          • Antibiotics/Steroids/Antihistamines/Decongestants: Not routinely recommended by NICE for managing OME.
        • Surgical:
          • Grommet (Ventilation Tube) Insertion: Consider if persistent bilateral OME for ≥3 months AND hearing loss in better ear ≥25-30 dBHL, OR significant impact on development/education/quality of life.
          • Adenoidectomy: Consider if nasal obstruction due to adenoids and OME, or as part of grommet surgery in certain cases.
        • Admission required: No, surgery is typically a day-case procedure.
      5. Follow Up
        • Frequency: After 3 months of active observation. Post-grommet insertion checks (e.g., 6 weeks, then as needed until extrusion).
        • Specialist referrals: ENT, Audiology, Speech and Language Therapy as indicated.
      6. Patient Education
        • Warning Signs/Red Flags: Ear discharge post-grommets, signs of acute infection, worsening hearing.
        • Educational materials provided: Leaflet on OME, grommets, autoinflation.
        • Useful resources/links: National Deaf Children's Society (NDCS), NHS website.

      4. Impacted Earwax (Cerumen Impaction)

      1. Initial Investigations
        • Blood work: Not applicable.
        • Imaging: Not applicable.
        • Other:
          • Otoscopy: (To visualize wax and confirm impaction, assess TM if visible).
          • Tuning fork tests (Rinne/Weber): May suggest conductive loss if wax is occlusive.
      2. Patient Communication
        • ICE: Ideas ("Do I need syringing?"), Concerns ("Is it damaging my hearing?"), Expectations ("I want it out now.").
        • Diagnosis disclosure: Explain impacted earwax.
        • Explanation of findings: Show otoscopy if possible, explain role of wax.
      3. Further Investigations
        • Specialized tests: Audiometry if hearing loss persists after wax removal or if underlying sensorineural loss suspected.
        • Referrals: ENT or audiology clinic for microsuction or irrigation if primary care methods fail or are contraindicated.
      4. Treatment Plan
        • Symptomatic treatment: Address any discomfort if present (rare).
        • Specific treatment: (Based on NICE CKS - Earwax)
        • Non-Medical:
          1. Advise against using cotton buds or other objects to clean ear canal.
        • Medical:
          • Ear drops to soften wax: Olive oil, almond oil, sodium bicarbonate 5%, or urea hydrogen peroxide 5% drops for 3-5 days.
        • Procedural (if drops fail and wax is symptomatic/obscuring TM view):
          • Ear Irrigation (previously syringing): With water at body temperature, if no contraindications (e.g., TM perforation, active infection).
          • Microsuction: Preferred method by many ENTs, especially if irrigation contraindicated or previous problems.
          • Manual removal: Using instruments by a trained professional.
        • Surgical: Not applicable for simple wax.
        • Admission required: No.
      5. Follow Up
        • Frequency: Usually not required unless symptoms persist after treatment or for recurrent impaction needing regular management.
        • Specialist referrals: As above.
      6. Patient Education
        • Warning Signs/Red Flags: Pain, discharge, or worsening hearing after attempted removal – seek review.
        • Educational materials provided: Leaflet on earwax and safe ear care, how to use ear drops.
        • Useful resources/links: NHS website.

      Further conditions like Tinnitus, Sudden Sensorineural Hearing Loss (SSNHL), Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo (BPPV), and Meniere's Disease would follow a similar structure, with investigations and treatments tailored to those specific NICE guidelines and clinical presentations. For example, SSNHL requires URGENT referral and consideration of steroids. BPPV involves diagnostic maneuvers (Dix-Hallpike) and treatment maneuvers (Epley). Tinnitus management focuses on coping and sound therapy.
