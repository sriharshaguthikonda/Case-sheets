History of Presenting Complaint – SODA CRAPPERS

  1. S – Site / field affected “Is the blurriness more central, around the edges, or everywhere in your vision?”
  2. O – Onset “When did you first notice your sight becoming hazy?”
  3. D – Duration / progression “Has it been getting gradually worse or does it come and go?”
  4. A – Aggravating factors “Does anything such as dim light, tiredness, or eye strain make it worse?”
  5. R – Relieving factors “Do rest, better lighting, or new glasses improve it?”
  6. C – Character “Would you describe the change as hazy, foggy, or loss of parts of your visual field?”
  7. R – Radiation / field defects “Have you noticed missing areas or tunnel vision at the sides?”
  8. A – Associated symptoms
    • Eye pain / headache / halos around lights – exclude acute angle‑closure
    • Redness, photophobia, watering
    • Headache, nausea, vomiting
  9. P – Progression / pattern “Can you see any pattern to the worsening?”
  10. P – Pattern (continuous vs intermittent)
  11. E – Effect on life / ICE “How is this affecting work or driving?”
  12. R – Red‑flags
    • Sudden vision loss → urgent ophthalmology (NICE QS7)
  13. S – Severity (0–10 scale) “On a scale of 0 to 10, how bad is the blurriness today?”

Risk‑Factor Screen

  • Previous eye disease, trauma, or surgery
  • Diabetes mellitus — retinopathy risk
  • Hypertension / vascular disease
  • Long‑term or high‑dose steroid use (oral, inhaled, nasal, topical) BNF
  • Family history of glaucoma (first‑degree relative) – earlier screening (NICE NG81)
  • High myopia / thin cornea
  • Ethnicity: African‑Caribbean heritage (higher prevalence, earlier onset)
  • Smoking status, alcohol intake

Past Medical History & Medications

  • Chronic illnesses: cardiovascular, endocrine, respiratory
  • Current & recent medications incl. OTC / herbal; specifically enquire about steroids, anticholinergics, antidepressants
  • Allergies & previous adverse drug reactions

Social History

  • Occupation (screen use, machinery, driving licence requirements)
  • Driving – vision standards & DVLA notification DVLA Guidance
  • Smoking & alcohol
  • Activities of daily living & support at home

ICE Questions

  • Ideas: “What do you think might be causing this change in your vision?”
  • Concerns: “What worries you most about your eyesight?”
  • Expectations: “What are you hoping we can do for you today?”

Safety‑Net Red‑Flag Check

  • Sudden painful visual loss, coloured halos, severe eye pain – suspect acute angle‑closure (same‑day referral) CKS Red Eye
  • New‑onset severe headache with visual symptoms – rule out neurological emergencies

CASE HISTORY (Rachel Keller, 44 y, GP surgery – 29 Jul 2025)

  • PC: Gradual, painless bilateral blurred vision for ≈ 4 months.
  • Onset / Course: Insidious, progressively worsening; no sudden drops.
  • Field: General haziness centrally & peripherally; dim‑light & night‑driving difficulty.
  • Associated symptoms: ❌ pain  ❌ redness  ❌ halos  ❌ headache  ❌ nausea.
  • Current correction: Wears reading glasses (prescription updated 1 y ago) – no improvement.
  • Aggravating: Dim lighting, end‑of‑day eye fatigue.
  • Relieving: Nil; brighter light only marginal benefit.
  • PMH: No diabetes, hypertension, or other chronic illness.
  • Medications: PRN paracetamol only; no steroids or eye‑drops.
  • Allergies: None known.
  • Family Hx: Mother treated with unknown eye‑drops for presumed glaucoma; grandmother had poor eyesight.
  • Social: Works on computer full‑time; drives (night vision affected); 2 children; non‑smoker & modest alcohol (not explicitly asked).
  • ICE: Idea – suspects glaucoma after Google search; Concern – fear of blindness & job loss; Expectation – prompt treatment to preserve sight.
  • Optometrist findings: Raised IOP → referred urgently.
  • Exam (GP): ↓ visual acuity OU; pupils equal/reactive; optic discs cupped; vitals normal.
  • Red‑flags ruled out: No acute pain, halos, or sudden vision loss – acute ACG unlikely.

Key references: NICE NG81 (Glaucoma Diagnosis & Management); RCOphth POAG Guidance. Template built with SODA CRAPPERS framework.

MANAGEMENT PLAN

Urgency & Referral

  • Same‑day telephone discussion with on‑call ophthalmology SpR if IOP > 30 mmHg or rapid progression suspected.
  • Otherwise urgent glaucoma outpatient referral within 2 weeks (NICE NG81) for definitive diagnosis & comprehensive assessment.
  • Provide written referral letter including VA, IOP values, optic‑disc photos & family history.

Immediate Medical Measures (Primary Care)

  • Start topical prostaglandin analogue (latanoprost 0.005 % nocte) unless contraindicated BNF.
  • If delay to clinic > 48 h, add beta‑blocker (timolol 0.5 % BD) unless asthma/COPD/bradycardia.
  • Demonstrate drop technique & advise punctal occlusion 1 min.

Baseline & Clinic Investigations

  1. Central corneal thickness (pachymetry) – calibrate IOP target.
  2. Goldmann applanation tonometry – confirm raised IOP.
  3. Optic‑nerve OCT & automated perimetry – staging.
  4. Blood pressure & cardiovascular screen.

Lifestyle & Safety‑Net Advice

  • Inform DVLA once field defect confirmed; avoid night driving until cleared DVLA.
  • Stop smoking; maintain regular aerobic exercise.
  • Seek urgent help for sudden eye pain, halos, vomiting, or vision drop.
  • Avoid high‑dose or prolonged steroid use; check with pharmacist.

Follow‑Up & Monitoring

  • Clinic review at 4–6 weeks to ensure ≥ 20 % IOP reduction.
  • Escalate to dual drops / SLT laser / surgery per NICE algorithm if target not reached.
  • Annual OCT & visual‑field once stable.

Patient Education & Support

  • Provide Glaucoma UK leaflet & local support‑group details.
  • Acknowledge anxiety; offer referral to counselling if needed.

Documentation

  • Record consent, medication issued, safety‑net & DVLA advice in GP notes.
  • Send e‑referral & copy to optometrist; give patient printed summary.

QUALITATIVE FEEDBACK – 11 KEY DOMAINS

1. Language

  • ❌ “Are you having any headache Hollows? Glares?” → ✅ “Have you had headaches or coloured halos around lights?” (GMC GMP §9 Clear communication)
  • ❌ “Your eyesight will recover…” → ✅ “Treatment can slow or stop further damage; vision already lost usually can’t be restored.”

2. Listening

  • ❌ Didn’t pick up on Rachel’s night‑driving difficulty. → ✅ Explore impact on driving & DVLA duties, then advise accordingly.
  • ❌ Asked about symptoms but rarely reflected back her answers. → ✅ Use phrases like “You mentioned it’s worse in dim light—let’s look at why that happens.”

3. Rapport

  • ❌ Frequent fillers (“uh…”) & abrupt question shifts can feel rushed. → ✅ Brief pauses & signposting keep the patient oriented.
  • ✅ Offering tissues & naming feelings showed empathy—keep this consistency.

4. Issues

  • ❌ Chose in‑patient admission although NICE NG81 suggests urgent outpatient care. → ✅ Match management setting to guideline severity.
  • ❌ Over‑promised visual recovery. → ✅ Focus on realistic goals (prevent progression).

5. Consultation Structure

  • ❌ Jumped to management before finishing history. → ✅ Follow ICE→History→Exam→Plan sequence.
  • ❌ Repeated questions (“Anything that happened?”) → ✅ Use closed paraphrase to clarify instead of repeating.

6. Professionalism

  • ❌ Provided false reassurance. → ✅ Be honest about uncertainty; signpost referral (GMC GMP §33).
  • ✅ Checked for allergies & consent before examination—retain.

7. Cultural Sensitivity

  • ❌ Did not enquire about beliefs or support systems that might affect eyedrop adherence. → ✅ Ask “Any cultural or personal factors that might affect using drops nightly?”

8. Handling Difficult Situations

  • ✅ Recognised anxiety & offered tissues.
  • ❌ Lacked a structured “SPIKES” approach when discussing potential glaucoma. → ✅ Preface bad news, pause, check understanding, then outline next steps.

9. Teamwork

  • ✅ Planned to involve ophthalmology & inform charge nurse.
  • ❌ Didn’t share findings with optometrist or document DVLA advice for wider team. → ✅ Copy correspondence to all contributors.

10. Self‑awareness

  • ❌ Spoke definitively about long‑term outcomes without specialist confirmation. → ✅ Acknowledge limits: “I’ll confirm with the glaucoma team.”

11. Voice & Pace

  • ❌ Rapid delivery in places (e.g., when listing plan) may overwhelm. → ✅ Slow slightly & chunk information; invite teach‑back.
