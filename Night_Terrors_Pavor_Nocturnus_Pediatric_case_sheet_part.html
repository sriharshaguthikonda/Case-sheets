• S: Site (of Manifestation)
    • Where does the event primarily occur? (e.g., child's bed, another room if sleepwalking is involved).
    • Are there any physical signs observed on the child during or after the episode (e.g., sweating, flushed face)?
    • "Site" is less about a location of pain and more about the setting and physical manifestations.
  • O: Onset
    • At what age did the night terrors first begin?
    • Was there any specific trigger or event around the time they started (e.g., illness, stress, change in routine, new medication)?
    • Did they start suddenly or was there a gradual increase in frequency/intensity?
    • Night terrors typically begin in early childhood, often between ages 3-7, but can occur earlier or later.
  • D: Duration
    • How long does a typical night terror episode last? (Usually a few minutes, but can range from 1 to 10+ minutes).
    • How long has the child been experiencing these episodes overall (weeks, months, years)?
  • A: Aggravating / Relieving Factors
    • Aggravating Factors (Triggers):
      • Sleep deprivation or irregular sleep schedules?
      • Fever or illness?
      • Stress, anxiety, or exciting/upsetting events during the day?
      • Full bladder?
      • Noisy sleep environment or other disturbances during sleep?
      • Certain medications (e.g., some stimulants, sedatives, antihistamines)?
      • Traveling or sleeping in an unfamiliar environment?
      • Growth spurts or developmental changes?
      • Family history of parasomnias (night terrors, sleepwalking)?
    • Relieving Factors (or attempted interventions during/after an episode):
      • Does waking the child fully stop the episode? (Usually difficult to wake, and may lead to confusion if woken).
      • What do caregivers typically do during an episode? (e.g., try to comfort, hold, speak to the child – often with little effect during the terror itself).
      • Does ensuring adequate sleep and a regular sleep schedule help reduce frequency?
      • Scheduled awakenings (waking the child briefly about 15-30 minutes before episodes typically occur, if predictable)?
      • Creating a safe sleep environment to prevent injury?
      • Reassurance after the child fully awakens (though they usually have no memory of the event)?
  • C: Character (Description of the Episode)
    • Child's Appearance and Behavior During Episode:
      • Sudden arousal from sleep?
      • Intense crying, screaming, shouting?
      • Signs of intense fear or panic (e.g., wide eyes, terrified expression)?
      • Sitting up in bed, thrashing, kicking, flailing?
      • May appear confused, disoriented, and unresponsive to comfort or attempts to awaken.
      • Eyes may be open with a "glassy" stare.
      • Sweating, rapid breathing (tachypnea), rapid heart rate (tachycardia), flushed skin, dilated pupils?
      • May mumble, speak incoherently, or not speak at all.
      • Sleepwalking (somnambulism) may co-occur.
    • Child's State After the Episode:
      • Usually returns to sleep quickly without fully waking up.
      • Amnesia for the event: Child has no memory of the terror, dream, or behaviors the next morning.
      • If awakened during an episode, the child may be confused, disoriented, and agitated for a period.
    • Is there any dream content recalled by the child? (Typically no, unlike nightmares).
  • R: Radiation
    • Not applicable to night terrors.
    • This element is not used for describing parasomnias.
  • A: Associated Symptoms (Daytime or other sleep-related)
    • Daytime sleepiness or fatigue (if episodes are frequent or disruptive to overall sleep)?
    • Behavioral problems or mood changes during the day?
    • Other sleep problems:
      • Sleepwalking (somnambulism)?
      • Sleep talking (somniloquy)?
      • Nightmares (distinct from night terrors – child usually remembers the dream and is easily comforted)?
      • Bruxism (teeth grinding)?
      • Restless Leg Syndrome symptoms?
      • Snoring or signs of sleep-disordered breathing (e.g., gasping, pauses in breathing observed by caregiver)?
    • Symptoms of anxiety or stress?
    • Headaches, especially if there's concern about underlying neurological issues or sleep deprivation.
    • Enuresis (bedwetting) can sometimes co-occur with other parasomnias.
    • Signs of underlying medical conditions that could trigger night terrors (e.g., GERD causing discomfort, fever).
  • P: Pattern (Timing / Frequency of Episodes)
    • Timing during the night:
      • Typically occur in the first third of the night (during deep non-REM sleep, stages N3). Usually 1-3 hours after falling asleep.
      • Rarely occur during naps.
    • Frequency:
      • How many episodes per night/week/month?
      • Are they clustered (several nights in a row then a period without)?
      • Is the frequency increasing, decreasing, or stable?
    • Is there a predictable time each night when they occur?
  • P: Progression
    • How have the night terrors changed since they first started? (e.g., become more intense, more frequent, less frequent, associated with new behaviors like sleepwalking).
    • Night terrors often decrease in frequency and resolve spontaneously as the child gets older, usually by adolescence.
  • E: Effect on life (Child and Family)
    • Child:
      • Although the child doesn't remember the episode, is there any observed daytime sleepiness, fatigue, or mood changes?
      • Risk of injury during an episode (e.g., falling out of bed, hitting objects if sleepwalking)?
    • Family:
      • Significant distress, anxiety, and sleep disruption for parents/caregivers witnessing the episodes.
      • Concern about the child's well-being or potential underlying problems.
      • Impact on family sleep quality.
      • Strain on parental relationships due to stress and sleep loss.
  • S: Severity / Scale
    • Severity is often judged by:
      • Frequency and intensity of episodes.
      • Degree of disruption to the child's and family's sleep.
      • Risk of injury.
      • Level of distress experienced by caregivers.
    • A specific numerical scale for night terror severity is not standard, but these factors assess clinical significance.

Additional Contextual Information to Gather for Night Terrors:

  • Detailed sleep history: Bedtime, wake time, total sleep duration, sleep hygiene practices, nap schedule.
  • Family history of parasomnias (night terrors, sleepwalking, nightmares), sleep disorders, or psychiatric conditions.
  • Child's daily routine, school performance, social interactions.
  • Any recent significant life events or stressors.
  • Medication history (prescription, over-the-counter, supplements).
  • Caffeine or stimulant intake (especially in older children/adolescents).
  • Screen time habits, especially before bed.
  • Previous evaluations or treatments for the night terrors or other sleep problems.

Negative History:

  1. Mention relevant negative findings related to the presenting illness, e.g., No h/o fever, vomiting, loose stools, seizures, etc.
