    DRUG HISTORY

    Confusion
    • 1. Sedatives & Hypnotics (Very Common)

      (Direct CNS depressants; a leading cause, especially in elderly and hospitalized patients.)

      • Benzodiazepines (e.g., Lorazepam, Diazepam, Alprazolam)
      • "Z-drugs" for sleep (e.g., Zolpidem, Eszopiclone)
    • 2. Anticholinergics (Very Common)

      (Classic cause of delirium; many are available over-the-counter.)

      • Antihistamines (e.g., Diphenhydramine - Benadryl)
      • Tricyclic Antidepressants (e.g., Amitriptyline)
      • Overactive Bladder Medications (e.g., Oxybutynin)
      • Some Antipsychotics (e.g., Olanzapine, Quetiapine)
    • 3. Opioid Analgesics (Common)

      (Especially in high doses, in the elderly, or in combination with other sedatives.)

      • Morphine
      • Oxycodone
      • Hydromorphone
      • Tramadol
    • 4. Corticosteroids

      (Known to cause mood changes, psychosis, and confusion, especially at high doses.)

      • Prednisone
      • Dexamethasone
    • 5. Dopamine Agonists (for Parkinson's)

      (Frequently cause hallucinations and confusion in their target population.)

      • Levodopa/Carbidopa
      • Pramipexole
      • Ropinirole
    • 6. Anticonvulsants (Anti-seizure medications)
      • Phenytoin (especially at toxic levels)
      • Carbamazepine
      • Gabapentin (especially with kidney impairment)
    • 7. Muscle Relaxants
      • Cyclobenzaprine
      • Baclofen
    • 8. Antibiotics

      (An important consideration, particularly in hospitalized or very ill patients.)

      • Fluoroquinolones (e.g., Ciprofloxacin, Levofloxacin)
      • Macrolides (e.g., Clarithromycin)
      • Cephalosporins (e.g., Cefepime)
    • 9. Cardiovascular Drugs (Less Common)

      (Important due to widespread use, but confusion is a less frequent side effect.)

      • Beta-blockers (especially Propranolol)
      • Digoxin (classic cause, especially in toxicity)
