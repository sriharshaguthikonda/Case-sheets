MEDICAL SCENARIO DISCUSSIONS & MANAGEMENT APPROACHES

SCENARIO 1: ORAL THRUSH IN INFANT (Video 0:20)

  • Situation: FY2 in GP surgery. Jennifer (26 y/o mother) is worried about her 5-month-old child who has had a white coating on the tongue for 1-2 weeks. Task: Talk, assess, manage. (Video 0:20-1:00)
  • History Taking (from Mother):
    • Elaborate on the "whitish coating": Onset, duration (given as 1-2 weeks), anything making it worse/better. (Video 1:11-1:46) (Information given: getting worse - Video 1:46-1:52)
    • Feeding & Discomfort: Is the child able to eat/drink? Any signs of discomfort or problems with feeding? (Video 1:57-2:20)
    • Systemic Symptoms: Fever? (Video 2:20-2:27)
    • Rule out Meningitis (Red Flag): Neck stiffness, photophobia. (Video 2:27-2:39)
    • Other Infections: UTI (crying on urination), diarrhea, constipation. (Video 2:39-2:44)
    • Other Rashes: Any rash elsewhere, especially in the nappy area (as thrush can be systemic). (Video 2:58-3:10)
    • Birth History & General Well-being: Who looks after the child, birth details. (Video 3:17-3:30)
  • Examination: General physical examination, vitals, oral cavity examination. (Image shown: whitish coating on tongue, consistent with oral thrush). (Video 3:35-3:47)
  • Risk Factors for Oral Thrush in Infants: (Video 3:50-3:54)
    1. Immunocompromised child. (Video 4:01-4:08)
    2. Recent antibiotic use. (Video 4:08-4:15)
    3. Steroid use (by child). (Video 4:15-4:20)
    4. Breastfeeding (mother might have thrush on nipples, transferring to baby). (Video 4:20-4:39)
    5. Improper sterilization of dummies (pacifiers) or feeding bottles. (Video 4:39-4:50)
  • Diagnosis & Explanation: Oral thrush, common in infants (around 6 months), caused by Candida (yeast). Appears as white patches, may cause mild discomfort, not usually dangerous. Often triggered after antibiotics. (Video 5:24-5:42)
  • Treatment: (Video 5:48-5:54)
    • Nystatin oral suspension OR Miconazole oral gel (first-line). (Video 5:54-6:00)
      • Nystatin: e.g., 1ml four times a day, use dropper to apply directly in the mouth. Continue for 48 hours after symptoms clear. (Video 6:00-6:06, 6:06-6:13)
  • Advice to Mother: (Video 6:18)
    • Do NOT try to scrape off the white patches. (Video 6:25)
    • Avoid sugary drinks or sweet-dipped dummies. (Video 6:25-6:32)
    • Continue regular feeding. (Video 6:32-6:37)
    • If breastfeeding: Mother should clean nipples and let them air dry. Check for maternal nipple thrush. (Video 6:37-6:49)
    • Sterilize bottles, dummies, and teats thoroughly. (Video 6:49-6:56)
  • When to Seek Further Help (Red Flags/Warning Signs): (Video 6:56-7:03)
    • No improvement in 7-10 days after starting treatment. (Video 7:10-7:21)
    • Child not eating or drinking well. (Video 7:21-7:28)
    • Rashes getting worse. (Video 7:28-7:34)
    • Recurrent episodes of thrush. (Video 7:34-7:39)

SCENARIO 2: SENSORY ATAXIA (Video 7:55)

  • Situation: Patient (35 y/o) in GP surgery presents with lower limb weakness. Task: Talk, assess, discuss management. (Video 7:55-8:11) (Differential could be GBS, cerebellar ataxia - Video 8:18-8:25)
  • Differentiating GBS vs. Cerebellar Ataxia (Brief Overview):
    • Guillain-Barré Syndrome (GBS): Rapid onset (days/weeks), ascending paralysis (legs upwards), often follows viral/bacterial infection, reflexes may be absent, primarily motor weakness. (Video 8:20-8:40, 8:40-8:50, 8:50-9:09, 9:15-9:21, 9:47-10:06, 10:25-10:30, 10:30-10:42, 11:02)
    • Cerebellar Ataxia: Gradual onset (weeks/months/years), uncoordinated movements, difficulty with balance/coordination, progression slow and fluctuating, may have normal reflexes, various causes (genetic, alcohol, toxins, vitamin B12 deficiency). (Video 8:45-8:50, 8:50-8:58, 9:28-9:35, 9:35-9:41, 9:41-9:47, 10:06-10:12, 10:12-10:19, 10:30-10:37, 11:02-11:15)
  • History Taking (for Lower Limb Weakness): (Video 11:46)
    • Patient reports weakness in legs for a few days. (Video 11:52-11:57)
    • Elaborate: Heaviness, tiredness, one or both legs (both legs suggests systemic). (Video 11:57-12:13)
    • Coordination: Losing balance? Trouble with coordination? (Patient: No). (Video 12:13-12:24)
    • Bladder/Bowel issues? (Patient: No). (Video 12:24-12:31)
    • Pain? (Patient: No). (Video 12:31)
    • Symptoms of GBS: Preceding illness (fever, flu)? Shortness of breath? Tingling/numbness spreading upwards? (Patient: No to all). (Video 12:57-13:24)
    • Past medical history, medications.
    • Diet: Patient is vegan (Risk for B12 deficiency). (Video 13:38-13:46)
    • Recreational Drugs: Uses Nitrous Oxide (laughing gas) - approx. 100 canisters a week. (Significant finding). (Video 13:48-14:28)
  • Examination & Investigations: (Video 14:22)
    • General physical exam, vitals, neurological exam, cerebellar exam.
    • Romberg's Test: Positive (Patient sways/falls with eyes closed, stable with eyes open, indicates sensory ataxia). (Video 14:44-15:01 (cerebellar if eyes open, sensory if eyes closed)) Examiner states positive Romberg. (Video 15:01-15:16)
    • Proprioception: Impaired in lower limbs. (Examiner finding). (Video 15:16-15:29, 929s-15:34s)
    • Bloods: Vitamin B12, folate, TFTs, FBC. (Video 15:41-15:55)
    • Nerve Conduction Studies (NCS), MRI/CT if indicated. (Video 15:55-16:08)
  • Diagnosis & Explanation: Sensory Ataxia. (Video 16:08-16:15)
    • "Your symptoms suggest sensory ataxia. This means your body has difficulty sensing where your legs are, leading to balance problems, especially with eyes closed." (Video 16:15-16:27)
    • Cause: Likely Vitamin B12 deficiency (due to vegan diet) exacerbated by Nitrous Oxide use. (Video 16:20-16:47)
      • Nitrous oxide stops the body from using B12 properly, leading to deficiency even if blood levels look normal initially. (Video 17:04-17:36)
  • Treatment: (Video 17:36-17:42)
    1. Stop Nitrous Oxide use. (Video 17:42-17:48)
    2. Vitamin B12 (Hydroxycobalamin) injections: Every other day for first 2 weeks, then monitor. (Video 17:48-18:00)
    3. Referral to neurologist. Physiotherapy. (Video 18:00-18:07)
    4. Dietary advice, B12 supplementation if continuing vegan diet. (Video 18:07-18:19)
  • Warning Signs & Follow-up: If symptoms worsen, return. (Video 18:19-18:31)
  • Risk of Untreated B12 Deficiency: Subacute Combined Degeneration of the Spinal Cord (SCDC). Symptoms: loss of balance, numbness/tingling, leg weakness, gait disturbance. (Video 18:38-19:10)
  • Important Note: If both B12 and folate deficient, treat B12 first. ("B comes first"). (Video 18:45 (B12 deficiency) then 1157s-19:32s)

SCENARIO 3: KAWASAKI DISEASE VS. SCARLET FEVER (Video 19:52)

  • Differentiating Features:
    • Kawasaki: Cause unknown (abnormal immune response), <5 y/o, fever >5 days, UNILATERAL painful cervical lymphadenopathy, dry red cracked lips, strawberry tongue, rash (maculopapular), potential coronary artery aneurysms (heart problems). Treatment: IVIG and Aspirin. Not contagious. No improvement with antibiotics. (Video 19:57-20:12, 20:12-20:25, 20:25-20:34, 20:52-21:04, 21:17-21:23, 21:34-21:34 (rash example), 21:55-21:55 (rash example), 22:07-22:17, 22:28-22:53, 22:53-23:04)
    • Scarlet Fever: Group A Streptococcus infection, 5-15 y/o, high fever resolving in 3-5 days with antibiotics, BILATERAL less prominent lymphadenopathy, strawberry tongue, sandpaper-like rash. Highly contagious. Improves with antibiotics. (Video 20:12-20:25, 20:25-20:40, 20:40-20:46, 21:04s-21:11, 21:23-21:29, 21:40s-21:49, 22:17-22:23, 23:04s-23:10, 23:10-23:16, 23:16-23:22)
  • Patient Scenario (Suspected Kawasaki): (Video 23:41)
    • Child (3 y/o) with right-sided lymph node swelling, fever 38°C for 5 days. Initial assessment by nurse. Father present for consultation. (Video 23:41-24:01)
    • History from Father: Unwell 5 days, fever 38°C not fully responding to paracetamol, generalized rash, body looks swollen, eating/drinking okay. One-sided neck gland swelling. Recent contact with cousin who had chickenpox (distractor). (Video 24:01-24:43 (elaboration), 24:43-24:50, 24:50-25:22, 25:22-25:30, 25:30-25:36, 25:36-25:42, 25:42-25:50, 25:50-25:56)
    • Examination/Investigations: General physical, vitals, FBC, throat swab. (Pictures shown: strawberry tongue, body rash - consistent with Kawasaki). (Video 26:01-26:08, 26:08-26:14, 26:14-26:21, 26:21-26:28, 26:28-26:34)
    • Diagnosis: Kawasaki Disease. Key features: fever >5 days, rash, swollen glands (ipsilateral), red eyes (not explicitly stated by father but typical), swelling of hands/feet. (Video 26:34-26:54)
    • Management: Refer to hospital for IVIG and Aspirin. Monitor with echocardiogram. Possible referral to pediatric cardiologist. Supportive care (fluids, paracetamol). (Video 26:54-27:00, 27:00-27:05, 27:05-27:11, 27:11-27:16, 27:16-27:21, 27:21-27:32, 27:32-27:39, 27:39-27:46, 27:46-27:53)
    • Warning Signs: Difficulty breathing/swallowing, severe abdominal pain (splenic rupture risk, though more with EBV), worsening high fever, increasing neck swelling, persistent symptoms >3 weeks. (Video 28:00 (follow-up context) then 28:31s-28:51s)
    • Prognosis: Most children recover completely with proper treatment, without long-term issues. (Video 28:14-28:26, 28:26-28:32, 28:32-28:38)

SCENARIO 5: HEPATITIS B BLOOD TEST REPORT DISCUSSION (Video 36:43)

  • Situation: Patient (47 y/o) in medicine, to discuss blood test results. (Video 36:43-36:49, 36:49-36:56, 36:56-37:03)
  • Results Given: ALT slightly high, ALP normal, HBsAg Positive, Bilirubin normal. (Video 37:03-37:11, 37:11-37:17)
  • History Taking:
    • "Why were these blood tests done?" (Patient: "Feeling sick"). Elaborate (ODIPARA, vomiting, dehydration). (Video 37:17-37:22, 37:22-37:27, 37:27-37:33, 37:33-37:39, 37:39-37:46, 37:46-37:53)
    • Other symptoms: Tiredness. (Video 37:53-38:00)
    • Hepatitis-Specific Symptoms: Jaundice, stool/urine color changes, itching, weight/appetite loss, diarrhea, fever/flu-like symptoms. (Video 38:00-38:07, 38:07-38:14, 38:14-38:20, 38:20-38:25, 38:25-38:34)
    • Risk Factors for Hepatitis B: Sexual history (multiple partners, unprotected sex), recreational drug use (sharing needles), tattoos/piercings, blood transfusion (especially outside UK). (Video 38:34-38:39, 38:39-38:45, 38:45-38:51, 38:51-38:57) (Patient: Sexually active, recent breakup, partner cheated, no safe sex - Video 39:03 (quick past personal) then 39:32s-39:51s)
    • Lifestyle: Smoking, alcohol (important for liver health), diet. (Video 39:03-39:08, 39:08-39:13, 39:13-39:20, 39:20-39:27, 39:27-39:32)
  • Explaining Results: (Video 40:03)
    • "Your blood test shows you've tested positive for Hepatitis B (HBsAg positive), meaning you have an active infection." (Video 40:03-40:09, 4009s-40:15, 40:15-40:21, 40:21-40:26)
    • ALT raised: Suggests some liver inflammation. (Video 40:26-40:31)
    • ALP normal: No sign of liver obstruction or severe damage currently. (Video 40:31-40:37, 40:37-40:45)
  • Further Tests (to determine acute vs. chronic): (Video 40:50-40:56)
    • Anti-HBc IgM (recent infection) & IgG (chronic/past infection). (Video 41:03 (bit of idea) then 41:03s-41:09, 41:09-41:15, 41:15-41:22, 41:22-41:28, 41:28-41:33)
    • HBeAg (indicates high viral activity/infectivity). (Video 41:15-41:22, 41:22-41:28)
    • Hepatitis B DNA (viral load). (Video 41:28-41:33)
  • Management:
    • Acute Hepatitis B: Often clears within 6 months with immune system. Treatment: rest, avoid alcohol, healthy diet, avoid sharing personal items, notify close contacts for testing/vaccination. (Video 42:05-42:11, 42:11-42:17, 42:17-42:23, 42:23-42:29, 42:29-42:34)
    • Chronic Hepatitis B (IgG positive, >6 months): Lifelong follow-up, possible antiviral treatment, regular liver ultrasound. (Video 42:34-42:41, 42:41-42:47, 42:47-42:54)
    • HIV screening is mandatory. Hepatitis B is a notifiable disease. (Video 42:54-43:00, 43:00-43:07)
  • Follow-up & Warning Signs: Repeat tests in 6 months. Refer to liver specialist. (Video 43:07 (treatment) then 43:13s-43:20, 43:20-43:27)

SCENARIO 6: VULVAL CANCER (DIFFERENTIAL WITH LICHEN SCLEROSUS) (Video 43:38)

  • Situation: 65 y/o female in GP surgery with a lump in her private area. (Video 43:38-43:46, 43:46-43:53)
  • Lichen Sclerosus (LS) as a Predisposing Factor: LS is a premalignant condition for vulval cancer. (Video 43:53-44:04)
    • LS: Chronic inflammatory skin condition of genital skin, common in postmenopausal women. Benign but can transform. Autoimmune. Symptoms: pain (esp. during intercourse/urination), burning, narrowing of vaginal opening. Treatment: topical corticosteroids, emollients. (Video 44:04 (title card) then 44:31s-44:39, 44:39-44:46, 44:46-44:53, 44:53-45:00, 45:00-45:06, 45:20s-45:27, 45:27-45:33, 45:33-45:38, 45:38-45:44, 45:44-45:50, 45:50-45:55, 45:55-46:03, 46:36s-46:41, 46:59s-47:04, 47:04-47:10, 47:10-47:17, 47:17-47:22)
    • Vulval Cancer: Malignant tumor, common in elderly. Risk factors: LS, HPV infection, immunosuppression. Symptoms: often initially asymptomatic, later weight/appetite loss, anemia symptoms, lumps/ulcers (may bleed, cauliflower-like). Diagnosis: Biopsy. Treatment: MDT approach, urgent oncology referral. (Video 44:04 (title card) then 44:46s-44:53, 44:53-45:00, 45:00-45:06, 45:06-45:13, 45:13-45:20, 46:03s-46:09, 46:09-46:15, 46:15-46:21, 46:21-46:27, 46:41s-46:47, 46:47s-46:53, 46:53-46:59, 47:04s-47:10, 47:10-47:17, 47:22s-47:36)
  • History Taking (for Vulval Lump): (Video 47:43)
    • Elaborate on lump: Duration (few weeks), worsening/increasing size. (Video 47:43-47:49, 47:49-47:55, 47:55-48:00)
    • Associated symptoms: Itching, pain, bleeding, discharge, burning, UTI symptoms, rash. (Video 48:00-48:07)
    • Red Flags (Cancer Symptoms): Weight loss, appetite loss, tiredness, dizziness, palpitations, lumps elsewhere. (Video 48:07-48:12, 48:12-48:19, 48:19-48:25)
    • HPV vaccination status? Sexual history? (Video 48:25-48:31, 48:31-48:36) (Patient: husband died years ago - Video 48:36-48:44)
    • Gynae history (PPP + Pap smear). (Video 48:44-48:50, 48:50-48:56)
    • Past medical history (Lichen Sclerosus diagnosed years ago, treated with cream - Video 49:02 (lifestyle) then 49:08s-49:15, 49:15-49:22, 49:22-49:28, 49:28-49:35, 49:35-49:40, 49:40-49:46). Social history (support system).
  • Examination & ICE: Bloods, urine, swab. Idea, Concerns, Expectations (Patient likely worried about cancer given history of LS and new worsening lump). (Video 49:46-49:53, 49:53-50:00, 50:00-50:06, 50:06-50:11, 50:11-50:18, 50:18-50:24, 50:24-50:30, 50:30-50:38)

SCENARIO 8: GLANDULAR FEVER (INFECTIOUS MONONUCLEOSIS) (Video 57:54)

  • Situation: FY2 in GP. Patient (22 y/o) presents with sore throat. 10 days ago, had fever, no cough. Exam then: painful cervical lymphadenopathy. Prescribed Penicillin. Now, sore throat not better. (Video 57:54-58:00, 58:00-58:07, 58:07-58:13, 58:13-58:18, 58:18-58:23, 58:23-58:31, 58:31-58:36, 58:36-58:41) (Centor criteria elements mentioned).
  • History Taking:
    • Elaborate on sore throat (worsening/improving, impact of antibiotics). (Video 58:59-59:05, 59:05-59:12, 59:12-59:17, 59:17-59:23)
    • Able to eat/drink/swallow? (Video 59:17-59:23, 59:23-59:30)
    • Associated symptoms: Rash? Fatigue? Abdominal pain (for splenomegaly/rupture risk). (Video 59:30-59:36, 59:36-59:43, 59:43-59:50, 59:50-59:58, 59:58-1:00:04, 1:00:04-1:00:11, 1:00:11-1:00:17)
    • Sexual history (EBV is "kissing disease"). (Video 1:00:17-1:00:37, 1:00:37-1:00:43)
    • Contact with similar illness? Physical activity (e.g., rugby player - important for splenic rupture risk). (Video 1:00:43-1:00:50, 1:00:50-1:00:57, 1:00:57-1:01:04)
  • Examination & Investigations: Vitals, ENT (tonsillar exudates, temp 38.5°C - examiner findings), abdominal exam (for spleen), lymph nodes. FBC, ESR, CRP, LFTs, throat swab, Monospot test (for EBV). (Video 1:01:04-1:01:09, 1:01:09-1:01:17, 1:01:17-1:01:24, 1:01:24-1:01:31, 1:01:31-1:01:37, 1:01:37-1:01:43, 1:01:43-1:01:50, 1:01:50-1:01:56)
  • Diagnosis & Explanation: Glandular Fever (Infectious Mononucleosis) caused by Epstein-Barr Virus (EBV). (Video 1:01:56-1:02:03, 1:02:03-1:02:09) Common, sore throat doesn't respond to antibiotics. Swollen nodes, fatigue, possible enlarged spleen. (Video 1:02:09-1:02:16, 1:02:16-1:02:23, 1:02:23-1:02:30)
  • Management: (Video 1:02:30)
    • Self-limiting (recovery can take a few weeks). (Video 1:02:30-1:02:35, 1:02:35-1:02:40)
    • No need for antibiotics (viral). (Video 1:02:40-1:02:48)
    • Symptomatic relief: Rest, avoid overexertion, paracetamol, hydration, salt water gargles. (Video 1:02:40-1:02:48, 1:02:48-1:02:53)
    • Crucial Advice: Avoid contact sports for 4-6 weeks due to risk of splenic rupture. (Video 1:02:53-1:03:00, 1:03:00-1:03:07) (Patient is a rugby player - Video 1:04:33 (in expectation))
    • Avoid further antibiotics unless secondary bacterial infection confirmed. (Video 1:04:07-1:03:13)
