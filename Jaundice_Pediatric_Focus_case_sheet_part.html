Elaboration of Presenting Complaint: Jaundice (with Pediatric Focus)

  • S: Site (Distribution of Jaundice)
    • Where was the jaundice first noticed? (e.g., eyes, face, chest)
    • How has it spread? (Progression is often cephalocaudal - head to toe)
    • In neonates, assessing the extent of jaundice (e.g., to the chest, abdomen, or soles of feet using Kramer's rule) can give a rough estimate of bilirubin levels, though biochemical testing is definitive.
    • Is it primarily skin, sclera (eyes), or also mucous membranes (e.g., inside the mouth)?
  • O: Onset
    • When was the jaundice first noticed by the patient or caregiver?
      • Neonatal Jaundice:
        • Physiological:
        • Typically appears after 24 hours of life,
        •  peaks around day 3-5, and
        • resolves within 1-2 weeks.

        • Pathological:
        • May appear within the first 24 hours of life,
        •  rise rapidly,
        • persist longer, or be
        • associated with other concerning signs.
      • Older Children/Adults: Acute (e.g., viral hepatitis, drug-induced, gallstones) vs. Gradual/Insidious (e.g., chronic liver disease, malignancy).
    • Was the onset sudden or gradual?
  • D: Duration
    • How long has the jaundice been present?
    • Is it constant, or does it fluctuate?
    • Physiological jaundice in neonates is transient.
    • Prolonged neonatal jaundice (beyond 2 weeks in term infants, 3 weeks in preterm) requires investigation.
    • In older children, duration helps differentiate acute from chronic conditions.
  • A: Aggravating / Relieving Factors
    • Are there any factors that make the jaundice appear worse? (e.g., illness, fasting, certain
    • medications - though this is more for underlying cause than jaundice itself)
    • herbal  medications
    • Are there any factors that make it seem better? (e.g., exposure to sunlight - historically and anecdotally for mild neonatal jaundice, but phototherapy is the medical treatment).
    • Breastfeeding jaundice: May be exacerbated by suboptimal milk intake in the first week.
    • Breast milk jaundice: Can persist for several weeks but the infant is otherwise well; sometimes temporarily interrupting breastfeeding can lower bilirubin (done under medical guidance).
    • Relieving factors for jaundice itself are usually related to treatment of the underlying cause rather than patient-initiated actions.
  • C: Character (of Jaundice and Associated Stool/Urine)
    • Color of Jaundice:
      • Yellowish (common)
      • Greenish tint (may suggest prolonged or severe cholestasis with biliverdin accumulation)
    • Urine Color:
      • Dark urine (tea-colored or cola-colored): Suggests conjugated hyperbilirubinemia (bilirubinuria).
      • Normal colored urine: May suggest unconjugated hyperbilirubinemia (as unconjugated bilirubin is not water-soluble and not excreted in urine).
    • Stool Color:
      • Pale or clay-colored (acholic) stools: Strongly suggests biliary obstruction (obstructive jaundice), as bilirubin is not reaching the gut.
        • A critical sign in neonates, may indicate biliary atresia.
      • Normal colored stools.
      • Dark stools (melena) if there's associated GI bleeding contributing to liver issues.
  • R: Radiation
  • A: Associated Symptoms
    • General:
      • Fever, chills (suggests infection, e.g., hepatitis, cholangitis).
      • Malaise, fatigue, anorexia (common with liver disease, infections).
      • Weight loss (concerning for malignancy, chronic infection, or severe chronic liver disease).
        • Poor weight gain or failure to thrive in infants/children with jaundice is a significant concern.
      • Pruritus (itching): Common with cholestasis due to bile salt deposition in the skin.
    • Gastrointestinal:
      • Abdominal pain (RUQ pain might suggest gallstones, hepatitis, cholangitis).
      • Nausea, vomiting (as seen in the previous example, can be associated with many causes of jaundice like hepatitis).
      • Abdominal distension (ascites in chronic liver disease).
    • Neonatal/Infant Specific:
      • Poor feeding, lethargy, irritability (can be signs of sepsis, severe hyperbilirubinemia/kernicterus, or metabolic disease).
      • High-pitched cry, abnormal muscle tone, seizures (concerning for kernicterus).
      • Vomiting (can be seen with sepsis, metabolic disorders, galactosemia).
      • Hepatosplenomegaly (enlarged liver/spleen on examination).
    • Other systems:
      • Easy bruising or bleeding (due to impaired synthesis of clotting factors by the liver).
      • Joint pain (arthralgia), muscle pain (myalgia) (can be seen in viral hepatitis).
      • Neurological changes (confusion, drowsiness, asterixis – signs of hepatic encephalopathy in severe liver disease).
  • P: Progression (Often covered by Onset, Duration, and Pattern, but can be a distinct inquiry about the course of the illness)
    • Is the jaundice getting progressively deeper yellow?
    • Are associated symptoms worsening or improving?
    • Tracking bilirubin levels (if known) provides objective measure of progression in neonates.
    • This element often overlaps with Pattern/Timing.
  • P: Pattern (Timing / Evolution)
    • How has the jaundice changed since it was first noticed? (Worsening, improving, stable, fluctuating)
    • Physiological neonatal jaundice follows a predictable pattern of rise and fall. Pathological jaundice may show a rapid rise or prolonged course.
    • Any previous episodes of jaundice? or siblings?
    •  Gilbert's or
    • Crigler-Najjar, or
    • hemolytic diseases).
  • E: Effect on life
    • Impact on feeding, activity levels, growth, and development in children.
    • Parental anxiety and concern, especially with neonatal jaundice.
    • For older individuals: ability to work, engage in daily activities, appetite, sleep.
    • This section would be filled with patient/caregiver specific information.
  • S: Severity / Scale
    • Often gauged by the level of bilirubin in blood tests.
    • Clinically, the extent of skin discoloration (cephalocaudal progression).
    • Severity of associated symptoms (e.g., degree of pruritus, presence of hepatic encephalopathy, degree of dehydration if vomiting is present).
    • In neonates, risk of kernicterus is a key aspect of severity based on bilirubin levels and risk factors (e.g., prematurity, sepsis, acidosis, hypoalbuminemia). Nomograms (e.g., Bhutani nomogram) are used to assess risk.
    • A numerical scale for jaundice itself isn't typically used; severity is a clinical and biochemical assessment.

Additional Contextual Information to Gather (Beyond SODA CRAPPERS directly for jaundice):

  • Birth History (Pediatric): Gestational age, birth weight, mode of delivery, any birth trauma (e.g., bruising, cephalohematoma which can increase bilirubin), maternal blood group and Rh status, history of maternal infections.
  • Feeding History (Pediatric): Breastfed or formula-fed? Adequacy of feeding, frequency, volume. Any difficulties with feeding.
  • Family History: Liver disease, hemolytic anemias (e.g., sickle cell, spherocytosis), Gilbert's syndrome, Crigler-Najjar syndrome, gallstones, metabolic disorders.
    History of jaundice or liver disease in siblings.
  • Medication History: Any new medications, herbal remedies, supplements (potential for drug-induced liver injury).
    Maternal medications during pregnancy or breastfeeding.
  • Travel History: To areas endemic for hepatitis viruses.
  • Exposure History: Blood transfusions, IV drug use, high-risk sexual contact (risk factors for viral hepatitis). Contaminated food/water.
  • Alcohol History (for older patients).
  • Vaccination History (Pediatric/Adult): Hepatitis A and B vaccinations.
