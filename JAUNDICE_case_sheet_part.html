• S: Site (Location of jaundice)
    • Typically observed in skin and sclera (whites of the eyes).
    • In severe cases, it can affect the entire body, including mucous membranes (e.g., inside the mouth).
  • O: Onset (Rapid or chronic)
    • Sudden (acute) onset may suggest:
      • Acute liver injury (e.g., viral hepatitis).
      • Bile duct obstruction (e.g., gallstones).
      • Hemolysis (e.g., hemolytic anemia).
    • Gradual (chronic) onset may indicate:
      • Chronic liver disease (e.g., cirrhosis).
      • Slow-growing obstructions (e.g., tumors).
  • D: Duration (How long the jaundice has been present)
    • Acute: Less than 6 weeks, often linked to:
      • Infections (e.g., viral hepatitis).
      • Acute obstructions (e.g., gallstones).
    • Chronic: More than 6 weeks, typically associated with:
      • Chronic liver diseases (e.g., cirrhosis).
      • Persistent obstructions (e.g., bile duct strictures).
  • A: Aggravating factors (What makes the jaundice worse)
    • Alcohol consumption (e.g., in alcoholic liver disease).
    • Fatty meals (e.g., in gallstone disease, triggering biliary colic or obstruction).
    • Certain medications (e.g., paracetamol, antibiotics in drug-induced liver injury).
    • Infections or inflammation (e.g., worsening viral hepatitis or cholangitis).
  • C: Character/Type (Description of the jaundice)
    • Unconjugated (pre-hepatic):
      • Often due to hemolysis (e.g., hemolytic anemia).
      • Genetic disorders (e.g., Gilbert's, G6PD deficiency, thalassemia, sickle-cell anemia, spherocytosis).
    • Conjugated (hepatic or post-hepatic):
      • Due to liver dysfunction (e.g., cirrhosis, hepatitis, Gilbert's syndrome, Crigler-Najjar syndrome, alcoholic liver disease, drug-induced hepatitis).
      • Bile duct obstruction (e.g., gallstones, tumors, pancreatic pseudocysts, Mirizzi syndrome, primary biliary cholangitis).
  • R: Radiation (What improves jaundice)
    • Treatment of underlying cause:
      • Antivirals (e.g., for hepatitis).
      • Surgery (e.g., for gallstones).
    • Avoiding alcohol or offending medications.
    • Supportive care (e.g., hydration, nutrition).
  • A: Associated symptoms (Other symptoms accompanying the jaundice)
    • Abdominal pain
      • gallstones,
      • pancreatitis
      • hepatitis
    • Fever (e.g., in infections like hepatitis, cholangitis).
    • Dark urine and pale stools (e.g., in obstructive jaundice).
    • Itching (pruritus, common in cholestatic conditions).
    • Nausea and vomiting.
    • Fatigue or weakness.
    • Loss of appetite.
  • G: Genetic Causes (Hereditary Hyperbilirubinemia)
    • Gilbert's (mild, ~5% population, stress-induced)
    • Crigler-Najjar syndrome type I, II (severe, complete UGT deficiency)
    • Dubin-Johnson syndrome (conjugated hyperbilirubinemia)
    • Rotor syndrome (mixed hyperbilirubinemia)
    • Lucey-Driscoll syndrome (rare, severe)
    • Heme Oxygenase-1 deficiency (rare, severe)
  • N: Neonatal Jaundice (Newborn Jaundice)
    • Physiologic (75% of cases):
      • Appears 24-48 hours after birth
      • Peaks at 48-96 hours
      • Resolves by 2-3 weeks
      • Due to immature UGT enzyme activity
    • Pathologic (requires investigation):
      • Blood group incompatibility (ABO/Rh)
      • G6PD deficiency (X-linked, oxidative stress)
      • Pyruvate kinase deficiency
      • Hereditary spherocytosis
      • Hereditary elliptocytosis
      • Sepsis
      • Polycythemia
      • Crigler-Najjar syndrome type I/II
  • P: Progression (How the jaundice has changed)
    • Worsening, improving, or remaining stable in intensity or spread.
    • Changes in associated symptoms (e.g., increasing pain or itching).
  • P: Pattern (Characteristics of jaundice)
    • Constant (persistent yellowing, e.g., in chronic liver disease).
    • Intermittent (e.g., in Gilbert's syndrome or intermittent obstructions).
    • Associated with other symptoms (e.g., pain, fever) or isolated.
  • E: Effect on life (Impact on daily activities and quality of life)
    • Fatigue or weakness affecting work or daily tasks.
    • Social or emotional distress due to visible yellowing of skin or eyes.
    • Sleep disturbances due to itching or abdominal pain.
    • Reduced quality of life from associated symptoms like nausea or loss of appetite.
  • R: Red Flags 
    • Cancer sx
      • Weight loss (e.g., in malignancies like pancreatic or liver cancer).
  • S: Scale of zero to 10 (Severity of jaundice or associated symptoms)
    • Rate the intensity of jaundice (e.g., degree of yellowing) or the impact of associated symptoms (e.g., pain, itching) on a scale from 0 (no symptoms) to 10 (severe symptoms).
