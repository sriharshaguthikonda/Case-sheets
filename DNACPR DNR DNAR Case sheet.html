EXPAND: DNACPR DISCUSSION - OSCE PROTOCOL (SPIKES FRAMEWORK)

(Scenario: A junior doctor discusses future care planning, specifically a DNACPR order, with the daughter of an elderly patient with multiple comorbidities.)

S: Setting up the Interview

  • Introduce yourself & your role"Hi there, my name's Leah, I'm one of the junior doctors working on Ward 36." (0:10)
  • Confirm relative's identity & relationship"Could I just check your name and ask how you're related to Mrs. Brown, please?" (0:14)
  • Confirm patient's details (Safety Check)"Could I just double-check, do you know Mrs. Brown's date of birth for me?" (0:23)

P: Assessing Perception

  • Explore their understanding of the patient's current situation (ICE)"Can I just check what you understand about your mum's condition so far?" (0:42)
  • Summarise their understanding to establish a shared baseline"So as you said, your mum's come into hospital with a chest infection this time... it seems like she is getting better..." (1:56)

I: Obtaining an Invitation

  • Explore their expectation for the meeting"Were you aware about what we were going to be talking about today?" (1:41)
  • Signpost the difficult nature of the conversation"Sometimes when we're talking about planning for the future... we also think about what might happen if your mum was to come back into hospital... if she was much more unwell..." (2:13)
  • Seek permission to proceed with the discussion"Are you happy if we talk a bit more about that now?" (2:57)

K: Giving Knowledge & Information

  • Introduce the specific term "DNACPR""Sometimes in the context of that planning, we think about something called a DNACPR. Have you ever heard of that before?" (2:40)
  • Explain the reality and low success rate of CPR in this specific patient context"Sometimes CPR is not successful, as it often is shown on the television... often it's not successful in people who have got lots of things wrong with them..." (4:51)
  • Describe the burdensome nature of CPR"CPR is quite an invasive process... pressing on the chest very hard which can cause damage to the chest..." (5:11)
  • Provide a clear medical opinion based on the patient's history"So what we would think about in your mum's circumstances... we would worry is that if we were to try and do CPR, that it wouldn't be effective and that it would actually end up causing more harm than good and probably wouldn't work." (5:28)

E: Addressing Emotions with Empathic Responses

  • Acknowledge and validate their concerns ("giving up")"It just sounds as if you're giving up... surely it's worth trying?" (6:33)
  • Provide direct, empathetic reassurance"I appreciate what you're saying and I just want to reassure you... that's certainly not the case." (6:58)
  • Acknowledge the difficulty of the conversation"I appreciate that and I'm really sorry if this has come as a bit of a shock to you to have this discussion today." (8:47)

S: Strategy and Summary

  • Clarify that a DNACPR order does NOT stop other treatments"In terms of treatments... none of the treatments that we would offer her change... We would still do all of the same." (7:07)
  • Clarify the medical team's responsibility for the decision"...to take the weight off your shoulders, it's not a decision that you need to make... it's a decision that the medical team would make..." (9:09)
  • Check understanding by asking them to summarise (Teach-back)"Is it okay if I just check what you've understood from the discussion? Would you be able to summarise it back to me?" (9:53)
  • Assess if they have any further questions"Are you comfortable that you've had enough information from this discussion?" (11:40)
  • Offer additional resources"I'll give you a leaflet when you leave just so that you can take that home, there's a little bit more information..." (12:04)
  • Close the consultation formally"Thank you for taking the time to talk to me." (12:16)
