  1. SITE (S):
    1. Can you point with one finger to where the pain is worst in your lower back?
    2. Is the pain central (over the spine), to one side (paraspinal â€“ right or left), or across the entire lower back?
    3. Is it in the lumbar region, sacral region, or sacroiliac (SI) joint area?
    4. Is it a superficial or deep pain?
  2. ONSET (O):
    1. When did the lower back pain first start? (Specific date/time if possible)
    2. Was the onset sudden (e.g., after lifting something heavy, a twist, a fall) or gradual (insidious)?
    3. If sudden, describe the exact activity or event that triggered it.
    4. Was it associated with any unusual physical exertion or new activity?
    5. Any preceding illness or fever?
  3. DURATION (D):
    1. How long have you been experiencing this lower back pain?
      1. Acute: < 6 weeks
      2. Subacute: 6-12 weeks
      3. Chronic: > 12 weeks
    2. Is the pain constant or intermittent?
    3. If intermittent, how long do episodes last, and how often do they occur?
  4. AGGRAVATING/RELIEVING FACTORS (A/R):
    1. Aggravating Factors: What makes the pain worse?
      1. Movements: Bending forward (flexion), bending backward (extension), twisting, lifting?
      2. Positions: Sitting for long periods, standing for long periods, lying down?
      3. Activities: Walking, running, driving?
      4. Valsalva maneuvers: Coughing, sneezing, straining at stool? (May suggest disc involvement)
      5. Time of day (e.g., worse in the morning, improves with movement)?
    2. Relieving Factors: What makes the pain better?
      1. Rest? Or gentle activity?
      2. Specific positions (e.g., lying flat, lying with knees bent, sitting, standing, walking)?
      3. Pain medications (OTC or prescribed)? Which ones and effectiveness?
      4. Heat or ice application?
      5. Physiotherapy or specific exercises?
  5. CHARACTER/TYPE (C):
    1. How would you describe the pain in your back?
      1. Dull, aching, sore, stiff?
      2. Sharp, stabbing, shooting, electrical? (Often suggests nerve involvement)
      3. Burning?
      4. Throbbing?
  6. RADIATION (R):
    1. Does the pain radiate or travel anywhere else?
      1. Into the buttocks (one or both sides)?
      2. Down the leg(s)? (Sciatica) - Specify path: posterior thigh, lateral calf, into the foot/toes?
      3. To the groin or anterior thigh?
      4. Is it above or below the knee?
  7. ASSOCIATED SYMPTOMS (A):
    1. Neurological symptoms in the legs/feet:
      1. Numbness or tingling (paresthesia)? (Specify location)
      2. Weakness in the legs or feet (e.g., foot drop, difficulty standing on toes/heels)?
      3. Changes in reflexes (patient might not know, but ask about perceived changes)?
    2. Bladder dysfunction:
      1. Urinary retention (difficulty starting or inability to pass urine)?
      2. Urinary incontinence (loss of control)?
      3. Altered sensation during urination?
    3. Bowel dysfunction:
      1. Fecal incontinence (loss of control)?
      2. Constipation (new or worsening)?
    4. Saddle anesthesia (numbness or altered sensation in the genital/perineal area, inner thighs)?
    5. Stiffness in the back, especially in the morning? How long does it last?
    6. Reduced range of motion in the back?
    7. Pain that wakes you up at night?
    8. Fever, chills, unexplained weight loss, night sweats? (Systemic red flags)
    9. History of cancer?
    10. History of recent infection (e.g., UTI, skin infection)?
    11. History of osteoporosis or steroid use?
    12. Pain in other joints or systemic inflammatory symptoms?
  8. PROGRESSION (P):
    1. How has the pain and any associated symptoms (especially leg pain/numbness/weakness) changed since onset? (Worsening, improving, stable?)
    2. Has the location or nature of the pain changed?
  9. PATTERN (P):
    1. Is the pain worse at certain times of day (e.g., morning stiffness improving with activity suggests inflammatory, pain worsening through the day suggests mechanical)?
    2. Is it related to specific activities or postures?
  10. EFFECT ON LIFE (E):
    1. How is the back pain affecting your ability to perform daily activities (sitting, standing, walking, dressing, personal care)?
    2. Impact on work (absenteeism, modified duties)?
    3. Impact on sleep, mood, social activities, hobbies?
  11. RED FLAGS (R) (for serious spinal pathology):
    1. Cauda Equina Syndrome (CES):
      1. Bilateral leg pain/sciatica.
      2. Saddle anesthesia/paresthesia.
      3. Recent onset bladder dysfunction (retention or incontinence).
      4. Recent onset bowel dysfunction (fecal incontinence, loss of sensation).
      5. Severe or progressive neurological deficit in lower extremities (e.g., major motor weakness).
      6. Lax anal sphincter.
    2. Spinal Fracture:
      1. History of significant trauma (or minor trauma in osteoporotic patients).
      2. Sudden onset severe pain, often localized and tender.
      3. Use of corticosteroids.
      4. Age > 50.
    3. Cancer/Malignancy:
      1. Age > 50 with new onset pain, or < 20.
      2. History of cancer.
      3. Unexplained weight loss, fever, night sweats.
      4. Pain that is constant, progressive, non-mechanical (present at rest, night pain disturbing sleep).
      5. Failure to improve after 4-6 weeks of conservative therapy.
    4. Infection (e.g., discitis, osteomyelitis, epidural abscess):
      1. Fever, chills.
      2. Recent bacterial infection (e.g., UTI, skin).
      3. IV drug use.
      4. Immunosuppression (e.g., diabetes, steroids, HIV).
      5. Pain worse at rest, night pain.
      6. Localized spinal tenderness.
    5. Significant or progressive motor weakness, numbness.
  12. SCALE (S) (Severity):
    1. On a scale of 0 to 10:
      1. Back pain right now? At its worst? On average?
      2. If leg pain present, rate leg pain separately right now? At its worst? On average?
