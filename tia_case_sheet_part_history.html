TRANSIENT ISCHAEMIC ATTACK (TIA) HISTORY

1ST COMPLAINT: FOCAL NEUROLOGICAL DEFICIT

  1. S: Site "Which side of the body was affected? Face, arm, leg?"
  2. O: Onset "Exactly what time did the symptoms begin? Were you able to note the last moment she was normal (Last Known Well)?"
  3. D: Duration "How long did each symptom last before resolving?"
  4. A / R: Aggravating & Relieving factors "Did anything bring the episode on or make it settle more quickly?"
  5. C: Character "Can you describe how her speech sounded? Was the weakness complete or partial?"
  6. R: Radiation "Did the weakness spread to other limbs?"
  7. A: Associated symptoms
    • Visual: transient loss / diplopia
    • Balance: vertigo, ataxia, falls
    • Sensory: numbness / tingling
    • Headache (thunderclap?)
    • Loss of consciousness / seizure‑like activity
    • Difficulty swallowing / aspiration
  8. P: Progression & Pattern "Did the symptoms come on suddenly and improve steadily, or fluctuate?"
  9. E: Effect on life "Was she able to walk, drink, speak on the phone?"
  10. R: Red flags "Any severe headache, chest pain, new confusion, or persistent deficit afterwards?"
  11. S: Severity / Scale "If 0 is normal and 10 is the worst you have ever seen her, where would you rate the episode?"

2ND SECTION: PAST MEDICAL HISTORY & RISK FACTORS

  • Hypertension – duration, recent BP readings, clinic vs home
  • Hyperlipidaemia – previous cholesterol results, treatment
  • Diabetes – diagnosis, HbA1c, complications
  • Cardiac disease – Atrial Fibrillation, ischaemic heart disease, heart failure, valvular disease
  • Previous stroke / TIA episodes
  • Peripheral vascular disease / carotid disease
  • Recent head or neck trauma (carotid/vertebral dissection)

3RD SECTION: MEDICATIONS & ALLERGIES

  • Antihypertensives (e.g., Amlodipine), compliance, timing of last dose
  • Antiplatelets / Anticoagulants – aspirin, clopidogrel, DOAC, warfarin
  • Statins, diabetic medications
  • Over‑the‑counter & herbal remedies (e.g., NSAIDs, supplements)
  • Known drug or food allergies & documented reactions

4TH SECTION: SOCIAL /LIFESTYLE HISTORY

  • Smoking – current, past, pack‑years
  • Alcohol intake – units per week, recent binge?
  • Illicit drug use (e.g., cocaine)
  • Dietary habits, salt & fat intake, caffeine
  • Physical activity level
  • Driving status / occupational hazards
  • Support network, living situation, stressors

5TH SECTION: FAMILY HISTORY

  • Stroke / TIA – age of onset in first‑degree relatives
  • Ischaemic heart disease, sudden cardiac death
  • Diabetes, hypercholesterolaemia
  • Bleeding or clotting disorders

6TH SECTION: SYSTEMS REVIEW

  • Chest pain, palpitations, shortness of breath, orthopnoea
  • Cough, wheeze, haemoptysis
  • Dysphagia, nausea/vomiting
  • Recent urinary changes
  • Rash, bruising
  • Recent cognitive changes, mood

7TH SECTION: ICE (IDEAS, CONCERNS, EXPECTATIONS)

  • Ideas – "What do you think happened to your wife?"
  • Concerns – fear of future stroke, disability, mortality
  • Expectations – admission, scans, medications, follow‑up plan

8TH SECTION: RED FLAGS FOR STROKE / MIMICS

  • Persistent deficit beyond 24 h
  • Thunderclap headache (subarachnoid haemorrhage)
  • Severe hypertension > 200/120 mmHg with symptoms
  • Altered consciousness, seizure, prolonged confusion
  • Worsening weakness after initial recovery

This history template follows the SODA‑CRAPPERS framework and incorporates multi‑system cues for a Transient Ischaemic Attack presentation.

MANAGEMENT PLAN – Transient Ischaemic Attack (TIA)

1. Urgency / Triage

  • High‑risk TIA (symptoms <24 h ago, crescendo events, carotid territory, or ABCD2 ≥ 4): Admit under Stroke team immediately
  • Lower‑risk TIA (symptom onset >24 h, ABCD2 < 4): Refer to TIA Clinic within 24 h

2. Immediate ED Measures

  • Give Aspirin 300 mg PO stat (unless anticoagulated or contraindicated)
  • Capillary blood glucose – exclude hypoglycaemia
  • Blood pressure control: keep <185/110; restart / optimise antihypertensives once stable
  • Maintain SpO₂ > 94 %; airway & swallow screen

3. Urgent Investigations (within 24 h)

  • Non‑contrast CT brain (or MRI DWI) to exclude haemorrhage & confirm ischaemia
  • Carotid Doppler / CTA neck vessels
  • 12‑lead ECG ± 24 h Holter for paroxysmal AF
  • Bloods: FBC, U&E, LFT, Lipid profile, HbA1c, glucose, ESR/CRP if vasculitis suspected

4. Secondary Prevention

  • Dual antiplatelet therapy: Aspirin 75 mg + Clopidogrel 75 mg for 21 days then Clopidogrel monotherapy (per 2023 RCP guidance)
  • High‑intensity statin: Atorvastatin 80 mg nocte
  • Antihypertensives: optimise to target <130/80 mmHg
  • Anticoagulation (DOAC) if AF or cardio‑embolic source confirmed

5. Risk‑Factor Modification & Education

  • Smoking cessation – NRT / referral to Stop Smoking Service
  • Alcohol: ≤14 units/week; brief intervention
  • Diet: Mediterranean / DASH, reduce salt & saturated fat
  • DVLA: No driving for 1 month post‑TIA
  • Provide written TIA leaflet; teach FAST, red‑flag return advice

6. Referrals / MDT

  • Stroke physician / Specialist TIA clinic
  • Vascular surgery – carotid endarterectomy if stenosis > 50 % (NICE NG128)
  • Physio & OT – assess mobility & ADLs
  • Speech & Language Therapy – swallow & communication
  • Psychology / counselling for post‑TIA anxiety

7. Safety‑Netting & Follow‑Up

  • Seek emergency help (999) if weakness, speech problems, vision loss recur
  • Stroke nurse review before discharge; GP letter same day
  • Clinic review at 1 week to confirm results & tweak therapy

8. Documentation & Capacity

  • Record onset time, ABCD2 score, NIHSS if any deficit
  • Document informed consent for scans & medications
  • Offer relative presence & involve in discussions
