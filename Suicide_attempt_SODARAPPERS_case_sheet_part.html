Suicide Risk Assessment (Post-Attempt/Poisoning) - SODARAPPERS Framework

  • S: Site/Setting of attempt/ideation
    • Where and when did the attempt/ideation occur?
    • Who was present?
    • Accessibility of the location.
  • O: Onset of current suicidal crisis/ideation
    • When did these specific thoughts/feelings begin?
    • Was the onset sudden or gradual?
    • Were there any identifiable triggers for the current crisis?
  • D: Duration of ideation/symptoms leading to attempt
    • How long has the patient been experiencing suicidal thoughts or feelings that led to the attempt/current crisis? (Hours, days, weeks, months)
    • Frequency and intensity of suicidal thoughts.
  • A: Aggravating/Relieving factors
    • What makes the suicidal thoughts/feelings worse? (e.g., specific situations, thoughts, substance use, interpersonal conflict)
    • What makes them better, even temporarily? (e.g., specific activities, people, coping strategies)
  • R: Radiation (Impact on other areas of life)
    • How has this suicidal crisis/attempt impacted:
      • Relationships (family, friends, partner)?
      • Work/School performance and attendance?
      • Self-care (hygiene, eating, sleeping)?
      • Engagement in previously enjoyed activities?
  • A: Associated Symptoms
    • Mood: Depression (low mood, sadness, tearfulness), hopelessness, anhedonia (loss of interest/pleasure), irritability, anxiety, panic attacks, agitation, anger.
    • Psychotic Symptoms: Hallucinations (especially command hallucinations to self-harm), delusions (e.g., guilt, persecution, nihilism).
    • Substance Use: Current intoxication or withdrawal symptoms from alcohol or drugs. History of substance abuse.
    • Cognitive Symptoms: Impaired concentration, indecisiveness, feelings of worthlessness, excessive guilt.
    • Physical Symptoms: Chronic pain, insomnia/hypersomnia, changes in appetite/weight.
    • Details of Attempt (if applicable):
      • Nature of poisoning/method used (e.g., type of substance, amount ingested/used; nature of self-injury).
      • Injuries sustained as a result of the attempt.
      • Level of consciousness when found/arrived at hospital.
      • Any medical interventions received prior to or upon arrival.
  • P: Previous attempts/history of self-harm
    • Number of previous suicide attempts.
    • Nature/method of previous attempts.
    • Perceived lethality of previous attempts.
    • Medical severity/treatment received for previous attempts.
    • Timing of previous attempts (how recent was the last one?).
    • History of non-suicidal self-injury (NSSI)? (Nature, frequency, triggers).
  • P: Plan (Current Suicidal Plan & Intent)
    • Plan Specificity: Does the patient have a specific plan now? (Method, timing, location).
    • Lethality of Plan: Patient's perception of the lethality of their current/recent plan. Clinician's assessment of lethality.
    • Access to Means: Does the patient have access to the means described in their plan? (e.g., firearms, medications, sharp objects).
    • Preparedness: Any steps taken to prepare for suicide? (e.g., writing a note, giving away possessions, researching methods).
    • Intent: Level of determination to die. To what extent does the patient currently wish to die versus wish to escape an unbearable situation? Ambivalence?
  • E: Exploration of the attempt/ideation (Patient's Perspective)
    • Precipitants: What events, thoughts, or feelings immediately preceded the attempt or the current severe ideation?
    • Perceived Lethality by Patient: How lethal did the patient believe their actions/plan to be?
    • Wish to Die vs. Wish to Escape: Explore the primary motivation behind the suicidal act/thoughts.
    • Impulsivity vs. Premeditation: Was the act impulsive or planned? How much thought went into it?
    • Precautions Against Discovery: Did the patient take steps to avoid being discovered?
    • Actions to Seek Help: Did the patient seek help or tell anyone before or after the attempt/during the crisis?
    • Patient's Thoughts/Feelings about Surviving/Current Crisis: Reaction to surviving (if attempt made). Current feelings about being alive. Hope for the future?
    • Expectations: What did the patient expect to happen as a result of their actions?
  • R: Risk Factors
    • Psychiatric Illness: Current diagnosis (e.g., Major Depressive Disorder, Bipolar Disorder, Schizophrenia, PTSD, Personality Disorder - esp. Borderline PD, Substance Use Disorder). Severity of symptoms.
    • History: Family history of suicide or mental illness. Personal history of trauma, abuse, or neglect.
    • Social/Environmental: Recent stressors (e.g., interpersonal loss, financial difficulties, legal problems, job loss, academic failure). Social isolation, lack of support system. Access to lethal means.
    • Demographic: (Consider age, gender, ethnicity, sexual orientation in context of specific population risks, but avoid stereotyping).
    • Behavioral: Impulsivity, aggression, agitation, severe anxiety, panic attacks. Recent discharge from psychiatric facility.
    • Anniversaries: Significant dates related to losses or past traumas.
  • S: Strengths/Protective Factors & Safety Plan
    • Internal Strengths: Coping skills, problem-solving abilities, reasons for living (identified by patient), religious/spiritual beliefs that discourage suicide (if any).
    • External Supports: Positive relationships with family, friends, partner. Responsibility to others (e.g., children, pets). Engagement with mental health services/therapist. Safe home environment.
    • Future Orientation: Any hope for the future, plans, or goals.
    • Safety Plan (Collaboratively Developed):
      • Warning signs of escalating crisis.
      • Internal coping strategies.
      • People to contact for distraction/support (friends, family).
      • Professional help to contact (therapist, crisis line, emergency services).
      • Steps to make the environment safe (e.g., removing/securing means).
