  • S: Site - Origin of sensation "Where do you feel the shortness of breath? Is it in your chest or throat?" "Does it feel like it’s coming from deep in your lungs or higher up?"
  • O: Onset - Start time, sudden/gradual, triggers "When did you first notice the shortness of breath?" "Did it start suddenly, like after an event, or gradually over time?" "What were you doing when it began?"
  • D: Duration - Length, acute/subacute/chronic "How long have you been experiencing shortness of breath?" "Is it less than 3 weeks, 3 to 8 weeks, or more than 8 weeks?"
  • A/R: Aggravating/Relieving Factors "What makes your shortness of breath worse or better?"
    1. Aggravating: Positional, activity, environmental, behavioral "Does it get worse when lying down, sitting up, or in certain positions?" "Is it triggered by exercise, cold air, dust, talking, or eating?" "Does it vary by time of day, like morning or night, or by season?"
    2. Relieving: Position, rest, medication, environmental "Does sitting up, resting, or any medication help ease it?" "Do warm drinks, steam, humidity, or specific positions make it better?" "Does it improve at certain times of day or in specific seasons?"
  • C: Character/Type - Effort, tightness, wheezing "Can you describe the feeling? Is it like tightness, heaviness, or struggling to breathe?" "Do you hear any wheezing or rattling when you breathe?"
  • R: Radiation - Spread of sensation "Does the shortness of breath stay in one area or spread elsewhere, like your back?"
  • A: Associated Symptoms "Have you noticed other symptoms along with the shortness of breath?"
    1. Fever, chills, night sweats "Have you had fever, chills, or night sweats?"
    2. Cough, sputum "Do you have a cough, and is it dry or producing sputum?"
    3. Chest pain, palpitations "Do you feel chest pain or a racing heart when breathless?"
    4. Edema "Have you noticed swelling in your legs or ankles?"
    5. Fatigue, weakness "Do you feel unusually tired or weak?"
  • P: Progression - Change over time "Is the shortness of breath getting worse, better, or staying the same?" "Has the feeling or frequency changed since it started?"
  • P: Pattern - Timing, frequency, triggers "Is the shortness of breath constant or does it come and go?" "Does it happen at specific times, like at night or during activity?" "How often do episodes occur, and how long do they last?"
  • E: Effect on Life - Functional impact "How is the shortness of breath affecting your daily life, like work or sleep?" "Does it stop you from exercising or cause lightheadedness?"
  • R: Red Flags "I need to ask about some serious symptoms to ensure we address them."
    1. Hemoptysis "Have you coughed up any blood?"
    2. Severe SOB "Is the shortness of breath severe or stopping you from normal activities?"
    3. Weight loss "Have you lost weight without trying recently?"
    4. High fever, night sweats "Do you have a high fever or drenching night sweats?"
    5. Chest pain "Are you experiencing any chest pain with the shortness of breath?"

1ST COMPLAINT: SHORTNESS OF BREATH (Dyspnea)

  • O: Onset "When did the breathlessness start? Did it come on suddenly over seconds, or has it been building up over hours, days, or weeks?"
    • SUDDEN (Seconds to Minutes): Pulmonary Embolism (PE), Pneumothorax, Anaphylaxis, Severe Asthma Attack
    • ACUTE (Hours to Days): Pneumonia, Heart Failure Exacerbation, COPD Exacerbation, Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS)
    • CHRONIC (Weeks to Months): COPD, Chronic Heart Failure, Anemia, Interstitial Lung Disease (ILD)
  • S: Site of Associated Discomfort "Do you have any discomfort anywhere else, for example, in your chest?"
    • Central, crushing chest pain/pressure -> ACS
    • Sharp, one-sided, pleuritic chest pain -> PE, Pneumothorax, Pneumonia
    • Calf pain or -> 
      • Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT)

    • leg swelling
      • DVT
      • PE
      • HF

  • D: Duration "When the breathlessness comes on, how long does it last?"
  • A/R: Aggravating & Relieving Factors "What makes it worse or better? For example, exertion, lying down, or using an inhaler?"
    • AGGRAVATING:
      • Exertion (quantify: stairs, distance on flat)
      • Lying flat (Orthopnea) -> Heart Failure
      • Triggers (cold air, allergens, dust) -> Asthma
    • RELIEVING:
      • Rest
      • Sitting upright, leaning forward ("tripodding") -> Heart Failure, COPD
      • Inhalers (which ones, how effective?)
  • C: Character "Can you describe the feeling? Is it a tightness in the chest, a feeling of suffocation, or that you can't get enough air in?"
    • Chest tightness -> Asthma/Bronchospasm, ACS
    • Feeling of suffocation / "air hunger" -> Heart Failure, Metabolic Acidosis (DKA)
    • Inability to take a satisfying breath -> Anxiety
  • R: Radiation (of Pain) "If you have chest pain, does it travel anywhere, like your jaw, shoulder, or arm?"
    • Radiation is a red flag for ACS.
  • A: Associated Symptoms "Along with the breathlessness, have you had a cough, wheeze, fever, or any swelling in your legs?"
    • RESPIRATORY:
      • Cough (dry vs. productive), Sputum (color?)
      • Wheeze (audible or on stethoscope)
      • Hemoptysis (coughing up blood) -> PE, Malignancy, Vasculitis
    • CARDIOVASCULAR:
      • Chest pain
      • Palpitations
      • Orthopnea (how many pillows?) & Paroxysmal Nocturnal Dyspnea (PND)
      • Peripheral Edema (leg swelling)
      • Nausea vomiting
    • SYSTEMIC / INFECTIOUS:
      • Fever, chills, rigors -> Pneumonia
      • Weight loss, night sweats
  • P: Progression "Has your breathing been getting progressively worse over time?"
  • P: Pattern "Is there a pattern to it? Is it worse at night or early in the morning?"
    • Diurnal variation (worse at night/early morning) is classic for Asthma.
  • E: Effect on Life "How has this breathlessness affected your daily activities? How far can you walk before you have to stop?"
  • R: Red Flags "I just need to ask some specific questions to make sure we're not missing anything serious."
    • Inability to speak in full sentences
    • New confusion or drowsiness
    • Central crushing chest pain
    • Cyanosis (blue lips or tongue)
    • Stridor (high-pitched noise on inspiration) -> Upper Airway Obstruction
    • Silent chest (in a known asthmatic during an attack)
  • S: Severity / Scale "On a scale of 1 to 10, how severe is your breathlessness right now?"
    • (Clinician note: Consider using the MRC Dyspnoea Scale for chronic SOB).
