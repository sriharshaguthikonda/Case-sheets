Allergy History (GIT)
  1. Allergens
    • Food : Inquire about specific, diagnosed allergies (e.g.,
      • cow's milk,
      • soy,
      • eggs,
      • wheat,
      • peanuts,
      • tree nuts,
      • fish,
      • shellfish).
      • Medication 
      • antibiotics,
      • NSAIDs
      • DD true allergic reaction from a known side effect like gastritis or diarrhea.
    • Nature of GI Sx: What specific gut symptoms occur? (e.g., abdominal pain, nausea, vomiting, cramping, diarrhea, bloating).
    • Timing of Sx: Are reactions
      • immediate (within minutes to 2 hours - IgE-mediated) or
      • delayed (hours to days later - non-IgE-mediated)?
    • Systemic Sx: Do other symptoms accompany the GI issues? (e.g., skin reactions like hives/eczema; respiratory issues like wheezing; oral itching/swelling).
  2. Conditions
    • Eosinophilic Esophagitis (EoE): A primary allergic condition. Ask about difficulty swallowing (dysphagia), food getting stuck (impaction), chest pain, and vomiting. A personal/family history of atopy (asthma, eczema, hay fever) is a major risk factor.
    • Food Protein-Induced Enterocolitis Syndrome (FPIES): Ask about severe, delayed (1-4 hours) projectile vomiting, diarrhea, and lethargy after eating a specific food (commonly milk, soy, grains).
    • Oral Allergy Syndrome (OAS): Ask about itching/swelling of the mouth and throat after eating raw fruits, vegetables, or nuts, which can sometimes be associated with nausea or abdominal pain. This is linked to pollen allergies.
  3. Conditions with Allergic Overlap or Mimicry
    • Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD): While autoimmune, food allergies/intolerances can trigger IBD flares. Ask if the patient has noticed specific food triggers for their Crohn's or Ulcerative Colitis symptoms.
    • Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS): Inquire if IBS symptoms (pain, bloating, altered bowel habits) are consistently triggered after consuming certain foods, as a food hypersensitivity may be a contributing factor for a subset of patients.
    • Celiac Disease: It is crucial to differentiate this autoimmune disorder from a wheat allergy. Ask about a formal diagnosis. Also, inquire about symptoms of lactose intolerance, which can be a temporary secondary issue due to gut damage in untreated celiac disease.
